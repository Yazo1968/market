# Research Categories — Detailed Guidance and Search Strategies

## Overview

The five research categories structure the external research workstream. Each category has distinct sources, search strategies, and interpretation guidance. This document provides detailed direction for conducting research in each category, identifying authoritative sources, and avoiding common pitfalls.

---

## Category 1: Market Validation

### Purpose
Verify market sizing, growth rate assumptions, market dynamics, customer demand patterns, and market trends. Market validation research answers: "Is the market real, how big is it, how fast is it growing, and who are the customers?"

### Search Strategy

**Primary Search Objectives:**
1. Establish total addressable market (TAM) with sourced methodology
2. Establish serviceable addressable market (SAM) and serviceable obtainable market (SOM)
3. Verify annual growth rates (CAGR) and trend trajectory
4. Identify customer adoption patterns and buyer behavior
5. Map emerging market threats or opportunities

**Search Queries (by category):**
- "Market size [industry/segment] 2024" → Analyst reports, government statistics
- "CAGR growth rate [industry] 2024–2026" → Analyst forecasts, trend reports
- "Market trends [industry] 2024" → Trade publications, news archives
- "[Segment] customer adoption [metrics]" → Industry surveys, academic research
- "Addressable market [industry]" → Business intelligence reports

### Authoritative Sources

**Tier 1 (Primary Authority):**
- Gartner (Magic Quadrant, Market Guide, research reports)
- IDC (market size reports, forecasts)
- Forrester (market analysis, benchmarking)
- McKinsey (industry reports, trend analysis)
- BCG (market research, competitive analysis)

**Tier 2 (Secondary Authority):**
- Government statistical agencies (Census Bureau, Bureau of Labor Statistics, equivalent international)
- Industry-specific analyst firms (e.g., Frost & Sullivan for specific sectors)
- Trade association publications and surveys
- University research centers (published studies)

**Tier 3 (Supporting):**
- Financial news sources (Bloomberg, Reuters, S&P Capital IQ)
- Industry trade publications
- News archives (major publications: TechCrunch, Wall Street Journal, Financial Times)
- Academic journals (peer-reviewed market research)

**Lower Confidence (use with corroboration):**
- Company-funded research (e.g., "State of the Industry" reports funded by players in the market)
- Crowdsourced surveys (SurveyMonkey, Qualtrics, etc.) without representative sampling
- Consultant white papers (Accenture, Deloitte, etc.) if they have commercial interest in conclusions
- News articles (report findings, not primary source)

### Search and Interpretation Guidance

**TAM (Total Addressable Market) Estimation:**

When searching for TAM, expect to find analyst estimates rather than company-provided data. Cross-reference at least 2–3 sources.

**Example Search Process:**
1. Search: "Enterprise software market size 2024 Gartner"
   - Expected result: Gartner report with $X billion figure, defined segment scope
   - Document: Definition of "enterprise software" (which products/services are included)

2. Search: "Enterprise software market size 2024 IDC"
   - Compare IDC estimate to Gartner
   - Note any discrepancies in segment definitions

3. Search: "Enterprise software addressable market by segment 2024"
   - Break down TAM by subsegment (ERP, CRM, BI, etc.)
   - Match against company's target segment

**Document Findings:**
- Analyst source and publication date
- Exact TAM figure and currency
- Segment definition (what products are included in this TAM?)
- Methodology (if available)
- Confidence classification (Verified if from authoritative analyst, Conflicted if sources disagree)

**Growth Rate (CAGR) Verification:**

Analysts publish growth forecasts; verify these are explicit, not inferred.

**Example Search Process:**
1. Search: "[Industry] market growth rate 2024–2026"
   - Expected: Analyst forecast with explicit CAGR percentage
   - Document: Forecast period, growth drivers, risk factors

2. Compare company's claimed growth rate to analyst forecasts
   - If company claims 30% growth and analyst forecasts 12%, document the gap

3. Historical trend analysis:
   - Search: "[Industry] historical growth 2020–2024"
   - Plot historical trajectory to assess if company's future projection is consistent with past trend
   - Red flag: If company projects much higher growth than historical trend without explanation

**Customer Adoption Patterns:**

Search for how customers adopt/purchase in this market, not just volume.

**Example Search Process:**
1. Search: "[Industry] customer adoption behavior 2024"
   - Understand buyer journey: How do customers discover, evaluate, purchase?

2. Search: "[Industry] customer segment distribution"
   - Which customer types dominate? SMBs, mid-market, enterprise?
   - Expected vs. actual customer profile

3. Search: "[Industry] customer acquisition cost benchmarks"
   - Understand cost to acquire a customer; useful for validating company's unit economics

4. Search: "[Industry] net expansion rate churn"
   - If available, understand how customers expand (upsell, cross-sell) or leave (churn)

### Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

**Pitfall 1: Accepting Company's TAM Estimate Without Verification**
- **Problem:** Company states market is $50B; analyst reports say $30B. Report as is without noting the gap.
- **Correction:** Always cross-reference company's TAM claim against 2+ analyst sources. Document discrepancies.

**Pitfall 2: Using Outdated Market Data**
- **Problem:** Cite 2021 market size in a 2026 assessment without noting it's stale.
- **Correction:** Prioritize data from past 2 years. If older data is used, explicitly note: "Most recent analyst data is from 2021; market may have evolved."

**Pitfall 3: Confusing TAM with SAM**
- **Problem:** Company claims $500M TAM; actually citing the segment it serves (SAM), not total addressable market.
- **Correction:** Clarify definitions. Total Addressable Market = entire market opportunity (universe). Serviceable Addressable Market = portion company can realistically serve.

**Pitfall 4: Treating News Articles as Market Research**
- **Problem:** Use news article stating "Market is booming" as evidence of market size.
- **Correction:** Use news articles for trend context; cite analyst reports for market sizing. News reports findings; doesn't create data.

**Pitfall 5: Not Verifying Geographic Scope**
- **Problem:** Quote analyst report on "global SaaS market" without noting whether company serves global or specific regions.
- **Correction:** Match analyst data to company's geographic scope. If company is US-only, don't cite global market size without breaking down US segment.

### Confidence Calibration by Source Type

| Source Type | Starting Confidence | How to Improve |
|---|---|---|
| Single Gartner/IDC/Forrester report | Verified | Corroborate with second analyst firm or government data |
| Company-funded research | Unverified | Corroborate with independent analyst or academic source |
| News article citing analyst | Verified (for the analyst's claim) | Original analyst report provides higher confidence |
| Government statistical data (recent) | Verified | Multiple government sources or analyst corroboration increases confidence |
| Conflicting analyst sources | Conflicted | Examine segment definitions; may not be contradictory if applied to different segments |

## Category 2: Competitive Intelligence

### Purpose
Independently map the competitive landscape, identify direct and indirect competitors, and assess relative market position. Competitive intelligence answers: "Who are the competitors, how do they position, what is their market share, and what are their strengths/weaknesses?"

### Search Strategy

**Primary Search Objectives:**
1. Identify direct competitors (same product category, target customer)
2. Identify indirect competitors (alternative solutions, substitutes)
3. Establish relative market position and market share
4. Assess competitor positioning, pricing, and go-to-market
5. Track competitive moats (defensibility, switching costs)
6. Monitor M&A activity and leadership changes

**Search Queries (by category):**
- "[Company's product category] competitors 2024" → Product comparison sites, analyst quadrants
- "Competitive landscape [industry] 2024" → Industry analyst reports
- "Market share [product category] 2024" → Analyst reports, news archives
- "Competitor funding [specific competitors]" → Crunchbase, funding databases
- "[Competitor name] pricing features" → Company websites, product review sites
- "[Industry] consolidation M&A 2024" → Financial news, deal databases

### Authoritative Sources

**Tier 1 (Primary Authority):**
- Crunchbase (private company funding, employee count, leadership)
- G2 (product reviews, user ratings, feature comparison)
- Gartner Magic Quadrant (competitive positioning by analyst)
- Company registration databases (SEC for US public companies; business registries for private)
- Patent databases (USPTO, Google Patents, for technology differentiation)

**Tier 2 (Secondary Authority):**
- Analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester, IDC competitive analysis)
- News archives (TechCrunch, VentureBeat, industry press for funding, M&A, announcements)
- Financial news sources (Bloomberg, Reuters, Wall Street Journal for public company news)
- Industry registries and directories (company certifications, memberships)

**Tier 3 (Supporting):**
- Product review sites (Capterra, SoftwareOne, G2, for customer perspective)
- LinkedIn (employee data, team composition, hiring trends)
- Company websites and marketing materials
- Analyst quadrants (Gartner Magic Quadrant positioning)

**Lower Confidence (use with corroboration):**
- Venture capital firm portfolio descriptions
- Company-published competitive analysis
- Industry blogs and podcasts
- Unmoderated review sites without quality control

### Search and Interpretation Guidance

**Identifying Direct Competitors:**

Direct competitors serve the same customer with similar solutions. Use multiple sources to build a comprehensive competitive map.

**Example Search Process:**
1. Search: "G2 [product category]" (e.g., "G2 project management software")
   - Expected result: Ranked product comparison
   - Document: Top 10–15 products in same category
   - Limitation: G2 lists products users review; misses very early-stage or non-SaaS competitors

2. Search: "Gartner Magic Quadrant [product category]"
   - Expected result: Analyst positioning of leaders, challengers, niche players
   - Document: How analyst categorizes competitors
   - Value: Shows not just market leaders but also emerging competitors

3. Search: "[Company's product] alternatives"
   - Expected result: Comparison articles, review sites listing alternatives
   - Document: How end-users perceive competing solutions

4. Cross-reference with company's own competitive analysis
   - Compare company's claimed competitors to research results
   - Red flag: If company omits major competitors or includes non-competitors

**Assessing Market Share:**

Market share data is often proprietary; look for proxies (funding, employee count, customer count if public).

**Example Search Process:**
1. Search: "[Product category] market share 2024" (for market share leader)
   - Expected: Analyst estimate of top 3–5 competitors' share
   - Document: Any quantified market share figures

2. Crunchbase research on funding:
   - More funding ≠ larger market share, but provides proxy for relative market position
   - Track: Which competitors have raised capital, in which funding rounds?
   - Red flag: Well-funded competitor with no market traction suggests funding may not drive share

3. Employee count as proxy:
   - If available, compare employee counts (Crunchbase, LinkedIn)
   - Larger company (by headcount) may indicate larger revenue/market share
   - Caveat: Early-stage capital-efficient startups may have fewer employees but larger market share

4. Customer count (if available):
   - Some companies publish customer counts or analyst estimates
   - Compare against company's own customer count to assess relative market position

**Competitor Positioning and Pricing:**

Assess how competitors position and price relative to this company.

**Example Search Process:**
1. Visit competitor websites:
   - Home page messaging (target customer, problem solved, unique value)
   - Pricing page (if public): What do they charge, what features at each price tier?
   - Compare positioning: Direct conflict, differentiation, or addressing different segments?

2. Search: "[Competitor name] customer testimonials case studies"
   - Understand competitor's customer profile
   - Target segments, customer outcomes
   - Compare against this company's target customer

3. Product review comparison:
   - Search G2 or Capterra for feature comparison (user-driven comparison tools)
   - Understand how users perceive competitor vs. this company
   - Red flag: If company claims unique features that competitor also offers

**Competitive Moats and Defensibility:**

Assess what makes competitors defensible (not easy to displace).

**Example Search Process:**
1. Patent analysis:
   - Search USPTO patents for competitor name
   - Do they have patents that block this company's approach?
   - Patent filings indicate technology differentiation

2. Customer switching costs:
   - Evaluate: How difficult is it to switch from competitor's product to new product?
   - Switching costs (data migration, retraining, integration) = defensibility

3. Market position and scale:
   - Larger, entrenched competitors may be harder to displace
   - But also: Newer competitors may have technical advantages

### Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

**Pitfall 1: Trusting Company's Competitive Analysis Uncritically**
- **Problem:** Accept company's list of competitors without independent verification.
- **Correction:** Always verify competitive landscape independently. Company may omit strong competitors or include non-competitors.

**Pitfall 2: Treating Market Leaders as Only Competitors**
- **Problem:** Focus only on the top 3 competitors; miss emerging competitors.
- **Correction:** Include leaders, challengers, and emerging players. Gartner Magic Quadrant helps identify all tiers.

**Pitfall 3: Confusing Funding with Market Share**
- **Problem:** Conclude competitor with more funding has larger market share.
- **Correction:** Funding and market share are correlated but not identical. Funding is raising capital; market share is revenue. Distinguish.

**Pitfall 4: Ignoring Indirect Competitors and Substitutes**
- **Problem:** Only analyze direct competitors; miss that customers could solve the problem differently.
- **Correction:** Include indirect competitors (alternative solutions) and substitutes (workarounds, custom builds). Example: To-do app faces direct competition from other to-do apps, but also indirect competition from general productivity suites (Notion, Microsoft Teams).

**Pitfall 5: Using Outdated Competitor Funding Data**
- **Problem:** Cite 2022 funding data in 2026 assessment as if it's current.
- **Correction:** Prioritize recent funding (past 2 years). If older data cited, note recency: "Most recent Series A for Competitor X was 2022; market may have evolved."

## Category 3: Regulatory Environment

### Purpose
Establish actual regulatory requirements, licensing mandates, compliance obligations, and regulatory risk. Regulatory research answers: "What regulations apply, what must the company do to comply, and what are the regulatory barriers to scaling?"

### Search Strategy

**Primary Search Objectives:**
1. Identify all applicable regulations in target jurisdiction(s)
2. Establish licensing or certification requirements
3. Determine compliance timelines and deadlines
4. Research regulatory enforcement history (penalties, precedent)
5. Track pending regulatory changes (proposed regulations, recent rulings)
6. Understand data privacy, security, and disclosure requirements

**Search Queries (by category):**
- "[Industry] regulatory requirements [jurisdiction] 2024" → Regulatory authority official guidance
- "[Industry] licensing requirements [country/state]" → Government databases, bar associations
- "[Regulation name] compliance timeline" → Official guidance, law firm summaries
- "[Industry] FDA approval process" → FDA official guidance (if medical)
- "[Industry] data protection requirements" → GDPR/CCPA guidance, law firm resources
- "[Company's industry] regulatory enforcement [jurisdiction]" → News, regulatory databases

### Authoritative Sources

**Tier 1 (Primary Authority):**
- Regulatory authority official websites and published guidance
  - FDA (US medical devices)
  - FCA/FRA (UK/EU financial services)
  - SEC (US securities)
  - State and federal regulatory boards (licensing)
  - FINRA (US financial industry)
  - State insurance commissioners (insurance)
  - GDPR (EU), various state privacy laws (US)
- Government databases and registries (public records of licenses, enforcement actions)
- Published regulations and rules (Federal Register, equivalent international)
- Legal precedent (published court decisions, rulings)

**Tier 2 (Secondary Authority):**
- Law firm regulatory practice summaries (Baker & McKenzie, DLA Piper, Skadden)
- Trade association guidance (industry body standards, best practices)
- Regulatory consulting firms (specialized in specific industries)
- Government announcements and press releases

**Tier 3 (Supporting):**
- News coverage of regulatory changes
- Academic legal research
- Company regulatory white papers (with skepticism; assess for bias)

**Lower Confidence (use with official verification):**
- Consultant white papers
- Vendor guidance materials
- Secondary news interpretation of regulations

### Search and Interpretation Guidance

**Identifying Applicable Regulations:**

Different industries have very different regulatory frameworks. Tailor search to company's specific business.

**Example Search Process — Financial Services:**
1. Identify business activity:
   - Is company a "broker," "dealer," "investment advisor," "lender," "money transmitter"? (Exact classification matters for regulations)
   - Location: Which jurisdiction(s) does company serve?

2. Search: "[State/country] requirements for [specific business activity]"
   - Example: "California requirements for money transmitters"
   - Expected result: Regulatory authority specifies licensing, capitalization, audit requirements

3. Search: "[Business activity] federal requirements"
   - Most financial services have both state and federal requirements
   - Example: Money transmission requires state licensing AND potential FINRA/FinCEN registration

4. Search: "[Regulation name] compliance checklist"
   - Law firms often publish checklist of what compliance entails
   - Gives operational understanding of what company must do

**Example Search Process — Medical Device:**
1. Identify device classification:
   - Is device Class I, II, or III? (FDA classification determines approval pathway)
   - Classification depends on intended use, risk level, controls

2. Search: "FDA [device classification] 510(k) requirement"
   - 510(k) is pre-market notification for Class II devices (most common)
   - Understand: Is company's device Class II (510(k) required) or exempt?
   - Expected result: FDA official guidance on whether device requires 510(k)

3. Search: "FDA 510(k) approval process timeline"
   - Expected result: FDA states typical timeline is 30–90 days
   - Document: Actual timeline, evidence requirements

4. Search: "FDA post-market requirements medical devices"
   - Approval is not end of compliance; understand ongoing requirements (adverse event reporting, modifications, etc.)

**Compliance Timelines:**

Regulations often have specific implementation dates or deadline compliance.

**Example Search Process:**
1. Search: "[Regulation] compliance deadline 2024–2025"
   - Example: "GDPR compliance deadline for third-party cookies"
   - Expected result: Specific dates, staged implementation

2. Phased implementation:
   - Many regulations phase in over time
   - Example: California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) had different effective dates for consumer rights vs. enforcement
   - Document: Exact dates when company must comply

3. Grace periods or safe harbors:
   - Some regulations provide transition periods
   - Document: Is there a grace period before enforcement?

**Regulatory Enforcement and Risk:**

Understand penalties for non-compliance and enforcement history.

**Example Search Process:**
1. Search: "[Regulation] violations penalties [jurisdiction]"
   - Example: "GDPR violations penalties CNIL enforcement"
   - Expected result: Maximum penalties (often % of revenue), historical fines

2. Search: "[Industry] regulatory enforcement actions [jurisdiction] 2023–2024"
   - Find news of enforcement actions against companies in the space
   - Understand: What violations are actually being enforced? (Regulatory intent can differ from rules)

3. Compliance precedent:
   - Search court decisions or regulatory rulings on interpretation of regulation
   - Example: How have courts interpreted "reasonable security" under data protection laws?

### Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

**Pitfall 1: Relying on Company's Self-Assessment Without Verification**
- **Problem:** Trust company's claim that "No licensing required" without verifying against official regulatory guidance.
- **Correction:** Always verify against regulatory authority official guidance, not company's interpretation.

**Pitfall 2: Assuming Regulations Haven't Changed**
- **Problem:** Cite 2022 regulatory guidance in 2026 assessment without checking for updates.
- **Correction:** Always search for most recent regulatory guidance. Regulations change; verify current status.

**Pitfall 3: Confusing Compliance with Approval**
- **Problem:** Assume that if company is compliant with regulations, it is approved to operate. (Compliance ≠ approval in regulated industries.)
- **Correction:** Distinguish between (1) compliance with requirements, (2) active licensing/approval, (3) ongoing obligations.

**Pitfall 4: Ignoring Jurisdiction-Specific Regulations**
- **Problem:** Cite federal regulations only; miss state-specific requirements.
- **Correction:** For US companies, check both federal and relevant state regulations (many industries are state-regulated).

**Pitfall 5: Missing Pending Regulatory Changes**
- **Problem:** Assess current regulations without considering pending changes that will affect compliance.
- **Correction:** Search for proposed regulations, pending rule changes, or anticipated enforcement shifts. Example: European AI Act is pending; companies should prepare even before finalization.

## Category 4: Team and Credential Verification

### Purpose
Verify founder and team credentials, experience, educational background, and prior track record. Team research answers: "Do the founders have relevant experience, have they succeeded before, and are there gaps in team expertise?"

### Search Strategy

**Primary Search Objectives:**
1. Verify educational credentials (degree, institution, graduation year)
2. Verify prior employment history (companies, roles, dates)
3. Confirm board positions, advisory roles, and professional networks
4. Research prior startup involvement (exits, outcomes, failures)
5. Identify professional licenses and certifications
6. Assess public reputation (awards, speaking, media appearances, regulatory history)

**Search Queries (by category):**
- "[Founder name] [degree] [university]" → University registries, LinkedIn, press
- "[Founder name] employment history" → LinkedIn, company bios, press
- "[Founder name] startup founder exit" → Crunchbase, TechCrunch, news archives
- "[Founder name] regulatory history" → SEC records, bar association, regulatory databases
- "[Founder name] awards speaking" → News archives, conference appearances
- "[Team member] professional license" → Licensing board registries (bar, medical, engineering)

### Authoritative Sources

**Tier 1 (Primary Authority):**
- LinkedIn (professional network; self-reported but widely used baseline)
- Educational institution registries (university alumni records, degree verification)
- Company registration databases (SEC filings, director databases, business registries)
- Professional licensing registries (bar associations, medical boards, engineering boards)
- Crunchbase (startup history, company roles, exits)
- News archives (major publications for notable achievements, exits, controversies)

**Tier 2 (Secondary Authority):**
- Company websites and bios (official but subject to company control)
- Business filing databases (corporate records, officer registrations)
- Financial databases (public company executive bios and filings)
- Trade association directories
- Academic publication databases (for PhD, research credentials)

**Tier 3 (Supporting):**
- Individual websites or personal bios
- Interview articles (report founder's claims)
- Speaking engagement lists
- Awards and recognition databases

**Lower Confidence (treat as self-reported):**
- Personal Twitter/social media bios
- Resume submitted by candidate
- Company press releases about team
- Interview transcripts (report founder's claims, not verified facts)

### Search and Interpretation Guidance

**Educational Credential Verification:**

For critical roles (especially regulated industries or claims of specialized expertise), verify degrees directly.

**Example Search Process:**
1. LinkedIn search:
   - View listed education (degree, institution, graduation year)
   - LinkedIn entries are self-reported but are public and can be fact-checked

2. University verification (for critical credentials):
   - Contact university registrar directly to verify degree (if critical to assessment)
   - Note: Universities may not confirm details without authorization

3. Red flags:
   - Graduation date inconsistent with stated work history
   - Degree field inconsistent with claimed expertise

**Prior Employment and Startup History:**

Track founders' prior company experience and prior startup outcomes.

**Example Search Process:**
1. LinkedIn employment history:
   - List of employers, roles, dates
   - Cross-check: Do dates overlap (impossible if same time at two companies)?
   - Gaps: Are there unexplained gaps in employment history?

2. Startup history (Crunchbase):
   - Prior startups founded
   - Status: Still active, acquired, failed?
   - Role: Founder, CEO, early employee?

3. Exit outcomes:
   - If founder had prior exits, what were the outcomes?
   - Search: "[Prior company name] exit acquisition IPO"
   - Successful exits indicate relevant founder experience

4. Employment gaps:
   - Unexplained gaps may indicate failed startups, time off, or non-linear career
   - Not necessarily negative, but worth clarifying

**Professional Licenses and Certifications:**

For regulated professions, verify actual licenses.

**Example Search Process:**
1. Identify relevant license:
   - What license applies to this role? (Securities broker, lawyer, CPA, medical doctor)

2. Search licensing board registry:
   - State bar for lawyers
   - State medical board for doctors
   - SEC records for securities brokers
   - Most registries are publicly searchable

3. Verify:
   - License is active (not revoked or suspended)
   - Disciplinary history (if any)

4. Red flag:
   - Claimed professional credential not in public registry = major concern
   - May indicate misrepresentation

**Regulatory and Legal History:**

For founders, especially in regulated industries, check for regulatory or legal issues.

**Example Search Process:**
1. SEC database search:
   - Search: "[Founder name] SEC enforcement"
   - Look for regulatory actions, bars from industry, sanctions

2. Legal databases:
   - Search court records for lawsuits involving founder
   - Note: Civil litigation is common and not necessarily disqualifying; felony convictions or fraud findings are serious

3. News search:
   - Search: "[Founder name] lawsuit fraud controversy"
   - Look for news coverage of major legal issues

4. Licensing board history:
   - If applicable: Bar association, medical board, regulatory board records
   - Check for disciplinary actions

### Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

**Pitfall 1: Over-Weighting Prestigious Companies**
- **Problem:** Assume that founder worked at Google/Apple = excellent leader.
- **Correction:** Understand founder's specific role and seniority. Junior engineer at prestigious company ≠ senior technical leader.

**Pitfall 2: Assuming LinkedIn = Verified**
- **Problem:** Treat LinkedIn profile as independently verified truth.
- **Correction:** LinkedIn is self-reported. For critical credentials (degree, licensing), verify directly if material to assessment.

**Pitfall 3: Penalizing Career Gaps Unnecessarily**
- **Problem:** Assume gap in employment history indicates failure or instability.
- **Correction:** Gaps may indicate founder sabbatical, caregiving, or time between roles. Clarify but don't assume negative intent.

**Pitfall 4: Ignoring Red Flags in Regulatory History**
- **Problem:** Assume regulatory action doesn't indicate character or competence issues.
- **Correction:** Regulatory findings, fraud convictions, or bars from industry are significant signals. Investigate thoroughly.

**Pitfall 5: Conflating Different Types of Success**
- **Problem:** Assume successful founder at big company will be successful founding startup.
- **Correction:** Different skills. Executive at large company ≠ startup founder. Both are valuable but different.

## Category 5: Comparable Transactions

### Purpose
Establish market-rate benchmarks for valuation, deal terms, customer metrics, and financial multiples. Comparable research answers: "What are similar companies valued at, what metrics do successful companies achieve, and what deal terms are typical?"

### Search Strategy

**Primary Search Objectives:**
1. Research recent funding rounds in the sector (amount, valuation, terms)
2. Identify customer acquisition cost (CAC) benchmarks by segment
3. Establish lifetime value (LTV) and LTV/CAC ratio benchmarks
4. Research revenue multiples for exits (M&A, IPO)
5. Investigate debt financing terms and rates
6. Benchmark customer metrics (churn, expansion rate, payback period)
7. Track M&A transaction prices and deal structures

**Search Queries (by category):**
- "[Industry] Series A funding amount 2024" → Crunchbase, funding database
- "[Segment] CAC benchmarks 2024" → Analyst reports, SaaS benchmarking
- "[Industry] revenue multiple acquisition" → M&A databases, news
- "[Company type] SaaS benchmarks LTV churn" → OpenView, Insight Partners reports
- "[Competitor] Series [Round] funding valuation" → Crunchbase, news
- "[Industry] debt financing terms 2024" → VC databases, lending platforms

### Authoritative Sources

**Tier 1 (Primary Authority):**
- Crunchbase (funding amounts, dates, terms, company information)
- PitchBook / PWC (M&A pricing, deal multiples)
- CB Insights (company metrics, funding trends)
- SEC filings (public company financials, revenue, multiples)
- Analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester benchmarks by segment)

**Tier 2 (Secondary Authority):**
- VC firm reports and surveys (Bessemer Venture Partners, Sequoia, Initialized on fund performance)
- SaaS-specific benchmarking (OpenView, Insight Partners, Tomono Labs)
- News archives (TechCrunch, Wall Street Journal for M&A announcements, funding rounds)
- Financial databases (Bloomberg, S&P Capital IQ for public multiples)
- Industry association surveys

**Tier 3 (Supporting):**
- Investor pitch decks and trend reports (VC firms sharing market observations)
- Academic research on exit multiples
- Industry blogs and analysis

**Lower Confidence:**
- Company-provided benchmarks (may be cherry-picked)
- Startup survey data (self-selected participants, likely biased toward well-performing companies)
- Social media anecdotes

### Search and Interpretation Guidance

**Funding Round Benchmarks:**

Research recent funding rounds in the sector to establish typical amounts, valuations, and stage norms.

**Example Search Process — Series A for SaaS:**
1. Crunchbase search:
   - Filter: "[Industry] SaaS companies Series A, 2023–2024"
   - Document: Top 20–30 Series A rounds: amount, post-money valuation, date
   - Expected range: $1–5M typical for Series A SaaS

2. Calculate median and range:
   - Median amount (50th percentile), 25th percentile, 75th percentile
   - Median post-money valuation
   - Ranges help calibrate whether this company's ask is typical, above, or below

3. Compare this company's metrics to benchmarks:
   - If company raises $10M Series A, is that 2x typical? 50% of typical?
   - Note: Exceptional companies raise more; later-stage metrics (e.g., $1M ARR) command higher valuations

4. Segment by sub-vertical:
   - Series A for "B2B SaaS" may be different from "B2C SaaS"
   - Fintech typically raises larger amounts than MarTech for same stage
   - Segment to find comparable companies

**Revenue Multiples for Exits:**

Research what acquisition/IPO prices paid for similar companies (revenue multiple, EBITDA multiple, etc.).

**Example Search Process:**
1. Public company comparables (for public companies or those with disclosed financials):
   - PitchBook or Bloomberg: Search public companies in same industry
   - Calculate: What revenue multiple did public company trade at? (Market cap ÷ Annual revenue)
   - Example: If SaaS companies trade at 10x revenue, and company has $10M revenue, implied valuation $100M

2. Recent M&A transactions:
   - Search: "[Company type] acquisition price 2023–2024"
   - PitchBook, news archives: What did acquirers pay for similar companies?
   - Calculate: Acquisition price ÷ Revenue = revenue multiple

3. Segment by growth rate:
   - High-growth SaaS (>40% YoY) typically commands higher multiples than mature software
   - Document: What multiples do companies in this growth range achieve?

4. Profitability adjustment:
   - Profitable companies trade at different multiples than high-burn companies
   - Example: Profitable SaaS might trade at 8x revenue; high-burn SaaS at 5x revenue (discount for burn)

**Customer Metrics and Unit Economics:**

Research benchmarks for customer-level metrics (CAC, LTV, churn, expansion).

**Example Search Process — SaaS Benchmarks:**
1. Analyst reports on SaaS metrics:
   - OpenView Labs, Insight Partners, Tomono publish annual SaaS benchmarks
   - Search: "[Year] SaaS benchmarks CAC LTV payback"
   - Expected findings: Median CAC $x, median LTV/CAC ratio 3x, median payback 12 months

2. Segment by annual contract value (ACV):
   - Enterprise SaaS (high ACV) has different metrics than SMB SaaS (low ACV)
   - Document: Benchmarks for company's specific ACV segment

3. Compare company's metrics to benchmarks:
   - If company claims 8-month payback, is that exceptional (better than typical 12-month)?
   - If LTV/CAC is 2x, is that weak (typical is 3x+)?
   - Red flag: Metrics significantly worse than benchmarks suggest unit economics problem

4. Industry variation:
   - SaaS is different from fintech is different from B2B services
   - Document: Segment benchmarks for accurate comparison

**Debt Financing Terms:**

Research typical debt terms, interest rates, and covenant structures.

**Example Search Process:**
1. Venture debt platforms:
   - Silicon Valley Bank, Gold Hill Capital, Mercury post typical VC debt terms
   - Expected findings: Interest rate 10–12%, warrant coverage 10–15%, 24–60 month terms
   - Search: "[Company stage] venture debt terms 2024"

2. Debt financing benchmarks by stage:
   - Early stage (pre-revenue): Limited debt availability; typically equity-like terms
   - Growth stage (revenue >$1M): More debt available; standard VC debt terms apply
   - Document: Which stage is company in, and what debt terms are available?

3. Collateral and security:
   - Unsecured debt typically carries higher interest rates
   - Secured debt (against assets, IP, revenue) carries lower rates
   - Document: What can company pledge as security?

### Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

**Pitfall 1: Using Averages Instead of Medians**
- **Problem:** Use average valuation across all Series A companies, which can be skewed by outliers.
- **Correction:** Use median (50th percentile) and quartiles. Medians are more representative of typical market than averages.

**Pitfall 2: Not Segmenting Comparables by Stage**
- **Problem:** Compare Series A company to Series B benchmarks, inflating expected metrics.
- **Correction:** Always segment by funding stage. Series B companies should be compared to other Series B companies.

**Pitfall 3: Assuming Public Company Multiples Apply to Private Companies**
- **Problem:** Use public company valuation multiples to assess private company, ignoring liquidity discount.
- **Correction:** Private companies typically trade at 30–50% discount to public multiples (illiquidity). Adjust accordingly.

**Pitfall 4: Treating Best-in-Class as Typical**
- **Problem:** Benchmark company against top 10% performers (e.g., best CAC, lowest churn) as if those are typical.
- **Correction:** Use median benchmarks. Top performers are outliers. Document when company outperforms or underperforms typical.

**Pitfall 5: Ignoring Market Cycle and Timing**
- **Problem:** Benchmark company against 2021 funding data (higher valuations) without noting market has changed.
- **Correction:** Always use recent comparables (past 2 years). Document market timing; 2021 and 2024 valuations differ significantly.
