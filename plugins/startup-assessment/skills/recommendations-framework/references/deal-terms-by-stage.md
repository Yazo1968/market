# Deal Terms by Funding Stage: Comprehensive Reference

This reference file details market-standard deal terms, valuation approaches, investor protections, and conditions by funding stage. Use this to construct Path B investment structure alternatives.

---

## Pre-Seed Stage ($100K–$500K)

### Common Instruments
- SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) — most common
- Convertible notes — less common, but emerging
- Small equity rounds — less structured, typically angel investments
- Friends and family rounds — minimal documentation

### Valuation Approach
- No formal valuation established; instrument defers valuation
- Post-money SAFE cap (if used): $500K–$2M depending on team strength and idea stage
- Convertible notes: cap $500K–$1.5M (significantly lower than seed)

### Typical Round Characteristics
- Round size: $100K–$500K
- Number of investors: 5–20 (family, friends, angels)
- Check size: $10K–$50K per investor
- Time to close: 15–30 days
- Lead investor: may not have a formal lead; often ecosystem accelerator (Y Combinator, Plug and Play, etc.)

### Standard Protective Provisions
- For SAFE: pro-rata rights in future rounds (optional)
- For convertible: interest rate 5–7%, maturity 24 months, cap discount 10–20%
- For equity: none (common stock, same voting rights as founder)

### Investor Rights
- Information rights: minimal (perhaps quarterly updates if organized)
- Board seat: none
- Board observer: none
- Liquidation preference: none (common stock)
- Drag-along: none

### Standard Conditions Precedent
- Incorporation and cap table clarity
- No pending litigation
- Founder vesting schedule (4-year vesting, 1-year cliff standard)
- Equity plan setup (option pool 10–20% of cap table)

### Typical Covenants and Restrictions
- Information rights (if formalized at all): quarterly financials email
- Most SAFEs and convertibles are non-diluting at pre-seed stage

### Geographic Variations

#### North America
- SAFE becoming standard; minimal legal friction
- Y Combinator and Techstars SAFE use widespread
- Typical post-money cap: $1–2M for post-YC startups
- Convertible notes less common (SAFE preferred)

#### Europe
- Convertible notes more common than SAFEs
- SAFT or SAFE-equivalent less standardized
- Typical cap: EUR 500K–1.5M
- More legal documentation expected

#### GCC/MENA
- Traditional equity rounds or convertible notes preferred (SAFEs less common)
- Typical cap: $1–2M (if priced)
- Angel investors often family offices or strategic angels
- Less formal, often term sheet basis

#### Southeast Asia
- Mix of SAFEs and convertible notes
- Typical cap: $500K–$1.5M
- Convertible more common in traditional markets (Indonesia, Vietnam); SAFE in Singapore
- Multi-currency complexity

## Seed Stage ($500K–$2M)

### Common Instruments
- SAFE (post-money) — dominant in North America
- Convertible notes — 40% of market
- Priced equity (Seed Preferred) — 20% (more common with institutional lead)
- SAFEty set (combination of SAFE and convertible)

### Valuation Approach
- If priced: pre-money valuation $2–5M (North America), EUR 1–3M (Europe), $1–3M (GCC/SEA)
- SAFE post-money cap: $1–3M (reflects that seed is "pre-valuation")
- Convertible note cap: $1–3M, discount 15–30% (discount incentivizes early conversion)

### Typical Round Characteristics
- Round size: $500K–$2M
- Check sizes: $25K–$250K per investor
- Investor mix: angels, micro-VCs, accelerator demos (Y Combinator, Techstars, 500 Global)
- Lead investor: 1 lead investor (50%+ of round) typical
- Time to close: 30–60 days
- Valuation process: negotiated cap (if SAFE/convertible) or simple comparable (if priced)

### Standard Protective Provisions (SAFE)
- Pro-rata rights: included in most SAFEs (founder option to participate in future rounds)
- Board seat: none (founder maintains control)
- Information rights: typically informal (email updates)
- Anti-dilution: none in SAFE (converts at discounted future round)

### Standard Protective Provisions (If Priced Equity)
- 1x non-participating liquidation preference (common)
- Pro-rata rights: yes (percentage of ownership)
- Board seat: no (seed rounds typically have 0–1 board seats total)
- Board observer: investor may request

### Standard Investor Rights
- Information rights: quarterly unaudited financials (or founder discretion)
- Registration rights: none (no IPO contemplated)
- Approval rights: none (founder has control)
- Anti-dilution: weighted average (if priced equity round)

### Standard Conditions Precedent
- Cap table certification
- Founder personal vetting (background check)
- No litigation
- Standard representations (business formation, IP ownership, no liens)
- Option pool established (20% of fully diluted cap table)

### Typical Covenants and Restrictions
- Information rights: quarterly email with key metrics (CAC, LTV, burn rate, ARR growth)
- Pro-rata rights: investor can participate in future rounds pro-rata to ownership
- No liquidation preferences (if SAFE)
- If priced, 1x non-participating preference is standard

### Market Multiples for Valuation (If Priced Seed)
- SaaS: 3–5x ARR (if revenues exist)
- FinTech: 2–4x revenue
- Hardware/deep tech: $500K–$5M fixed valuation (product-dependent)
- B2C: $1–3M pre-money (consumer traction valued at engagement metrics)
- Marketplace: $1–5M based on GMV growth and unit economics

### Geographic Variations

#### North America
- SAFE standard for seed (95%+ market share)
- Convertible note use declining (VCs prefer SAFE)
- Typical cap: $2–3M post-money for quality founders
- Accelerator demos (Y Combinator, 500 Global) set market cap expectations
- Lead investor typical; syndicated angel rounds less common than before

#### Europe
- Convertible notes still common (30–40% of market)
- SAFEs gaining adoption (ERC or similar SAFE-equivalent emerging)
- Typical cap if convertible: EUR 1–2M
- Smaller round sizes than North America (EUR 500K–1M average)
- Less formal lead investor structure; often bank of angels

#### GCC/MENA
- Convertible notes and simple agreements preferred over SAFEs
- Priced equity more common than SAFEs
- Typical cap or valuation: $1.5–3M
- Regulatory: note if cross-border (GCC investor into other market) creates SAMA/DFSA/ADGM considerations
- Local VCs may impose governance requirements (quarterly board meeting, reporting)

#### Southeast Asia
- Convertibles gaining popularity (declining from 70% to 50% market share in favor of SAFE)
- Multi-currency complexity: funding in USD common; operations in local currency
- Typical cap: $1–2M
- Lead investor structure common (Singapore/Jakarta-based micro-VCs)
- Regulatory: flag MAS (Singapore), Thai SEC, or Indonesian OJK considerations if cross-border

## Series A Stage ($2M–$10M)

### Common Instruments
- Series A Preferred Stock — 100% of institutional rounds
- May include warrant coverage or ESOP refreshes

### Valuation Approach
- Pre-money valuation: $5–30M (highly variable by vertical, geography, traction)
- Valuation method: comparable company analysis (VC SaaS: 5–10x ARR, FinTech: 3–8x revenue)
- SaaS benchmark: $5–20M pre-money for Series A in North America
- Benchmarks by vertical (North America 2025):
  - SaaS: 5–10x ARR (ARR between $500K–$2M typical at Series A)
  - FinTech: 3–6x revenue
  - HealthTech: 3–5x revenue
  - Deep tech / Climate: $5–15M fixed valuation (product stage dependent)
  - B2C: 2–4x revenue (or user-based multiple: $50–100 per active user)
  - Marketplace: 2–5x GMV (or take-rate multiple: 3–8x take-rate revenue)

### Typical Round Characteristics
- Round size: $2–10M (often $3–5M median)
- Lead investor: 1 lead investor (Tier 1 VC, 50%+ of round)
- Syndication: 2–4 follow-on investors
- Check sizes: $1–3M (lead), $500K–$1M (followers)
- Time to close: 60–120 days
- Board composition: 3 seats (1 founder CEO, 1 lead VC investor, 1 independent director or founder CFO)

### Standard Protective Provisions

#### Liquidation Preferences
- 1x non-participating preferred (most common)
  - Meaning: Series A holders get paid 1x investment amount in exit; founders get remaining proceeds
- Participation rights: less common at Series A (more common at Series B+)
- Dividend: none (early-stage company, no dividends typical)

#### Anti-Dilution
- Weighted-average anti-dilution (market standard per NVCA)
- Full ratchet anti-dilution: rare in Series A (considered founder-unfriendly)
- Example: if down round occurs, Series A holders' price per share is protected

#### Redemption / Conversion
- None (Series A Preferred converts to Common on IPO or change of control)

### Standard Investor Rights

#### Board and Governance
- Board seat: 1 (lead investor)
- Board observer: possibly 1 (large follower investor)
- Board meetings: quarterly (or more frequent if company struggling)
- Founder retains CEO role and operational control

#### Information Rights
- Monthly financial statements (unaudited P&L, balance sheet, cash flow, key metrics)
- Quarterly board deck with business update
- Annual audited financials (if >$10M ARR or investor request)
- Cap table certification annually

#### Approval Rights (Veto)
Standard protective provisions requiring Series A consent:
- Liquidation or sale of company
- Merger with another company
- Redemption of Series A Preferred
- Incurrence of debt >$X (e.g., >$500K)
- Related-party transactions >$Y (e.g., >$100K)
- Change of CEO or key executives (if material)
- Sale of material assets
- Hiring/firing of CFO
- New rounds of fundraising (cannot dilute Series A below threshold)

#### Pro-Rata Rights
- Series A investors can participate in future rounds pro-rata to ownership
- Allows lead investor to maintain pro-rata as company scales

#### Registration Rights
- Demand rights: lead investor can require IPO registration (typically if company reaches $100M+ ARR)
- Piggyback rights: Series A holders can register shares in IPO at company expense
- S-3 rights: special registration rights if company qualifies

### Standard Conditions Precedent
- Updated cap table certification
- IP audit (patents, trademarks, source code ownership)
- Legal opinion (no litigation, good standing)
- Founder vesting (4-year vesting, 1-year cliff for founder shares)
- Updated option pool (20–30% of fully diluted cap table)
- Escrow arrangements for founder shares (typically 6–12 month post-close)
- Key-person life insurance (sometimes requested, especially if single founder)

### Typical Covenants and Restrictions
- Information rights: monthly financials, quarterly board deck
- Pro-rata participation in future rounds
- Anti-dilution: weighted average
- Board observation rights: 1 slot for large follower investors
- Anti-poaching: key employees may have non-compete (founder negotiated)
- Governance: quarterly board meetings minimum

### Geographic Variations

#### North America (US/Canada)
- NVCA model term sheet (80% of deals use as baseline)
- Pre-money valuation range: $5–20M (SaaS), $3–15M (FinTech), $5–30M (strong growth)
- Series A multiples (SaaS): 5–10x ARR, increasing if hyper-growth (15x+ for exceptional companies)
- Board size: 3 (often founder, lead VC, independent director)
- Information rights: monthly (or quarterly if early in Series A)
- Time to close: 60–90 days
- Syndication: 2–4 investors (lead takes 50–70%)

#### Europe (UK, Germany, France)
- BVCA / Invest Europe model (less standardized than NVCA)
- Pre-money valuation range: EUR 3–10M (EUR 1–1.5M ARR equivalent)
- Series A multiples: 4–7x ARR (lower than North America)
- Board size: 2–3 (founder, lead VC, possibly independent director)
- Information rights: quarterly (monthly less common)
- Time to close: 90–120 days
- Governance: may include supervisory board (German/Dutch companies)

#### GCC/MENA
- Pre-money valuation range: $3–15M
- Series A multiples: 3–5x revenue (lower than North America)
- Lead investor: typically regional VC (MEVP, Wamda, Nuwa Capital) or strategic investor
- Board: 2–3 seats, may include corporate representative (if strategic investor)
- Information rights: quarterly or monthly (investor preference)
- Governance: may require Sharia-compliance documentation or SAMA/ADGM regulatory review
- Time to close: 120–180 days (regulatory delays possible)
- Currency: USD common; may include FX hedging for regional founders

#### Southeast Asia
- Pre-money valuation range: $2–10M
- Series A multiples: 3–5x revenue (market-dependent by country)
- Lead investor: Singapore, Jakarta, or Bangkok-based VC (Sequoia, Spark Capital, Y Ventures)
- Board: 2–3 seats
- Information rights: quarterly + monthly email updates typical
- Governance: founder retains control; lead VC veto over major decisions
- Currency complexity: funding in USD; operations in multiple local currencies (THB, IDR, PHP, VND)
- Time to close: 90–120 days
- Regulatory: flag any MAS, Thai SEC, or OJK filings required

## Series B Stage ($10M–$40M)

### Common Instruments
- Series B Preferred Stock — 100% of institutional rounds
- Occasionally secondary equity (founder share sales) or restricted stock sales (employee buyouts)

### Valuation Approach
- Pre-money valuation: $20–100M+ (highly variable by vertical, stage maturity)
- Series B multiples (North America, SaaS): 7–15x ARR (ARR typically $2–5M at Series B)
- Valuation method: comparable company analysis (recent M&A, public company multiples)
- Benchmarks by vertical (North America 2025):
  - SaaS: 7–15x ARR (up from Series A 5–10x)
  - FinTech: 3–8x revenue (depending on segment)
  - Marketplace: 3–6x take-rate revenue
  - B2C consumer: 2–5x revenue (or per-user multiples if growth exceptional)
  - HealthTech: 4–8x revenue (if strong clinical validation)

### Typical Round Characteristics
- Round size: $10–40M (often $15–25M median)
- Lead investor: 1–2 lead investors (Tier 1 VCs, 40–60% of round)
- Syndication: 3–5 investors (more syndication than Series A)
- Check sizes: $5–15M (lead investors), $1–3M (followers)
- Investor composition: 40% lead VC, 40% existing Series A investors (pro-rata), 20% new
- Time to close: 90–150 days
- Board composition: 4–5 seats (founder CEO, 1–2 lead VCs, 1 existing VC, 1–2 independent directors)

### Standard Protective Provisions

#### Liquidation Preferences
- 1x non-participating (still common): Series B gets 1x liquidation, then founders split remainder
- 1x participating (increasingly common at Series B): Series B gets 1x AND participates in remainder up to cap (e.g., 3x cap)
  - Meaning: Series B holders get their money back first, then participate pro-rata in upside (capped at 3x return)
- Stacking / seniority: Series B liquidation preference is junior to Series A preference

#### Anti-Dilution
- Weighted-average anti-dilution (standard per NVCA)
- Full ratchet: increasingly uncommon (founder-unfriendly)
- Provision applies to both down rounds and future up rounds (pro-rata)

#### Redemption
- None (Series B Preferred converts to Common on exit)

### Standard Investor Rights

#### Board and Governance
- Board seats: 4–5 total
  - Founder CEO: 1
  - Lead VC(s): 1–2 seats
  - Series A lead (if participating pro-rata): 1 seat possible
  - Independent director(s): 1–2 seats (often recruited by VCs)
- Board observer rights: large followers may request observer seat
- CEO employment agreement: formal employment terms, severance, change-of-control provisions
- CFO hire: VCs often help recruit, may require approval

#### Information Rights
- Monthly financial statements (P&L, balance sheet, metrics dashboard)
- Quarterly board deck with business update
- Annual audited financials (standard at Series B)
- Cap table certification (quarterly)
- Optional: monthly office hours with founder/CFO

#### Approval Rights (Veto)
Standard protective provisions requiring Series B (often all VCs) consent:
- Exit or M&A (sale, merger, liquidation)
- Debt incurrence >$X (e.g., >$1M)
- Capital expenditures >$Y (e.g., >$500K)
- Related-party transactions >$Z
- Sale of material assets
- Change of control (>25% of company)
- New financing rounds (cannot issue debt or equity that dilutes Series B below threshold)
- Redemption of preferred stock
- Hiring/firing of CEO, CFO, CTO
- Major contract wins or losses (may require board approval if >$1M/year)

#### Registration Rights
- Demand rights: lead VCs can request registration (IPO or special registration)
- Piggyback rights: Series B can include shares in IPO registration
- S-3 rights: if company qualifies and board decides to register

#### Pro-Rata Rights
- All Series B investors can participate pro-rata in future rounds
- Carries through remaining company life (applies at C, D, E, etc.)

### Standard Conditions Precedent
- Updated cap table certification with all security holders
- IP audit (expanded: patents filed/issued, source code escrow, trademark registrations)
- Lien search (UCC-1 searches on company and key assets)
- Legal opinion: incorporation, good standing, no litigation, IP ownership
- Updated employment agreements: CEO, CFO, key executives
- Founder share escrow: typically 25% of founder shares escrowed for 6–12 months (ensures founder retention)
- Option pool: 25–30% of fully diluted cap table
- Representations and warranties: standard package (no material adverse change, no breaches, etc.)

### Typical Covenants and Restrictions
- Quarterly board meetings (mandatory minimum)
- Monthly financial reporting with KPI dashboard
- Annual audited financials
- Pro-rata participation in future rounds
- Anti-dilution: weighted average
- Drag-along rights: 50%+ of preferred holders can force founder to participate in exit
- Anti-poaching agreements: key employees typically under 2-year non-compete
- Corporate governance: board committee structure may be formalized (audit, compensation)

### Geographic Variations

#### North America
- NVCA model term sheet (standard for Series B)
- Valuation range: $20–80M pre-money (strong upward trajectory from Series A)
- Series B multiples (SaaS): 8–15x ARR
- Board: 4–5 seats (founder, 2 VCs, 1–2 independents)
- Lead investor(s): Tier 1 VC (Sequoia, Andreessen Horowitz, Benchmark, etc.)
- Time to close: 90–120 days
- Information rights: monthly financials + quarterly board meetings mandatory

#### Europe
- BVCA model (less standardized than NVCA but broadly similar)
- Valuation range: EUR 15–50M pre-money
- Series B multiples: 5–10x ARR (lower than North America)
- Board: 3–4 seats (may include supervisory board if German/Dutch company)
- Lead investor: European VC (Index Ventures, Accel, Balderton)
- Time to close: 120–150 days
- Governance: may require legal opinion on company law compliance; supervisory board if applicable

#### GCC/MENA
- Valuation range: $10–40M pre-money
- Series B multiples: 3–5x revenue (lower than North America)
- Lead investor: regional VC (MEVP, STV, Disrupt Africa) or international VC (IVP, Qualcomm Ventures)
- Board: 3–4 seats, may include corporate/strategic representative
- Time to close: 150–200 days (regulatory, currency considerations)
- Governance: quarterly reporting mandatory; may include Sharia compliance or SAMA/DFSA review

#### Southeast Asia
- Valuation range: $8–30M pre-money
- Series B multiples: 4–6x revenue (regional variation)
- Lead investor: Singapore, Bangkok, or Jakarta-based VC (Accel, Sequoia, Y Ventures)
- Board: 3–4 seats
- Currency complexity: significant (USD funding, local currency operations)
- Time to close: 120–150 days
- Regulatory: may require MAS or local regulator filings if financial services company

## Series C+ Stage ($30M+)

### Common Instruments
- Series C Preferred Stock (and D, E, F as company grows)
- Secondary equity: founder or employee share sales
- Earnout or merger consideration (later rounds)
- Growth equity structures (Series C-like with longer horizons)

### Valuation Approach
- Pre-money valuation: $100M+ (unicorn range)
- Valuation method: comparable M&A analysis, public company multiples, growth trajectory extrapolation
- Series C multiples (North America, SaaS): 10–20x ARR (ARR >$5M typical)
- Benchmark by vertical (North America 2025):
  - SaaS: 10–20x ARR (hyper-growth) to 5–10x ARR (slower growth)
  - FinTech: 4–10x revenue
  - Marketplace: 3–8x take-rate revenue
  - B2C / Consumer: 2–4x revenue (or per-user multiples if exceptional)
  - HealthTech: 5–12x revenue

### Typical Round Characteristics
- Round size: $30M–$100M+ (often $50–75M median for high-growth)
- Lead investor: 1–3 lead investors (growth/late-stage VCs, 40–60% of round)
- Syndication: 4–8 investors (significant syndication)
- Check sizes: $10–30M (lead), $2–10M (followers)
- Investor composition: mix of existing VCs (Series A/B), growth-stage VCs (Lightspeed, Tiger Global), strategic investors
- Time to close: 120–180 days (complex due diligence, diligence)
- Board composition: 5–7 seats (founder CEO, 2–3 VCs, 1–2 independent directors, possibly 1 strategic investor)

### Standard Protective Provisions

#### Liquidation Preferences
- Participation rights (1x capped at 3x total return): increasingly standard at Series C+
  - Meaning: Series C holders get their 1x investment back first, then participate pro-rata in remainder up to 3x cap
- Senior to Series A/B: Series C preference is junior to A/B in liquidation waterfall
- Redemption: sometimes included (investor can force company to redeem shares at 5–7 year mark if IPO not imminent)

#### Anti-Dilution
- Weighted-average anti-dilution (standard)
- Down-round provisions: if valuation declines in Series D, all previous investors' anti-dilution kicks in
- Broad-based weighted average: accounts for all outstanding securities

### Standard Investor Rights

#### Board and Governance
- Board seats: 5–7 total (professional board expected)
- Founder CEO: 1 seat (sometimes replaced if growth requires external CEO)
- Lead VCs: 2 seats
- Independent directors: 2–3 seats (increasingly recruited from industry)
- Strategic investor (if included): 1 seat possible
- Committees: audit committee (independent director chair), compensation committee, nominating committee often established
- CEO employment agreement: formal, with severance, change-of-control provisions

#### Information Rights
- Monthly financial statements (detailed P&L, balance sheet, cash flow, KPI dashboard)
- Quarterly board deck with detailed business update
- Annual audited financial statements (required)
- Cap table certification (quarterly)
- Annual strategic plan / budget
- Founder/CEO to be available for monthly calls with lead investor

#### Approval Rights (Veto)
Series C holders typically have veto over:
- Exit or M&A (sale, merger, IPO)
- Debt incurrence >$X (e.g., >$2M)
- Capital expenditures >$Y (e.g., >$1M)
- Related-party transactions >$Z (e.g., >$250K)
- New financing rounds (series D+, new convertibles, debt)
- Sale of significant assets
- Change of CEO or other key executives
- Redemption or repurchase of preferred stock
- Dividends
- Major contract wins/losses (>$5M/year)

#### Drag-Along Rights
- If Series C + any prior preferred holders representing >50% of preferred holders want to exit, they can drag all other shareholders (including founders) along in exit

#### Anti-Dilution
- Weighted average (applies to all down rounds through exit)

#### Pro-Rata Rights
- Series C investors can participate in future rounds pro-rata to ownership

### Standard Conditions Precedent
- Updated cap table (all shareholders, option grants, convertible notes)
- Comprehensive IP audit (patents, trademarks, copyrights, source code escrow, employment IP assignment)
- Lien search and UCC-1 searches
- Customer concentration analysis: top 10 customers, customer contracts
- Employee/founder vesting schedule and restricted stock agreements
- Material contracts review (lease, vendor contracts, customer contracts >$X)
- Compliance audit: tax filings, regulatory compliance (SOC 2, HIPAA if applicable, GDPR if EU operations, etc.)
- Insurance review (D&O, general liability, cyber, employment practices liability)
- Litigation search / no material pending litigation representation
- Founder share escrow: typically 25% escrowed for 6–12 months

### Typical Covenants and Restrictions
- Quarterly board meetings (mandatory)
- Monthly financial reporting with detailed KPIs
- Annual audited financial statements
- Pro-rata participation rights in future rounds
- Drag-along on exit (if majority Series C + preferred holders want exit)
- Founder non-compete agreements (typically 1–2 years post-exit)
- Key employee non-poaching (2–3 years)
- Anti-dilution: weighted average on all down rounds
- Governance: board committees (audit, compensation, nominating)
- Material adverse change (MAC) clause: if business deteriorates significantly, investor has certain remedies

### Valuation Comps and Benchmarks (Series C+)
- Public company multiples (similar SaaS/FinTech companies): research analyst reports, Bloomberg
- Recent M&A: PitchBook, Crunchbase, company announcements
- Venture capital surveys: PitchBook, CB Insights, Carta analysis
- ARR multiples (SaaS): 8–20x depending on growth rate and retention
- Revenue multiples (non-SaaS): 2–8x depending on vertical

### Geographic Variations

#### North America
- Lead investors: Tier 1 growth VCs (Lightspeed, Menlo, Andreessen Horowitz, Greylock)
- Valuation: $50–200M pre-money (strong companies)
- Series C multiples (SaaS): 12–20x ARR for hyper-growth
- Board: 5–7 seats, professional composition
- Time to close: 120–180 days
- Process: often auction or dual-track (growth rounds highly competitive)

#### Europe
- Lead investors: European growth VCs (Balderton, Sequoia, Index)
- Valuation: EUR 30–100M pre-money
- Series C multiples: 6–12x ARR (lower than North America)
- Time to close: 150–200 days
- Governance: may include supervisory board if German/Dutch

#### GCC/MENA
- Lead investors: international growth VCs (IVP, Insight Partners) or strategic investors
- Valuation: $20–80M pre-money
- Limited Series C activity (most companies exit at Series B in region)
- Time to close: 180–240 days (regulatory complexity)

#### Southeast Asia
- Lead investors: Accel, Sequoia, Y Ventures, Tiger Global
- Valuation: $10–50M pre-money
- Multi-currency complexity increases at this stage (may establish regional headquarters)
- Time to close: 150–200 days

## Growth Equity Stage ($10M–$100M+)

### Common Instruments
- Growth Preferred Equity (similar to Series C but with different terms)
- Secondary equity (founder/early employee secondary sale)
- Earnout structures (contingent on EBITDA or revenue milestones)
- Seller financing (founder retains small stake, receives promissory note for remainder)

### Valuation Approach
- EBITDA multiple approach: 4–10x EBITDA for profitable companies (highly vertical-dependent)
- Revenue multiple: 2–6x revenue for non-profitable growth companies
- DCF analysis: common for mature growth companies with predictable cash flows
- Comparable company M&A: similar transactions in space

### Typical Round Characteristics
- Round size: $10M–$100M+ (often $30–50M median for typical growth deal)
- Lead investor: 1–2 growth equity investors (Stripes, Accel, Index)
- Investor type: Growth PE, late-stage VC, family offices, corporate venture arms
- Check size: lead investors typically $20M–$50M
- Investor composition: lead growth equity firm (60–80%), co-investors (20–40%)
- Time to close: 120–180 days (more operational diligence than VC)
- Ownership target: growth equity firm typically targets 20–40% ownership post-close

### Valuation Method
- EBITDA multiple (if profitable): 4–8x EBITDA
  - Example: $10M EBITDA company valued at $50–80M (5–8x multiple)
- Revenue multiple (if high-growth): 2–4x revenue
- ARR multiple (if SaaS): 8–15x ARR
- Growth adjustment: faster-growing companies command premium multiples (3x EBITDA for 20% growth vs. 6x EBITDA for 50% growth)

### Standard Protective Provisions

#### Liquidation Preferences
- 1x non-participating (most common in growth equity)
  - Growth investors get their money back first; founders/employees get remainder
- Capped participation: sometimes 1x participating capped at 2.5–3x return
- Redemption: growth equity investors sometimes have redemption rights (can force company to redeem shares if exit not imminent after 5–7 years)

#### Anti-Dilution
- Weighted average anti-dilution (if future down rounds)
- Full ratchet: less common in growth equity

#### Board Control
- Majority or near-parity board: growth equity investors often negotiate for board control (3 of 5 seats) or near-control (2 of 4)
- Minority put: if founder/employees not bought out entirely, they may have minority protection (right to force exit within 5–7 years)

### Standard Investor Rights

#### Board and Governance
- Board seats: growth equity investor(s) typically 2–3 of 5 seats
- CEO: may be replaced with external CEO if founder not suitable for scaled company
- Independent directors: 1–2 seats
- Founder seat: founder retains seat if still CEO; if not, founder may have observer rights
- Executive team: growth equity firm often helps recruit COO, VP Sales, CFO

#### Information Rights
- Monthly financial statements (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow, KPIs)
- Quarterly board meetings (mandatory)
- Annual audited financials
- Annual strategic plan / budget
- Monthly KPI updates

#### Approval Rights
- Exit or M&A
- New debt >$X
- Capital expenditures >$Y
- Related-party transactions
- New rounds of financing
- Hiring/firing CEO or key executives
- Change of business model or market focus

#### Earn-Out / Milestone Clauses
- Common in growth equity: additional payment contingent on EBITDA or revenue hitting targets within 2–4 years post-close
  - Example: $100M base price, $20M earnout if EBITDA reaches $20M in 3 years
  - Example: $50M base price, $10M earnout per $5M additional ARR over next 2 years

#### Seller Financing
- Founder may retain small equity (5–10%), receive promissory note for bulk of sale price
- Note typically has 3–5 year term, interest rate 4–7%, potentially participates in future exit
- Aligns founder incentives: founder continues to participate in company upside post-exit (secondary sale)

### Standard Conditions Precedent
- Comprehensive financial audit (3-5 years of audited financials)
- Customer concentration analysis (top 10–20 customers, churn, retention)
- IP audit + employment IP assignment confirmations
- Material contracts review (customer contracts, vendor contracts, lease)
- Compliance audit (tax, regulatory, insurance, data privacy)
- Founder/executive employment agreements (post-close incentive compensation, non-compete)
- Environmental/safety compliance (if manufacturing or facility-based)
- Technology assessment / code audit (if software company)

### Typical Covenants and Restrictions
- Monthly financial reporting with KPI dashboard
- Quarterly board meetings
- Annual audited financials
- Earnout measurement / verification (independent accounting firm sometimes involved)
- Key employee retention (often required vesting post-close)
- Non-compete: founder/key executives typically 1–2 year non-compete, 5–mile radius (if local business)
- Change of control: if founder's retained equity is sold, founder consent or founder buy-in to new structure required
- Material adverse change: if business significantly deteriorates post-close, investor may have clawback rights or price adjustment

### Hold Period and Exit Expectations
- Typical hold: 3–5 years
- Exit target: IPO or strategic sale to larger company or PE firm
- Secondary sale: common (growth investor may sell to PE firm or other strategic buyer before founder/employees liquidity)
- Dividend recapitalization: growth investor may take dividend to return some capital while retaining equity

### Geographic Variations

#### North America
- Lead investors: Stripes, Accel, Insight Partners, Vista Equity (PE-focused growth equity)
- Typical EBITDA multiples: 5–8x (for 20–40% growth)
- Hold period: 3–5 years
- Earnout: 15–30% of purchase price
- Time to close: 120–150 days

#### Europe
- Lead investors: Balderton, Index, Perspectiva
- EBITDA multiples: 4–6x (lower than North America)
- Time to close: 150–180 days

#### GCC/MENA
- Limited growth equity activity (most regional companies still venture-backed)
- Strategic investors (family offices, SWFs) occasionally provide growth equity-like structures
- Time to close: 180–240 days if occurs

#### Southeast Asia
- Growing growth equity market (Accel, Insight Partners entering market)
- EBITDA multiples: 3–5x (market-dependent)

## Venture Debt Stage ($500K–$5M)

### Common Instruments
- Venture debt term loan (secured or unsecured)
- Revenue-based financing (alternative structure)
- Equipment financing (secured against equipment assets)
- Line of credit (revolving credit facility)

### Valuation Approach for Lender
- Debt amount: typically 25–50% of equity round raised
  - Example: $10M Series B raises venture debt of $2.5–5M
- Monthly burn rate (for early-stage): lender provides debt based on months of runway financed
- Monthly revenue (for revenue-based financing): lender provides debt based on % of revenue

### Typical Facility Characteristics
- Facility size: $500K–$5M (occasionally larger for growth-stage)
- Term: 3–5 years (typical amortization schedule)
- Interest rate: 8–12% per annum (market-dependent, varies by risk)
- Warrant coverage: 10–20% of facility size (additional equity upside for lender)
- Covenants: typically 2–3 financial covenants (minimum cash, revenue floor, debt/revenue cap)
- Personal guarantee: yes (if early-stage); sometimes waived if >$2M Series B raised
- Security: first lien on company assets (receivables, inventory, IP)

### Venture Debt Terms

#### Interest Rate
- Early-stage (pre-revenue): 10–12% per annum
- Seed-stage (some revenue): 8–10%
- Series A+ (profitable or strong growth): 6–8%
- Add-on: 1–2% if personal guarantee required

#### Warrant Coverage
- Typical range: 10–20% of facility size
- Example: $2M venture debt facility, 10–20% warrant coverage = 200K–400K shares at strike price (typically at par at close)
- Warrant terms: 7–10 year expiration, full ratchet anti-dilution (or weighted average)

#### Amortization Schedule
- Typical: 3–5 year amortization
- Example: $2M facility, 4-year amortization = $41.7K monthly payment (principal + interest)
- Payment frequency: monthly (sometimes quarterly for smaller amounts)
- Prepayment: often no penalty (or 1–2% penalty in first year)

#### Covenants
- Minimum monthly revenue: typically $50K–$100K for early-stage, $500K+ for later stage
- Minimum cash balance: company must maintain cash floor (e.g., $500K minimum)
- Debt service coverage ratio (DSCR): if profitable, often 1.25x minimum (debt payments <80% of cash flow)
- Revenue decline cure: if monthly revenue declines >20%, potential cross-default (lender can accelerate)

#### Conditions Precedent
- Personal guarantee from founder (early-stage)
- Subordination agreement: if future equity raised, equity holders agree that venture debt is senior
- UCC-1 filing: lender files lien on company assets
- IP security: sometimes lender takes security in IP (junior to equity holders)
- Insurance: company must maintain general liability, business interruption insurance

### Venture Debt Suitable For
- Bridge financing: to reach next equity milestone (Series A, Series B)
- Extend runway: 12–24 months additional runway without diluting equity
- Growth acceleration: debt can fund sales hiring or marketing without equity dilution
- Equipment financing: debt secured against physical assets (servers, equipment)

### NOT Suitable For
- Pre-revenue companies (extremely rare; warrants alone insufficient)
- Companies with declining revenue (lender wants revenue growth)
- Capital-intensive businesses (high debt/revenue ratio becomes problematic)

### Geographic Variations

#### North America
- Lenders: Silicon Valley Bank (dominant), Horizon Technology, Gold Hill, Lighthouse, Gold Hill Capital
- Interest rate: 6–10% (standard terms)
- Warrant coverage: 10–15% (standard)
- Terms: 4-year amortization typical
- Time to close: 30–45 days

#### Europe
- Lenders: Barclays Growth Ventures, Comerica Growth Capital (expanding into Europe)
- Interest rate: 7–11% (slightly higher than North America)
- Warrant coverage: 10–20%
- Time to close: 45–60 days

#### GCC/MENA
- Limited venture debt market (traditional banks skeptical of startups)
- Some growth in corporate venture arms offering venture debt
- Interest rate: 10–15% (higher risk premium)

#### Southeast Asia
- Emerging venture debt market
- Lenders: Anterra Capital, Vala Capital (occasional venture debt)
- Interest rate: 10–15%

## Revenue-Based Financing Stage ($250K–$5M)

### Common Instruments
- Revenue-based financing agreement (non-dilutive)
- No equity: founder retains 100% ownership
- Repayment based on monthly revenue percentage (typically 1–3%)

### Valuation / Financing Amount
- Amount financed: based on projected annual revenue
  - Example: $500K/month recurring revenue, lender finances $1M (2 months of revenue)
- Lender expects repayment cap: typically 2–2.5x amount financed
  - Example: $1M financed, repay $2–2.5M total before cap reached

### RBF Terms

#### Repayment Percentage
- Monthly repayment: 1–2.5% of monthly revenue (varies by risk profile)
- Effective annual rate: 6–30% depending on monthly percentage
- Example: 1.5% monthly = 18% annual (if revenue constant)
- Acceleration: if revenue grows, repayment accelerates (proportionally)
- Pause: if revenue declines, repayment can pause or reduce

#### Repayment Cap
- Typical cap: 2–2.5x amount financed
- Example: $1M financed, repay cap at $2–2.5M total
- Once cap reached: company stops making repayments (no equity given up)
- Typical repayment term: 3–7 years (until cap reached or term expires)

#### Term Length
- 5–7 year maximum term
- If cap reached earlier: financing ends (company keeps 100% ownership)
- If term expires before cap: any remaining balance forgiven (company owns equity free-and-clear)

#### Conditions
- Monthly revenue reporting (via API integration or manual reporting)
- Revenue decline cure: if revenue declines >20% month-to-month, potential default triggers
- Fund usage: some lenders restrict use (no founder draw, no related-party transactions)
- Insurance: company must maintain business interruption insurance

### RBF Suitable For
- SaaS / subscription companies (predictable recurring revenue)
- Digital businesses (low capex, high margin)
- Companies that don't need equity dilution or debt covenants
- Growth acceleration: RBF funds sales hiring, marketing, product development
- Non-venture-suitable founders: some founders prefer not to take VC money

### NOT Suitable For
- Seasonal or volatile revenue (RBF needs predictable monthly revenue)
- Early-stage (pre-revenue or <$50K/month): lenders won't finance
- Businesses requiring large capex (debt service ratio becomes problematic)
- Service businesses with low margins (<60% gross margin problematic)

### Market Terms (2025)

#### Standard Terms
- Advance: $250K–$5M (typical range)
- Monthly repayment: 1–2.5% of revenue
- Cap: 2–2.5x amount financed
- Term: 5–7 years
- Lenders: Clearco, Uncapped, Lighter Capital, Pipe (software-as-a-service revenue financing)

#### Higher-Risk Adjustments
- If revenue volatile: 2.5% monthly (higher end)
- If <$100K/month revenue: cap may be 2x (not 2.5x)
- If <$30K/month: may not qualify (below minimum revenue threshold)

### Geographic Variations

#### North America
- Dominant market for RBF
- Lenders: Clearco, Uncapped, Lighter Capital, Pipe
- Typical terms: 1.5–2% monthly, 2–2.5x cap, 5-year term
- Time to close: 15–30 days (fast process)

#### Europe
- Growing RBF market
- Lenders: Uncapped (UK-based), Wayflyer (Ireland)
- Typical terms: 1.5–2.5% monthly, 2–2.5x cap
- Time to close: 20–40 days

#### GCC/MENA
- Emerging (limited availability)
- Strategic investor interest growing

#### Southeast Asia
- Emerging (very limited availability)
- Mostly fintech platforms offering RBF for e-commerce sellers
