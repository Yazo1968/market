# Domain 8: Risk Profile

## Domain Overview

Risk Profile is the probabilistic assessment of how outcomes could deviate from the base case due to market, operational, technology, financial, regulatory, people, concentration, and macroeconomic factors. Understanding and assessing risks is critical because high-risk ventures require higher expected returns to justify investment, and risk mitigation strategies can materially improve outcome probability.

Mature venture investors think in terms of "risk-adjusted returns": a business with 40% outcome probability and 10x return potential (4x expected value) may be more attractive than a business with 80% outcome probability and 2x return potential (1.6x expected value). This domain requires identifying the specific risks to this company, estimating probability and magnitude, and evaluating the quality of mitigation strategies.

This domain influences:
- Investor risk-return expectations and valuation
- Board decision-making on strategic pivots or contingency plans
- Capital allocation (how much buffer/runway is needed)
- Probability-weighted expected value of the investment
- Urgency of de-risking initiatives (customer validation, technical proof-of-concept, etc.)

## Module 1: Market Risk

### Definition
Risks arising from demand dynamics, market timing, competitive displacement, pricing pressure, or changing customer preferences. Market risk is often the highest-impact risk because no execution can overcome non-existent or declining demand.

### Strong Evidence of Risk
- Addressable market timing uncertain: early-stage TAM that requires market education or behavior change (e.g., "blockchain will transform supply chain" with no current adoption)
- Demand validation weak: MVP or alpha testing with only founder-adjacent customers, not arms-length validation
- Competitive landscape crowded: multiple well-funded competitors or category leaders with distribution advantages
- Differentiation narrow or easily copied: product differentiation based on features that competitors can replicate within 6-12 months
- Pricing pressure evident: customers pushing back on price, or competitors undercutting on price
- Competitive dynamics favoring incumbents: incumbent switching costs high, or Category consolidating around one or two leaders
- Customer concentration risk: revenue concentrated in few customers who could switch or reduce orders
- Demand tied to one use case: if primary use case adoption slows, company has no diversification

### Strong Evidence of Mitigation
- Direct customer validation: letters of intent (LOIs), pilot agreements, or committed customer contracts demonstrating demand
- Multi-use case development: company has identified 2-3 distinct use cases or customer segments; not dependent on one trend
- Competitive moat: defensible advantage (IP, network effects, data, or switching costs) that protects against competitor attack
- Pricing power: ability to increase prices without major customer churn; willingness to pay validated through pilots or early sales
- Market benchmarking: TAM supported by industry reports, analyst firms, or bottom-up customer research
- Go-to-market adapted to competition: if competitors exist, company has identified differentiated channel, customer segment, or approach
- Customer diversification plan: early customers are diverse (different industries, use cases, geographies); reducing concentration risk over time
- Contingency plan documented: if TAM grows slower than expected, what is Plan B? (different segment, different use case, different geography)

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the evidence of market demand beyond founder intuition? (customer interviews, LOIs, pilot contracts)
2. How large is the addressable market (TAM) and how is it sized? (top-down, bottom-up, analyst reports)
3. How many credible competitors exist? What is their funding, customer base, and differentiation?
4. What is the company's defensible differentiation vs. competitors?
5. Is pricing power being validated? Are customers willing to pay at the proposed price point?
6. What percentage of revenue is from top 3 customers? Is customer concentration decreasing?
7. What happens to the business if the primary market trend slows (e.g., AI adoption slows, remote work reverses)?
8. Are there 2+ customer segments or use cases identified? Or is demand dependent on one narrow segment?
9. How frequently are you validating with customers? Is market feedback incorporated into product roadmap?
10. If market adoption is slower than expected, what is the contingency plan? Pivot to new segment? Adjust pricing?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Customer validation: LOIs, pilot agreements, or signed customer contracts demonstrating willingness to pay
- Customer research: interviews, surveys, or research reports supporting market size and demand
- Competitive landscape: overview of competitors, their positioning, and company differentiation
- TAM analysis: top-down and bottom-up market sizing, supported by analyst reports or customer data
- Pricing validation: evidence of customer price sensitivity, willingness to pay testing, or pilot pricing data
- Customer concentration: revenue by customer showing lack of concentration in top 3-5 customers
- Use case diversification: multiple customer segments, industries, or geographies being pursued
- Contingency plan: if primary market opportunity slows, what is Plan B?

## Module 2: Execution Risk

### Definition
Risks arising from company's ability to deliver against product roadmap, operational goals, and milestone commitments. Execution risk is often amplified by complexity, scale, or scope, and is heavily influenced by management team capability (assessed in Domain 6).

### Strong Evidence of Risk
- Lack of milestone history: company has not shipped product, acquired paying customers, or achieved prior-stated goals
- Product complexity high: engineering effort or timeline significant (e.g., 18+ month development cycle for core product)
- Scope creep evident: roadmap expands while delivery slips; features promised in Q2 delivered in Q4
- Organizational inexperience: team has not scaled or executed at the stage they're pursuing
- Resource constraints: insufficient engineering, product, or commercial resources to execute roadmap
- Milestone dependencies: future milestones dependent on successful completion of prior milestones with no contingency
- Delivery history poor: prior timeline estimates have been consistently optimistic (missing by >30%)
- Scaling complexity unaddressed: company has product-market fit at small scale but unclear how to scale to $10M revenue

### Strong Evidence of Mitigation
- Delivery track record: team has shipped products, features, or updates on schedule in past 2+ quarters
- Milestone history: company has achieved quarterly goals in 80%+ of quarters (assessed in Domain 6)
- Staffing aligned with roadmap: engineering and operational headcount sufficient to deliver stated roadmap
- Phased execution: roadmap broken into achievable milestones with clear go/no-go criteria for each phase
- Dependency mapping: critical path identified; high-risk milestones resourced heavily; contingency plans for key dependencies
- Execution discipline: documented processes for project planning, status tracking, and variance management
- Learning from past misses: team acknowledges prior overruns, documents root causes, adjusts estimation and planning
- Conservative timeline estimates: estimates include buffer for risk; consistently hit by 10% margin or better
- Parallel workstreams: product development proceeding while sales/GTM validates market; not sequential dependency
- Risk mitigation for critical paths: highest-risk milestones (core product performance, regulatory approval) addressed first or in parallel

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the team's track record of hitting milestone dates? (past 4 quarters)
2. How complex is the core product? What are the technical risks or engineering challenges?
3. How is the product roadmap broken down? What are the key dependencies and milestones?
4. What is the critical path to key milestone (e.g., product launch, first paying customer, revenue target)?
5. Is the team resourced sufficiently to execute the roadmap? Any resource constraints?
6. How are milestone estimates made? Do estimates include buffer for risk?
7. What are the highest-risk milestones? How are these being de-risked (architecture proofs, vendor partnerships, etc.)?
8. How frequently is execution against plan tracked and communicated?
9. If a major milestone slips 2-3 months, how does it cascade to dependent milestones?
10. What is the contingency plan if a critical milestone is at risk (e.g., different vendor, parallel approach)?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Historical milestone tracking: quarterly goals from past 2+ years showing achievement rate
- Product roadmap: high-level roadmap with major milestones and target completion dates for next 12-18 months
- Critical path analysis: dependency map showing which milestones are on critical path
- Team resource allocation: headcount plan aligned with roadmap complexity and timeline
- Delivery metrics: shipped features, cycle time, defect rates, or velocity metrics indicating execution health
- Risk mitigation for key milestones: specific plans to de-risk highest-risk deliverables
- Estimation accuracy: historical estimates vs. actuals showing prediction accuracy
- Project management practices: documented approach to estimation, planning, status tracking
- Buffer and contingency: evidence that timeline estimates include buffer for uncertainty

## Module 3: Technology Risk

### Definition
Risks related to product build, system scalability, technical architecture, cybersecurity, technical debt accumulation, and technology obsolescence. Technology risk includes both "build risk" (can we build it?) and "scale risk" (will it work at 10x scale?).

### Strong Evidence of Risk
- Build risk high: unproven technology or architecture; team has not built at this scale before
- Scalability questions: product works for small scale (1K users) but unclear if it scales to 1M users
- Technical debt accumulating: code quality degrading, defects increasing, development velocity slowing
- Single point of failure: core functionality dependent on one library, vendor, or person's expertise
- Security gaps: data handling practices undocumented; no third-party security audit or compliance certification
- Vendor lock-in risk: product highly dependent on one vendor (AWS, Stripe, etc.) with high switching cost
- Obsolescence risk: technology choice could become obsolete (e.g., built on deprecated framework) within 3-5 years
- Performance untested: system performance at scale not validated; response time, throughput, or reliability unknown
- Regulatory compliance unclear: if data-sensitive or regulated, compliance approach not defined or third-party assessed

### Strong Evidence of Mitigation
- Scalable architecture: system designed for 10x scale; load testing or traffic projections reviewed; architecture defensible
- Code quality metrics: defect rates, code coverage, or technical debt tracking showing healthy trends
- Security discipline: third-party security audit completed; SOC 2, ISO 27001, or similar certification in place
- Contingency architecture: critical components have failover or redundancy built in
- Vendor diversification: product not heavily dependent on single vendor; ability to switch vendor with moderate effort
- Open standards: technology choices based on open standards or widely-adopted frameworks (not proprietary)
- Continuous improvement: engineering practices include regular refactoring, dependency updates, technical debt paydown
- Security practices documented: data handling, encryption, access control, and incident response processes in place
- Compliance roadmap: if regulatory, clear plan and timeline for compliance certification
- CTO/Technical leadership: strong technical leader with architecture and scale experience
- Third-party validation: independent security audit, scalability review, or technical diligence completed

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the core technology architecture? Is it scalable to 10x current size?
2. What are the highest-risk technical components or dependencies?
3. Has the system been load-tested or stress-tested? What are the performance limits?
4. What is the technical debt level? Is it being actively managed and paid down?
5. How many people understand the core architecture? What happens if lead engineer departs?
6. What is the security posture? Has a third-party security audit been conducted?
7. What compliance requirements apply (GDPR, HIPAA, SOC 2)? Is the company compliant or on path?
8. What vendor dependencies exist (AWS, Stripe, etc.)? How difficult would switching be?
9. What is the technology refresh strategy? How do you stay current with security patches and dependencies?
10. What would it take to 10x the system (architecture changes, vendor upgrades, capacity planning)?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Architecture diagram: high-level system architecture showing major components and data flows
- Scalability assessment: documented analysis of system scalability to 10x current scale
- Load testing results: performance testing showing response time, throughput at projected usage levels
- Technical debt inventory: documented technical debt (code quality, dependencies, architecture) and paydown plan
- Security audit: third-party security audit report or penetration testing results
- Compliance status: GDPR, HIPAA, SOC 2, or other relevant compliance certifications and timeline to certification
- Vendor dependency analysis: list of critical vendor dependencies and switching costs/effort
- Technology stack rationale: justification for technology choices and scalability assessment
- CTO/technical leadership: background of lead technologist and architecture expertise

## Module 4: Financial Risk

### Definition
Risks related to company's financial position including cash burn rate, runway, profitability path, capital requirements, debt service capacity, covenant compliance, and sensitivity to forecast misses. Financial risk determines whether company can survive misses in revenue or expenses.

### Strong Evidence of Risk
- High burn rate relative to capital: if current burn continues, runway is <18 months
- Declining runway: burn rate increasing or capital efficiency decreasing over time
- No clear path to profitability: EBITDA break-even projected 5+ years out; excessive capital required
- Profitability dependent on unrealistic assumptions: margin improvement or cost reduction assumed with no credible path
- Weak unit economics: LTV:CAC <2:1, CAC payback period >24 months, negative gross margin
- Working capital consuming cash: as revenue grows, receivables or inventory increases faster than payables
- Covenant risk: if debt present, company at risk of breaching debt covenants in downside scenario
- No contingency funding: no line of credit, investor commitment, or capital reserve if capital raise delayed
- High dilution from future rounds: fully-diluted cap table shows founders/early investors will be heavily diluted
- Currency or commodity exposure: business exposed to currency or commodity price swings

### Strong Evidence of Mitigation
- Strong unit economics: LTV:CAC 3:1 or better, CAC payback <12 months, gross margin >60% for software
- Healthy runway: current burn rate implies 24+ months runway; multiple capital raise opportunities
- Path to profitability: EBITDA break-even projected within 3-4 years with reasonable capital required
- Capital efficiency improving: burn rate per $ revenue decreasing or magic number (revenue per $ S&M) improving
- Positive working capital dynamics: receivables collecting faster, payables extending; working capital is source of cash
- No debt or low debt leverage: debt terms reasonable; DSCR >1.25x and interest coverage >2x in all scenarios
- Conservative financial management: actual results tracking to projections; no material misses or surprises
- Contingency capital: additional funding available (credit line, investor commitment) if capital raise delayed
- Diversified capital sources: not dependent on single investor or funding round
- Foreign exchange or commodity hedging: if exposed to FX or commodity, hedging in place

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the current monthly burn rate and runway (months of cash)?
2. Is burn rate trending up or down? Is capital efficiency improving or declining?
3. What is the path to profitability (EBITDA breakeven)? How much total capital required?
4. If capital raise is delayed 6 months, does company run out of cash?
5. What is the sensitivity of cash runway to key assumptions (revenue 20% lower, OpEx 10% higher)?
6. If company misses revenue projections by 30%, how does this impact runway?
7. Are there debt obligations? What is the debt service coverage ratio in base and downside cases?
8. Are there financial covenants that could be breached? What is the safety margin?
9. How is working capital evolving? Is it a source or use of cash as company scales?
10. What contingencies are in place if capital raise is delayed or oversubscribed capital is not available?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Cash flow projections: 18-24 month monthly cash flow forecast showing runway
- Burn rate analysis: current monthly burn, burn rate trend, and capital efficiency metrics
- Sensitivity analysis: cash runway under base, upside, downside scenarios; sensitivity to revenue, OpEx misses
- Path to profitability: timeline to EBITDA break-even and total capital required to reach profitability
- Unit economics: LTV:CAC ratio, CAC payback period, gross margin trajectory
- Debt analysis (if applicable): debt principal, interest rate, maturity, covenants, DSCR, interest coverage
- Working capital analysis: trend in accounts receivable, accounts payable, inventory, and impact on cash flow
- Contingency plan: what is the plan if capital raise is delayed 3-6 months?
- Capital sources: clarity on who is expected to fund next rounds and likelihood of capital availability

## Module 5: Regulatory and Legal Risk

### Definition
Risks arising from regulatory compliance requirements, litigation exposure, IP disputes, licensing dependencies, licensing cost escalation, or regulatory changes that could impact business model or unit economics.

### Strong Evidence of Risk
- Regulatory requirements unclear: business operates in regulated industry (finance, healthcare, aviation) but compliance approach not fully defined
- License dependencies critical: business dependent on government licenses or permits; not assured of renewal or cost uncertain
- IP infringement risk: company uncertain if product infringes third-party patents; no patent search or analysis completed
- Litigation exposure high: company in dispute with customer, vendor, or competitor; lawsuit filed or threatened
- Regulatory changes pending: pending regulation could materially impact business (e.g., data privacy rules, AI regulation)
- Compliance costs escalating: regulatory compliance costs increasing faster than revenue (legal, audit, compliance staff)
- Operating in gray area: business model operates in regulatory gray area with unclear enforcement
- Material contracts at risk: key customer, supplier, or partner agreement subject to regulatory change or challenge

### Strong Evidence of Mitigation
- Compliance framework documented: regulatory requirements identified, compliance approach defined and documented
- Regulatory counsel engaged: company retained regulatory specialist or law firm to advise on compliance
- Compliance certifications: relevant certifications obtained (SOC 2, ISO 27001, HIPAA, PCI-DSS, etc.)
- IP protected: patents filed or trademarked; product does not infringe third-party IP per counsel review
- License assured: licenses or permits obtained and renewal process well-understood
- Regulatory moat: compliance becomes barrier to entry; competitors must also comply
- Contingency planning: if regulatory environment changes, company has alternative business model or pricing
- Clean litigation history: no active litigation; disputes resolved amicably
- Regulatory engagement: company engaged with regulators and has positive relationship
- Insurance: appropriate D&O, general liability, and specialized insurance in place

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the regulatory environment for this business? What regulations apply?
2. Is the company compliant with applicable regulations? Are there any outstanding regulatory issues?
3. Does the business depend on licenses or permits? How likely are these to be renewed and at what cost?
4. Has an IP search been conducted? Is there risk of infringing third-party patents?
5. Are there any material pending regulatory changes that could impact the business?
6. What is the cost of regulatory compliance? How does this scale with business size?
7. Are there any active litigation matters or threats? What is the potential financial exposure?
8. If the regulatory environment changes unfavorably, what is the contingency plan?
9. What insurance is in place for litigation and regulatory risk?
10. Is the company engaged with regulators? What is the relationship quality?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Regulatory analysis: documentation of applicable regulations and compliance requirements
- Legal opinion: external counsel assessment of regulatory compliance and IP risk
- Compliance status: certification of SOC 2, ISO 27001, HIPAA, PCI-DSS, or other relevant certifications
- IP portfolio: patents filed, trademarks registered; IP search report showing no infringement risk
- Licenses and permits: list of material licenses/permits, renewal schedule, cost, and assured renewal
- Litigation history: summary of any active or threatened litigation with counsel assessment of exposure
- Regulatory risk assessment: documentation of regulatory risks and contingency plans
- D&O and liability insurance: summary of insurance coverage including amounts and deductibles
- Legal counsel contacts: names of external counsel advising on regulatory, IP, and compliance matters

## Module 6: People Risk

### Definition
Risks arising from key person dependency, critical talent attrition, succession gaps, or organizational capability gaps. People risk is amplified in early-stage companies where few individuals possess critical expertise or relationships.

### Strong Evidence of Risk
- Key person dependency: critical relationships, expertise, or decisions concentrated in one individual
- Lack of succession planning: no identified successor for CEO, CTO, or other critical roles
- Recent critical departures: key employees (engineer, salesperson, product lead) have recently left
- Founder fatigue evident: founder working excessive hours, burnout signals present, personal stress high
- Skill gaps not being addressed: critical capability gap (CFO, VP Sales) identified but no hiring plan or timeline
- High turnover rate: voluntary attrition >20% annually; people leaving due to culture, compensation, or disagreement
- Competitive vulnerability: key employees could be recruited away by larger competitors
- Vesting cliffs approaching: team members reaching end of vesting; significant departures risk if bonus/refresh not provided
- Founder/employee misalignment: tension between founders or between founders and early employees evident

### Strong Evidence of Mitigation
- Succession planning: documented succession plan for CEO, CTO, VP Sales; identified internal successors in development
- Bench strength: multiple people capable of handling critical functions; redundancy in place
- Retention mechanics: equity refresh grants, retention bonuses, and competitive compensation aligned to extend tenure
- Talent pipeline: strong track record of recruiting and retaining talent; low voluntary attrition (5-10%)
- Leadership development: formal mentorship, training, or development programs for high-potential people
- Diversity and inclusion: diverse team reduces key-person dependency; multiple perspectives on decisions
- Team stability: core team members have been with company 2+ years; low turnover among key roles
- Culture and values: strong evidence of positive culture, alignment on mission, and team cohesion
- Founder health: founder appears energized and healthy; balanced personal/professional life
- Compensation competitive: compensation benchmarked and positioned at market rates; not losing offers to competitors

### Key Assessment Questions
1. Who are the 3-5 most critical people to company success? What happens if each departs?
2. What is the plan if the CEO departs? Is there an identified successor?
3. What is the voluntary attrition rate? Why are people leaving?
4. How much of critical expertise (engineering, product, sales) is concentrated in one person?
5. What percentage of the team will be at vesting cliffs in the next 12 months?
6. What is the tenure distribution? How many team members have been with company 2+ years?
7. What is the compensation position relative to market? Are you losing offers to competitors?
8. What retention mechanisms are in place (equity refresh, bonuses, etc.)?
9. What evidence is there of founder health and resilience? Is founder at risk of burnout?
10. How is the team morale? What is the voluntary attrition risk if next milestone is missed?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Succession planning document: identified successors for CEO, CTO, VP Sales, and other critical roles
- Attrition analysis: voluntary departures by role and reason; attrition rate by tenure
- Vesting schedule: option vesting dates for key team members; equity refresh plan for approaching cliffs
- Compensation analysis: current compensation by role compared to market benchmarks; offer acceptance rate
- Talent retention plan: mechanisms to retain critical talent (equity refresh, retention bonuses, career development)
- Organizational depth: evidence of redundancy in critical functions; multiple people capable of key roles
- Team feedback: anonymous team feedback or engagement survey data indicating culture and morale
- Founder health: evidence of founder engagement and absence of burnout signals
- Recruiting track record: historical recruiting and retention metrics showing ability to build team

## Module 7: Concentration Risk

### Definition
Risks arising from concentration in customers, suppliers, channels, geography, or revenue streams. Concentration reduces resilience and increases vulnerability to loss of a single customer, supplier, or market.

### Strong Evidence of Risk
- Customer concentration high: top 3 customers represent >50% of revenue; loss of one customer materially impacts business
- Supplier concentration high: 80%+ of supply comes from single supplier; alternative suppliers not readily available
- Geography concentration: 80%+ of revenue from single country or region; exposed to local economic conditions
- Channel concentration: 80%+ of revenue from single channel (direct sales, marketplace, partnerships); channel disruption risk
- Revenue stream concentration: 80%+ of revenue from single product or use case; limited diversification
- Vendor concentration: critical functionality dependent on single vendor (Stripe, AWS, etc.); switching difficult
- Key account dependency: single large account is disproportionately important to revenue or profitability

### Strong Evidence of Mitigation
- Customer diversification: revenue spread across 50+ customers; top 3 customers <40% of revenue
- Supplier diversification: multiple suppliers for critical inputs; established relationships and qualified alternatives
- Geographic diversification: revenue across 2+ geographies; growth in underpenetrated geographies
- Channel diversification: revenue from 2+ channels (direct, marketplace, partnerships); balanced channel mix
- Revenue stream diversification: revenue from 2+ products or use cases; not dependent on one revenue source
- Vendor alternatives: multiple vendors provide critical functionality; switching feasible with moderate effort
- Account management: large accounts actively managed; relationship strong and expanding; customer success metrics positive
- Churn management: for recurring revenue, churn rate low and stable; not losing customers to competitors

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the revenue concentration? What percentage comes from top 3 customers?
2. If the largest customer churned, what would be the impact on revenue and profitability?
3. How diversified are suppliers? What is the switching cost if primary supplier becomes unavailable?
4. What percentage of revenue is geographic? Is there geographic diversification?
5. How dependent is revenue on single channel (direct sales, marketplace, etc.)?
6. Are there 2+ product lines or revenue streams? What percentage does each represent?
7. How reliant is the business on single vendor? What is the switching cost?
8. For largest customer, what is their percentage of their spend going to company? (reduces switching risk)
9. What is the plan to diversify revenue as company grows?
10. How does concentration compare to peer companies or industry benchmarks?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Customer concentration: revenue by top 10 customers showing concentration % and trend
- Supplier analysis: list of critical suppliers and switching cost for each
- Geographic revenue: revenue breakdown by geography; concentration % in top market
- Channel analysis: revenue by channel (direct, marketplace, partnerships, etc.); concentration by channel
- Product revenue: revenue by product or use case; concentration by product line
- Vendor dependency: list of critical vendor dependencies and switching cost/timeline
- Diversification plan: plan to grow revenue in new geographies, channels, or product lines
- Account relationship metrics: largest customers' satisfaction, NPS, and growth trajectory
- Churn analysis: by customer, by product, by channel; identifying concentration risks

## Module 8: Macroeconomic and Systemic Risk

### Definition
Risks arising from macroeconomic conditions (interest rate changes, inflation, recession), currency exposure, sector cyclicality, or systemic market disruptions. Macro risks are largely outside company's control but impact demand and capital availability.

### Strong Evidence of Risk
- Business highly cyclical: revenue declines sharply during recessions or economic downturns
- Interest rate sensitivity: customer demand inversely correlated with interest rates (e.g., luxury goods, travel, financial services)
- Inflation sensitivity: cost structure highly exposed to inflation; gross margin compresses during inflationary periods
- Currency exposure: significant revenue or costs in foreign currencies; currency swings impact profitability
- Commodity exposure: raw material costs major component of COGS; vulnerable to commodity price swings
- Sector disruption risk: technology or regulatory change could disrupt entire sector
- Leverage high: debt ratios high; rising interest rates increase debt service burden
- Dependent on external funding: company requires access to capital markets; credit tightening impacts fundraising ability

### Strong Evidence of Mitigation
- Anti-cyclical demand: product or service demand increases during downturns (e.g., cost reduction software, discount retailers)
- Diversified customer base: customers across sectors, geographies, and industries; downturn in one sector affects minority of revenue
- Pricing discipline: company can increase prices during inflation; customers have limited price elasticity
- Currency hedging: foreign exchange exposure hedged; profit insulated from currency swings
- Commodity contracts: critical commodities locked in via long-term contracts; exposure managed
- Conservative capital structure: low debt ratios; company can weather credit tightening
- Recession contingency plan: documented plan to reduce OpEx, preserve cash, and remain viable if recession occurs
- Business model resilience: subscription or recurring revenue provides cash flow stability
- Niche or mission-critical: business targets niche with stable demand; or provides mission-critical function (won't be cut during downturns)

### Key Assessment Questions
1. How has the company's business performed during prior economic downturns or recessions?
2. Is demand for the product/service cyclical or counter-cyclical?
3. Is the company exposed to interest rate changes? How does demand change if rates rise or fall?
4. How exposed is the company to inflation? What happens if inflation accelerates?
5. Is there currency exposure? What happens if USD strengthens or weakens?
6. What is the largest raw material or commodity exposure? How much would a 20% price swing impact profitability?
7. If there were a 20% reduction in customer demand due to recession, how would the company respond?
8. How dependent is the company on capital markets for funding? What if credit markets tighten?
9. Does the company have recession contingency plan? What would be cut to remain viable?
10. How does company's sector or vertical compare to others in terms of recession resilience?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Historical performance analysis: revenue and profitability during prior recessions or economic downturns
- Cyclicality analysis: documentation of business cycle (counter-cyclical, acyclical, or cyclical)
- Interest rate sensitivity: analysis of customer demand vs. interest rate environment
- Inflation impact: analysis of COGS and pricing power during inflationary periods
- Currency exposure: documentation of foreign currency exposure and hedging strategy
- Commodity exposure: documentation of commodity costs and hedging strategy
- Recession contingency plan: documented plan to reduce burn, preserve cash, and remain viable if recession occurs
- Leverage analysis: debt ratios and debt service coverage under stressed macro scenarios
- Comparable performance: how has sector or category performed during prior downturns?

## Module 9: Mitigation Assessment

### Definition
Evaluation of the quality and credibility of the company's risk mitigation strategies. Not all risks can be eliminated, but strong mitigation strategies increase the probability of favorable outcomes despite risks.

### Strong Evidence of High-Quality Mitigation
- Proactive risk management: risks identified in advance; mitigation strategies in place before risks materialize
- Measurable de-risking: milestones and metrics defined for validating risk reduction (e.g., "achieve LTV:CAC 3:1 by Q3")
- Owner accountable: each risk mitigation strategy has a clear owner and accountability
- Resource allocation: sufficient resources allocated to highest-risk mitigation areas
- Timeline credible: mitigation timeline is reasonable and achievable given company stage and resources
- Progress tracking: company tracks progress against mitigation milestones and adjusts if falling behind
- Contingency plans: for critical risks, fallback strategies defined if primary mitigation is insufficient
- Evidence of execution: team has demonstrated ability to execute on prior mitigation strategies
- Advisor/board support: mitigation strategy reviewed and endorsed by board, investors, and advisors
- Learning orientation: team learns from setbacks; mitigation strategies evolve based on experience

### Weak Evidence of Mitigation
- Risks acknowledged but no mitigation plan: "we know market risk is high, but we're optimistic"
- Vague mitigation: "we'll validate market through customer interviews" without clear success criteria
- No resources dedicated: mitigation strategy identified but no person or budget allocated
- Timeline unrealistic: mitigation planned to complete in 1 month when 6 months credible
- No tracking: progress against mitigation plan not monitored or communicated
- Contingency absent: if primary strategy fails, no fallback plan articulated
- Team lacks relevant execution history: team has not successfully executed similar mitigation before
- Board/investor skepticism: mitigation plan reviewed but concerns not addressed
- Execution discipline weak: prior mitigation initiatives fell behind or incomplete

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What are the top 3-5 risks to the company (by probability x impact)?
2. For each risk, what is the mitigation strategy? How will risk be reduced?
3. How will company know if mitigation is working? What metrics or milestones indicate success?
4. Who is accountable for each mitigation? Who owns the strategy?
5. What resources (budget, headcount, time) are allocated to mitigation?
6. What is the timeline for mitigation? How does this align with company funding runway?
7. If primary mitigation fails, what is the contingency plan?
8. Has the team successfully executed similar mitigation strategies before?
9. Do board members and investors believe mitigation strategies are credible?
10. How frequently is progress against mitigation plan reviewed and communicated?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Risk register: comprehensive list of top 15-20 risks ranked by probability x impact
- Mitigation strategy: for top 5-10 risks, documented mitigation approach with clear success criteria
- Accountability: risk owner identified for each major risk
- Timeline: mitigation timeline with quarterly milestones and success metrics
- Progress tracking: quarterly review of progress against mitigation plan
- Contingency plans: for critical risks (>$5M impact), fallback strategy documented
- Resource allocation: team and budget allocated to highest-priority mitigation areas
- Comparable experience: evidence that team has successfully executed similar mitigation before
- Board/investor engagement: mitigation plan discussed and endorsed by board and key investors

## Assessment Notes for Scoring Agent

Evaluate risk profile systematically across the nine modules using this framework:

**Gather Evidence**

1. Conduct risk assessment interview: ask leadership "what keeps you up at night?" and "what is your biggest risk?" Identify top 5-10 risks by spontaneous mention.
2. Map each risk mentioned to the nine modules (Market, Execution, Technology, Financial, Regulatory, People, Concentration, Macro, Mitigation).
3. Analyze financial projections for sensitivity: what assumptions, if 20% worse, most materially impact outcomes?
4. Review historical performance: how has company performed vs. prior projections? Variance indicates execution risk magnitude.
5. Examine team and board composition (Domain 6): diversity, experience, and depth inform people and execution risk assessment.
6. Assess competitive landscape (Module 1): number and capability of competitors inform market risk assessment.
7. Review regulatory/legal status (Domain 10): pending litigation, compliance status, IP position inform regulatory risk.
8. Conduct scenario analysis stress-test (Domain 7): downside scenario triggers? What would cause 50%+ outcome variance?

**Score Each Module**

For each of the nine modules, assign a risk rating:
- **Green (Mitigated)**: Risk identified and credible mitigation strategy in place. Risk probability or impact is low, or strong evidence of mitigation reduces concern. Company has demonstrated ability to manage similar risk.
- **Yellow (Acknowledged)**: Risk is present and acknowledged, with partial mitigation in place. Risk could materially impact outcomes if mitigation is insufficient. Monitoring and adjustment required.
- **Red (High Risk)**: Risk is material and inadequately mitigated. Risk could be outcome-determining (>30% probability, >20% impact). Insufficient evidence of mitigation or execution capability.

**Synthesize Domain Score**

Roll up individual module scores to derive overall Domain 8 score:
- Count Green modules. If 6+ of 9 modules are Green, risk profile is acceptable relative to stage and expected returns.
- Count Yellow modules. 2-3 Yellow modules are normal; more than 3 Yellow indicates material risk across multiple areas.
- Count Red modules. Even 1 Red module requires detailed investigation and conviction on mitigation. 2+ Red modules suggest risk is too high for the expected return or investor thesis must be explicitly risk-adjusted.

**Red Flags**
- Market risk: no customer validation beyond founder network; TAM only from founder intuition
- Execution risk: track record of missing milestones >30%; no improvement in execution discipline over time
- Technology risk: core product scalability unproven; single-point-of-failure architecture; no technical leadership
- Financial risk: <18 month runway; burn rate accelerating; no path to profitability or unit economics unsustainable
- Regulatory risk: unclear compliance requirements; litigation pending; IP infringement risk not assessed
- People risk: key-person dependency without succession plan; high recent turnover; founder burnout evident
- Concentration risk: top customer >50% of revenue; top supplier 80%+ of supply
- Macro risk: highly cyclical business with no recession contingency plan
- Mitigation risk: risks identified but no concrete mitigation strategy; no progress tracking against mitigation plan

**Green Flags**
- Market risk: strong customer validation (LOIs, paying customers); differentiated positioning; multi-use case development
- Execution risk: consistent track record of hitting 80%+ of milestones; delivery improving or stable; sufficient resources allocated
- Technology risk: scalable architecture validated; security audit completed; technical leadership strong
- Financial risk: 24+ months runway; burn rate declining or stable; unit economics 3:1 LTV:CAC or better; path to profitability clear
- Regulatory risk: compliance framework documented; regulatory counsel engaged; relevant certifications obtained
- People risk: succession plans documented; low voluntary attrition; strong team retention and recruitment track record
- Concentration risk: revenue diversified across 50+ customers; top 3 <40%; geographic and channel diversification planned
- Macro risk: counter-cyclical or resilient business model; contingency plan documented; low leverage
- Mitigation risk: proactive risk management; clear mitigation strategies with owners, metrics, and timelines; board engagement on risks

## Cross-Domain Dependency Notes

**Domain 8 feeds into:**
- **Domain 7 (Financial Performance)**: Risk mitigation strategies inform financial projections and scenario planning. High execution or market risk suggests conservative financial assumptions.
- **Domain 9 (Capital Structure)**: Risk profile informs capital raise structure, investor protections (covenants, board seats), and required return. Higher risk warrants more investor control or higher return expectations.

**Domain 8 depends on:**
- **Domain 6 (Management)**: Team capability and track record directly determine ability to mitigate risks. Weak execution risk mitigation suggests weak management team.
- **Domain 7 (Financial Performance)**: Financial sensitivity analysis informs which assumptions have outsized risk impact. Financial discipline is evidence of risk awareness.
- **Domain 10 (Legal/Governance)**: Regulatory compliance status and litigation exposure directly inform Regulatory Risk assessment (Module 5).
