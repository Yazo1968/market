# Domain 3: Business Model Assessment Reference

## Domain Overview

The Business Model domain evaluates the economic sustainability of the startup—how the company will generate revenue, at what cost, and whether the unit economics support profitable scale. This domain assesses whether the business model is viable given market conditions, customer economics, and cost structure. A strong business model demonstrates that the startup can acquire customers profitably, retain them, and expand within accounts—the fundamental engine of sustainable business growth.

A compelling market opportunity and excellent product can be destroyed by a broken business model (for example, a product that costs $10 to deliver but can only be sold for $5). Your assessment here evaluates whether the financial model is grounded in realistic assumptions and whether the startup has a clear path to unit economics profitability and sustainable growth.

---

## Module 1: Revenue Model

### Definition
The mechanism by which the startup generates revenue—the core answer to "how do we make money?" Revenue models typically fall into a few categories: subscription/SaaS (recurring), transaction-based (per-transaction), usage-based (per-unit consumed), licensing (perpetual or time-limited), hybrid, or other. A sound revenue model aligns with customer preferences and industry norms while supporting sustainable unit economics.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Clear revenue model articulation**: The submission explicitly states the revenue model. Example: "We use a per-seat SaaS subscription model with annual contracts. Pricing is $500/month per user (or $5,500/year, providing 8% discount for annual payment)."
- **Model alignment with customer preferences**: The revenue model matches how customers prefer to buy and how they measure value. Example: "Customers measure value in terms of users/seats, so per-user pricing aligns customer incentives with ours."
- **Industry norm alignment**: The revenue model is standard for the category or has clear justification if non-standard. Example: "Usage-based pricing is becoming standard for cloud infrastructure; we adopt this model to align costs with customer value consumption."
- **Model sustainability**: The revenue model supports sustainable economics and does not depend on predatory practices. Example: "We don't use artificial price increases or lock-in tactics; we compete on value and switching costs are low, so we must deliver continuous value."
- **Multiple revenue model validation**: If the startup uses multiple revenue models (e.g., per-user + implementation services), each is articulated separately with % contribution to total revenue. Example: "SaaS subscription: 75% of revenue; professional services: 20%; API/integration partners: 5%."
- **Pricing evolution**: The submission acknowledges how pricing or revenue model might evolve. Example: "We start with per-user pricing; as product matures, we'll move toward value-based pricing based on customer ROI."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Vague revenue model**: "We'll make money from our users" without specifying the mechanism (subscription, transaction, licensing, etc.).
- **Non-standard model without justification**: "We're free and will figure out monetization later" or "We'll pivot to a different revenue model once we have customers."
- **Model misaligned with customer value**: The customer's primary value is cost reduction (usage-based), but the startup charges per-user or per-seat.
- **Unsustainable revenue model**: The startup assumes predatory pricing, lock-in tactics, or other non-sustainable practices.
- **No pricing articulation**: The submission does not specify pricing; pricing is vague ("We'll be competitive").
- **Unrealistic pricing**: Pricing is not justified by customer research or comparable products; pricing may be too high or too low.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the primary revenue model (subscription, transaction, usage, licensing, hybrid)? How is revenue generated?
- What is the pricing? Is it per-unit, per-user, per-transaction, per-month, per-year?
- Why is this revenue model aligned with customer value and industry norms?
- Is pricing validated with customer research or comparable products?
- How sustainable is the revenue model? Does it align customer incentives with the startup's incentives?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Revenue model articulation** (subscription, transaction, usage, licensing, hybrid, etc.)
- **Specific pricing** with unit (per-user, per-transaction, per-month, per-year, etc.)
- **Pricing justification** based on customer value, comparable products, or customer research
- **Model sustainability assessment** (how sustainable is the model; are there predatory elements?)
- **Multiple revenue streams** if applicable, with % contribution of each
- **Pricing evolution** articulating how pricing or model will evolve over time

## Module 2: Pricing Strategy

### Definition
The strategic approach to pricing—how the startup will set and evolve prices to capture customer value, optimize revenue, and remain competitive. Pricing strategy is distinct from pricing itself. Strategy addresses: value-based vs. cost-plus pricing, price discrimination by segment, freemium vs. paid-only, bundling, and pricing evolution. Pricing strategy must balance capturing customer value (high enough to maximize revenue) with remaining competitive (not so high that customers avoid adopting).

### What Good Looks Like

- **Value-based pricing approach**: The submission articulates pricing based on customer value (ROI, cost savings, productivity gains) rather than cost-plus markup. Example: "If a customer saves $100K/year in manual labor costs, our $20K/year product captures 20% of those savings, leaving 80% for the customer. This is attractive to the customer and captures meaningful value for us."
- **Price segmentation**: Pricing is segmented by customer segment (size, vertical, use case) with different price points for different values. Example: "Enterprise segment: $50K ACV; mid-market: $20K ACV; SMB: $5K ACV, reflecting value differences across segments."
- **Willingness to pay validation**: Pricing is informed by customer research on willingness to pay. Example: "We surveyed 50 target customers; 80% indicated willingness to pay $10K–$15K annually; we set pricing at $12K to capture value without being a deal-blocker."
- **Competitive anchoring**: Pricing is informed by competitive landscape without being anchored to competitors' pricing alone. Example: "Incumbents charge $500/user/year; our per-user model is $400, but includes features competitors charge separately for, creating better value."
- **Freemium or free trial strategy (if applicable)**: If using freemium or free trial, strategy is articulated—how will free users convert to paid, or what is the purpose of the free tier? Example: "We offer free tier supporting up to 10 users; 30% of free users convert to paid when they hit the user limit; average free-to-paid CAC is $500."
- **Price evolution**: The submission articulates how pricing will evolve over time as the product matures. Example: "We start with penetration pricing ($5K/year) to build customer base; after 2 years, we'll increase pricing 10–15% annually as we improve product and reduce churn through increased switching costs."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Cost-plus pricing without value consideration**: "Our product costs $1K to build and deliver; we mark up 4x for $4K/year pricing" without validating customer willingness to pay.
- **Pricing unclear or not specified**: "We'll be competitive on pricing" without stating actual pricing.
- **No customer research on willingness to pay**: Pricing is asserted without evidence that customers will pay the claimed price.
- **Price anchored entirely to competitors**: "Competitors charge $500/user; we charge $450" without independent value assessment.
- **No price segmentation**: Same price for all customers despite dramatically different value delivery.
- **Freemium without conversion strategy**: "We offer free tier" without articulating how free users will convert or what the economics are.
- **No pricing evolution**: Implies pricing will remain static forever, which is unrealistic.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- Is pricing based on customer value (value-based) or cost-plus? Can you quantify the customer ROI?
- Is pricing validated through customer research? What percentage of target customers indicated willingness to pay at this price point?
- Is pricing segmented by customer segment? How does value differ by segment?
- How does pricing compare to competitors? Why is the startup's pricing better value despite potential premium?
- If using freemium, what is the conversion strategy? What % of free users convert to paid, and at what CAC?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Pricing strategy articulation** (value-based, penetration, premium, freemium, etc.)
- **Specific pricing with value justification** (customer ROI, competitor comparison)
- **Willingness-to-pay research** showing customer acceptance of pricing
- **Price segmentation** showing different pricing for different customer types or company sizes
- **Freemium strategy** (if applicable) with conversion mechanics and economics
- **Pricing evolution plan** showing how pricing will change over 3–5 years
- **Price anchoring justification** if pricing is compared to competitors

## Module 3: Cost Structure

### Definition
Breakdown of the costs required to deliver the product and acquire customers. Cost structure includes: cost of goods sold (COGS)—the variable cost to serve each customer; operating expenses—fixed costs including R&D, sales, marketing, and G&A; and infrastructure costs. Understanding cost structure is foundational to assessing unit economics and path to profitability.

### What Good Looks Like

- **COGS articulated**: The submission estimates the variable cost to serve each customer. For SaaS, this typically includes infrastructure costs (cloud computing, databases, storage), payment processing fees, and customer support. Example: "Our COGS is approximately 15% of revenue: cloud infrastructure $2/month per customer, payment processing at 2.9% of ACV, support at $500 per customer per year for mid-market segment (2,000 customers = $1M support cost / $2M revenue = 50% margin COGS)."
- **Operating expense breakdown**: The submission breaks out major cost categories: R&D (product development), sales, marketing, and G&A. Example: "Annual OpEx model: R&D 40% (4 engineers + 1 PM), Sales 25% (2 account executives + 1 SDR), Marketing 15%, G&A 20%."
- **Cost scaling understanding**: The submission articulates which costs are fixed and which scale with customers. Example: "Infrastructure costs scale linearly with customer volume; R&D is partially fixed (product team headcount) and partially variable (outsourced QA); sales and marketing scale linearly with revenue targets."
- **Unit economics articulation**: COGS and OpEx per customer are estimated. Example: "COGS per customer: $200/year. OpEx allocation per customer: $1,500/year (based on $3M OpEx / 2,000 customers). Total cost per customer: $1,700/year. With $2,500 ACV, contribution margin is 32%."
- **Cost assumptions documented**: Assumptions underpinning cost estimates are articulated (e.g., infrastructure per customer, support hours per customer, sales efficiency metrics).
- **Headcount efficiency**: The submission models headcount and revenue per head. Example: "Target revenue per sales employee: $1M; revenue per engineer: $500K."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **No COGS articulated**: The submission treats software as zero-marginal-cost with no acknowledgment of infrastructure, support, or other variable costs.
- **Cost structure not broken down**: Operating expenses are lumped together with no visibility into R&D, sales, marketing, and G&A.
- **Unrealistic cost assumptions**: COGS is extremely low (implying no customer support or infrastructure costs) or extremely high (implying inefficient delivery).
- **No understanding of cost scaling**: Does not distinguish between fixed and variable costs.
- **Overhead not allocated to customers**: Does not estimate cost per customer or contribution margin per customer.
- **No headcount model**: Does not model headcount required to achieve revenue targets.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the estimated COGS per customer (as % of revenue or as absolute dollars)? What does this include?
- What are the major operating expense categories? What percentage of revenue does each represent?
- Which costs are fixed, and which scale with customer volume?
- What is the estimated contribution margin per customer (ACV minus COGS)?
- What is the headcount required to achieve revenue targets? What is revenue per head?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **COGS breakdown** (infrastructure, payment processing, support, other variable costs) as % of revenue or per-customer
- **Operating expense model** showing R&D, Sales, Marketing, G&A as % of revenue
- **Fixed vs. variable cost identification** (which costs scale, which are fixed)
- **Unit economics per customer** (COGS, OpEx allocation, contribution margin)
- **Headcount model** showing staffing required to hit revenue targets
- **Cost scaling assumptions** (e.g., support hours per customer, infrastructure per customer)

## Module 4: Unit Economics (CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC Ratio, Gross Margin, Payback)

### Definition
Quantification of the fundamental business model metrics: Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)—the fully-loaded cost to acquire a customer; Lifetime Value (LTV)—the total profit a customer generates over their lifetime; LTV:CAC ratio—the ratio of lifetime value to acquisition cost (typically should be >3:1 for sustainable SaaS); gross margin—the percentage of revenue remaining after COGS; and payback period—the time required for a customer to generate enough profit to pay back their acquisition cost. Strong unit economics are the foundation of a scalable, profitable business.

### What Good Looks Like

- **CAC calculated and articulated**: The submission estimates CAC by dividing fully-loaded sales and marketing expenses by the number of customers acquired. Example: "Year 1 fully-loaded sales and marketing expense: $800K (2 AEs + 1 SDR + marketing); customers acquired: 20. CAC = $40K. This includes salary, benefits, tools, and overhead allocation."
- **CAC by segment**: CAC is modeled separately for different customer acquisition channels or segments. Example: "Self-serve CAC: $3K (digital marketing); mid-market CAC: $20K (direct sales); enterprise CAC: $80K (direct sales + implementation support)."
- **LTV calculated**: LTV is estimated based on gross margin and customer retention. Formula: LTV = ARPU (Annual Revenue Per User) × Gross Margin × (1 / Churn Rate). Example: "Mid-market segment: $20K ACV, 70% gross margin, 10% annual churn. LTV = $20K × 70% × (1/0.10) = $140K."
- **LTV:CAC ratio assessed**: The submission calculates LTV:CAC and assesses sustainability. Example: "LTV:CAC = $140K / $20K = 7:1. This is attractive and indicates sustainable unit economics."
- **Payback period calculated**: The submission estimates how many months until a customer pays back their acquisition cost. Formula: Payback = CAC / (ARPU × Gross Margin / 12). Example: "CAC: $20K; monthly revenue: $1,667; gross margin: 70%. Monthly contribution margin: $1,167. Payback period: $20K / $1,167 = 17 months."
- **Gross margin articulated**: The submission specifies gross margin (revenue minus COGS as % of revenue). Example: "Gross margin target: 75% (typical for SaaS companies). This assumes 15% COGS for infrastructure and support."
- **Path to unit economics**: If current unit economics are not yet profitable, the submission articulates the path to improvement. Example: "Year 1 unit economics: LTV:CAC = 2:1 (unsustainable); Year 2 target: 4:1 (achieved through 20% price increase and 10% churn reduction)."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Unit economics not calculated**: The submission does not estimate CAC, LTV, or payback period.
- **CAC understated**: CAC calculation excludes significant costs (marketing, implementation support, or overhead allocation).
- **CAC over-amortized**: CAC is spread over multiple years or calculated in ways that hide true acquisition cost.
- **LTV overestimated**: LTV assumes unrealistic retention or does not account for churn.
- **Unit economics not segmented**: One set of unit economics for all customers, when acquisition costs vary dramatically by segment.
- **Path to improvement not articulated**: If unit economics are poor, there is no plan to improve them.
- **LTV:CAC not calculated**: The submission does not assess the ratio or benchmark it against industry standards.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the estimated CAC in Year 1? How is this calculated? Does it include all fully-loaded sales and marketing costs?
- What is the estimated LTV? What assumptions are made about customer lifetime, retention rate, and ARPU?
- What is the LTV:CAC ratio? Is it >3:1 (sustainable) or <2:1 (unsustainable)?
- What is the payback period? Is it <12 months (excellent), 12–18 months (good), or >18 months (concerning)?
- How do unit economics differ by customer segment? Which segments have best/worst unit economics?
- If unit economics are currently poor, what is the plan to improve them (price increase, retention improvement, cost reduction)?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **CAC calculation** showing fully-loaded sales and marketing costs divided by customers acquired
- **CAC by segment** if segments have different acquisition costs
- **LTV calculation** based on ARPU, gross margin, and churn rate
- **Payback period** showing months to pay back CAC
- **LTV:CAC ratio** with benchmark (should be >3:1 for sustainable growth)
- **Gross margin** target (typically 70%+ for SaaS)
- **Path to improvement** if initial unit economics are poor
- **Sensitivity analysis** showing how changes in churn, pricing, or CAC affect unit economics

## Module 5: Operating Leverage

### Definition
The degree to which operating expenses can grow slower than revenue, enabling profitability and scaling. Operating leverage is high when the business can grow revenue significantly without proportionally growing operating expenses (e.g., SaaS businesses with digital distribution). Operating leverage is low when operating expenses must grow with revenue (e.g., consulting or services businesses where headcount grows with revenue).

### What Good Looks Like

- **Leverage identified**: The submission identifies sources of operating leverage. For SaaS: "Leverage comes from digital distribution (marketing reach scales faster than cost), platform scalability (one product serves many customers), and efficient customer support (self-service and community reduce per-customer support cost)."
- **Revenue scale without cost scale**: The submission shows how revenue will scale faster than operating expenses. Example: "Year 1: $500K revenue, $1M OpEx, OpEx ratio = 200%. Year 3: $5M revenue, $2M OpEx, OpEx ratio = 40%. Operating leverage increases as we spread fixed costs over larger revenue base."
- **Profitability pathway**: The submission articulates a clear path to profitability and shows the operating leverage driver. Example: "At $5M ARR, OpEx is 40% of revenue; variable costs (COGS) are 20%; net margin is 40%. This path depends on customer acquisition efficiency improving (CAC declining) and retention improving (churn declining)."
- **Leverage constraints acknowledged**: The submission identifies constraints on leverage. Example: "Leverage is constrained by customer support costs, which grow linearly with customers; to improve leverage, we must invest in self-service and product-driven support."
- **Headcount efficiency**: The submission shows improving revenue per head as the company scales. Example: "Year 1: $500K revenue / 5 employees = $100K per head. Year 3: $5M revenue / 12 employees = $417K per head. This 4x improvement in headcount efficiency is driven by R&D leverage (one team serves more customers) and scaling sales."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **No operating leverage identified**: Operating expenses are assumed to grow proportionally with revenue.
- **Leverage too aggressive**: Assumes revenue can grow 10x while operating expenses remain flat; unrealistic.
- **No profitability pathway**: Shows scale but no path to profitability; assumes losses will continue indefinitely.
- **Headcount not modeled**: Does not show how staffing will grow with revenue or whether headcount efficiency improves.
- **Service business without leverage acknowledgment**: A services business with labor-intensive delivery assuming high operating leverage; unsustainable.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What sources of operating leverage exist in this business model? (Digital distribution, platform scalability, self-service, etc.)
- How will operating expenses grow relative to revenue growth? At what revenue level does the company break even?
- What is the timeline to profitability? Is this consistent with available capital?
- How much headcount is required to hit revenue targets? What is the revenue per employee at scale?
- What constraints limit operating leverage, and how will these be addressed?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Identified sources of leverage** (digital distribution, platform scalability, self-service, shared services)
- **Operating expense as % of revenue** modeled over time (should decline as company scales)
- **Profitability timeline** showing when company breaks even
- **Headcount model** showing staffing by function and revenue per employee
- **Leverage constraints** identified and mitigation plan
- **Financial model** showing EBITDA margin progression

## Module 6: Revenue Quality

### Definition
Assessment of the sustainability and predictability of revenue. High-quality revenue is predictable, recurring, concentrated among stable customers, and not dependent on one-time events or low-quality customers. Low-quality revenue is unpredictable, one-time, concentrated, or dependent on customer churn.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Recurring vs. one-time**: The submission specifies what percentage of revenue is recurring (subscription, usage) vs. one-time (implementation, services, licenses). Example: "75% of revenue is recurring subscription; 25% is one-time implementation services."
- **Customer concentration**: The submission discloses customer concentration (% revenue from top 10 customers). Example: "Top 10 customers represent 35% of revenue; no single customer is >8% of revenue. This is appropriately diversified."
- **Churn and retention**: The submission articulates customer churn and retention. Example: "Gross revenue retention (including expansions): 115%; customer churn: 10% annually; this indicates strong product-market fit."
- **Cohort economics**: For mature businesses, the submission shows cohort-level economics. Example: "2024 customer cohort: 90% one-year retention; 75% two-year retention; average customer lifetime 7 years."
- **Payment quality**: For businesses with longer contracts, the submission discloses payment terms and any bad debt. Example: "90% of customers are on monthly or annual paid-in-advance terms; bad debt is <1% of revenue."
- **Revenue stability**: The submission shows revenue growth and stability (not lumpy or dependent on large deals closing). Example: "Revenue has grown 20% QoQ for 6 consecutive quarters with <5% QoQ volatility; this indicates reliable underlying demand."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **High percentage of one-time revenue**: >50% of revenue is from services, implementation, or one-time licenses; not recurring.
- **High customer concentration**: Top 3 customers are >50% of revenue; business is fragile if any customer leaves.
- **High churn**: Customer churn is >20% annually; revenue is not sustainable.
- **No churn/retention disclosed**: The submission does not report retention or churn metrics.
- **Revenue lumpy from large deals**: Revenue growth is inconsistent based on closing of large individual deals.
- **No bad debt disclosure**: For businesses with deferred payments, no disclosure of bad debt.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What percentage of revenue is recurring vs. one-time?
- What is customer concentration? What percentage of revenue comes from top 10 customers? Top customer?
- What is customer retention rate? What is annual churn rate?
- For mature businesses, what is cohort-level retention over time?
- What is the revenue growth rate and consistency (month-to-month volatility)?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Revenue composition** (% recurring, % one-time) with trend over time
- **Customer concentration** (% revenue from top 10, top single customer, Herfindahl index if available)
- **Retention and churn metrics** (gross and net revenue retention, customer churn rate)
- **Cohort analysis** showing retention by cohort/year
- **Revenue visibility** (months/quarters of revenue in signed contracts or LOIs)
- **Bad debt or write-off** disclosure

## Module 7: Customer Economics

### Definition
Assessment of the economic health of the customer base—whether customers are profitable, whether they are expanding within accounts, and whether the business is attracting the right customer quality. This includes expansion revenue, logo retention, and net revenue retention (NRR), which measures the percentage of prior-year revenue retained and expanded through upsells.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Net Revenue Retention calculated**: The submission calculates NRR (also called net dollar retention), which includes churn and expansion. Example: "Year 1 cohort: $100K ARR at end of year. Year 2: $95K from same customers (5% churn) + $10K from expansion/upsell = $105K. NRR = 105%."
- **Logo retention vs. revenue retention**: The submission distinguishes between logo retention (% of customers retained) and revenue retention (% of revenue retained). Example: "Logo retention: 90%; NRR: 110%. This indicates we're retaining most customers and expanding revenue in retained customers."
- **Expansion revenue quantified**: The submission specifies what percentage of revenue comes from expansion in existing customers vs. new customer acquisition. Example: "New customer acquisition: 60% of revenue; expansion in existing customers: 40%. This is a mature business model where retained customers generate growing revenue."
- **Customer quality assessment**: The submission assesses whether the company is attracting the right customers (customers likely to succeed, expand, and stay). Example: "Ideal customer profile: $50M+ annual revenue, decentralized procurement, high digital maturity. These customers have 90% one-year retention and 2.5x expansion rate."
- **Upsell and cross-sell strategy**: The submission articulates how the company will drive expansion revenue. Example: "We upsell additional user seats as customers' teams grow; cross-sell complementary modules (e.g., from AP automation to expense management); we actively identify expansion opportunities through quarterly business reviews."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **NRR not calculated**: The submission does not report net revenue retention.
- **No expansion revenue**: The business model depends entirely on new customer acquisition; no expansion revenue.
- **High churn despite expansion claims**: Claims to have 110% NRR, but underlying churn is >20%, which undermines sustainability.
- **No ICP or customer quality assessment**: Does not acknowledge that different customers have different economics; treats all customers the same.
- **Expansion not planned**: No strategy for driving expansion revenue or upsells.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the Net Revenue Retention (NRR)? Is it >100% (indicating expansion revenue exceeds churn)?
- What is logo retention (% of customers retained) vs. revenue retention (% of revenue retained)?
- What percentage of new revenue comes from expansion in existing customers vs. new acquisition?
- What is the ideal customer profile? Do early customers match the ICP?
- What is the expansion strategy (upsell, cross-sell, usage-based expansion)?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Net Revenue Retention metric** (typically >100% indicates healthy expansion)
- **Logo retention vs. revenue retention** showing both metrics
- **Expansion revenue as % of total revenue** or customer segment
- **Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)** definition and early customer fit to ICP
- **Upsell and cross-sell strategy** showing mechanics of expansion
- **Cohort-level expansion** showing how expansion rate changes with customer tenure

## Module 8: Monetization Sequencing

### Definition
The strategy for rolling out monetization over time—whether the company launches fully monetized, uses freemium to drive adoption, or sequences monetization in phases. Monetization sequencing is a strategic choice that impacts early metrics, customer acquisition, and time to revenue.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Monetization strategy articulated**: The submission is explicit about when monetization begins. Example: "Phase 1 (Months 1–6): Free/freemium to drive adoption and build network effects; Phase 2 (Months 7–12): Introduce paid tier for advanced users; Phase 3 (Year 2): Full monetization with premium pricing."
- **Rationale for sequencing**: The submission explains why this sequencing is optimal. Example: "We use freemium to overcome chicken-and-egg problem (network effects require critical mass); once we have 10K users, we introduce paid premium tier for power users; estimated conversion is 5% of free to paid."
- **Conversion mechanics**: If using freemium, the submission specifies conversion strategy. Example: "Free tier supports up to 100 transactions/month; premium tier supports unlimited transactions and advanced features. Estimated 30% of free users exceed free tier limits within 3 months, driving conversion."
- **Phase timing**: The submission specifies when each monetization phase begins. Example: "Q4 2024: Free tier launch; Q2 2025: Premium tier launch; Q4 2025: Enterprise tier launch."
- **Revenue impact modeled**: The submission models how monetization sequencing affects revenue timeline. Example: "Freemium phase generates $0 revenue; at Month 7, premium tier launch, estimated 5K of 50K users convert at $50/year = $250K ARR; by Month 12, 8K conversions = $400K ARR."
- **Risk mitigation**: The submission acknowledges risks (e.g., difficulty in converting free to paid) and mitigation plans. Example: "Risk: Free users resist payment; mitigation: Use segmentation to identify high-value free users early and drive conversion through premium features."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Monetization not specified**: "We'll figure out monetization later" with no plan.
- **Unrealistic conversion assumptions**: "10% of free users will convert to paid" with no evidence; typical SaaS free-to-paid conversion is 2–5%.
- **No monetization timeline**: Unclear when paid tier launches or what triggers each phase.
- **Monetization assumed to be painless**: Assumes free-to-paid conversion will not impact growth; typically conversion reduces growth rate.
- **No risk acknowledgment**: Does not acknowledge that monetization may trigger churn or slow adoption.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the monetization sequencing strategy? When does the company transition from free to paid?
- Why is this sequencing optimal for achieving the company's goals (e.g., network effects, adoption, quick to revenue)?
- If using freemium, what is the conversion strategy? What percentage of free users are expected to convert, and by when?
- What is the revenue impact of each monetization phase? When does the company break even on customer acquisition?
- What risks are associated with monetization transitions, and how will these be mitigated?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Monetization strategy** (immediate, phased, freemium-to-paid, value-based, etc.)
- **Sequencing timeline** showing when each phase launches
- **Conversion mechanics** (if freemium) showing how free users convert to paid
- **Conversion rate assumptions** (realistic benchmarks: 2–5% for B2B SaaS)
- **Revenue impact modeling** showing revenue trajectory under different conversion scenarios
- **Risk mitigation** acknowledging conversion/churn risks

## Cross-Domain Dependencies

The Business Model domain feeds into and is informed by:

- **Domain 1 (Market and Opportunity)**: Market sizing and customer segmentation drive revenue targets and unit economics assumptions. Customer willingness to pay (from demand validation) informs pricing.
- **Domain 2 (Solution and Product)**: Product cost structure drives COGS and gross margin assumptions. Product roadmap and feature complexity affect operating expenses.
- **Domain 4 (Go-to-Market)**: Sales and marketing strategy drives CAC assumptions. Channel mix affects CAC by channel and revenue per channel.
- **Domain 5 (Traction)**: Early customer revenue, churn, and expansion validate business model assumptions. Actual unit economics must align with projections.

Conversely, these domains inform business model assessment:

- **Traction**: Actual customer acquisition costs, churn rates, and retention must align with business model assumptions.
- **Go-to-Market**: Sales efficiency and channel performance directly impact CAC.
- **Product**: Product pricing power and customer willingness to pay affect revenue potential.

---

## Assessment Notes for the Scoring Agent

When assessing the Business Model domain:

1. **Validate unit economics realism**: Are CAC, LTV, and payback assumptions grounded in market research or comparable companies? Be skeptical of "our CAC will be $5K" without supporting evidence.

2. **Check math on unit economics**: Verify LTV:CAC ratio, payback period, and gross margin calculations. Math errors are common and indicate weak financial thinking.

3. **Assess gross margin reasonableness**: For SaaS, gross margin <60% is concerning; <70% may indicate high COGS or inefficient delivery. For marketplace or transaction businesses, margins may be lower.

4. **Evaluate operating leverage**: Does the business model support scaling? Can the company grow revenue faster than operating expenses? If operating expenses grow with revenue (like services), ask how the company achieves scale and profitability.

5. **Stress-test unit economics**: How do unit economics change if CAC increases 25%, churn increases 5%, or pricing must decrease 20% due to competition? Is the business still viable?

6. **Assess revenue quality**: High customer concentration, high churn, or one-time revenue are red flags. NRR >100% and logo retention >90% are green flags.

7. **Validate monetization strategy**: If using freemium, are conversion assumptions realistic? (<2% is concerning; 5–10% is strong). If monetizing immediately, is there evidence customers will pay?

8. **Check for sufficient capital**: Do unit economics and operating leverage support the business to profitability within expected capital runway? If CAC is $20K and gross margin is 60%, and the company is raising $500K, how many customers can be acquired before running out of money?

## Summary: Good Business Model Assessment

A strong Business Model assessment demonstrates:

- **Clear revenue model** aligned with customer value and industry norms
- **Realistic pricing** validated through customer research
- **Positive unit economics** with LTV:CAC >3:1 and payback <18 months
- **Operating leverage** showing path to profitability and sustainable scale
- **High-quality revenue** that is recurring, diversified, and growing
- **Customer expansion** driving NRR >100% and high retention
- **Sequenced monetization** (if applicable) with realistic conversion assumptions

Business model strength is often the difference between a venture-scale outcome and a lifestyle business or failure. Strong business model + execution wins.
