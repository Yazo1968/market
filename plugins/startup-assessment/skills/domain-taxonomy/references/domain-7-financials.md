# Domain 7: Financial Performance and Projections

## Domain Overview

Financial Performance and Projections is the quantitative foundation of investment decision-making. This domain assesses the quality and credibility of the company's historical financial performance (if applicable), the rigor and assumptions underlying financial projections, the path to profitability and cash generation, and the capital requirements to reach key milestones.

Strong financial management and realistic projections are critical signals of execution discipline and capital efficiency. Early-stage startups with poor financial controls, inaccurate projections, or unclear paths to unit economics profitability will face challenges in raising subsequent capital and returning value to investors. Conversely, companies that meticulously track metrics, model customer acquisition costs, and optimize burn rate demonstrate operational maturity and increase outcome probability.

This domain is typically the highest-leverage assessment area because:
- It directly influences valuation and capital raise feasibility
- Financial projections inform risk mitigation strategies and contingency planning
- Unit economics (LTV, CAC, payback period) determine scalability and profitability trajectory
- Cash flow forecasts determine runway and capital timing needs
- Debt capacity and return potential are derived from financial trajectory

## Module 1: Historical Financials

### Definition
Audited or quality-assured financial statements (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow statement) for all prior fiscal years. For early-stage pre-revenue or minimal-revenue companies, this may be management accounts; for Series B+ companies or those seeking debt, audited statements are expected.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Financial statements audited or reviewed by reputable third-party accounting firm (Big 4, mid-market, or specialized audit firm)
- Complete statements: P&L showing gross profit, operating expenses, EBITDA, and net income; balance sheet; cash flow statement
- Statements prepared in accordance with GAAP or IFRS; consistent accounting principles year-over-year
- Clear reconciliation between actual cash spent and P&L accruals (accounts payable, accounts receivable, deferred revenue)
- Supporting schedules for major line items: revenue by customer or segment, headcount and compensation by function, cap ex by category
- Clean audit opinion; no significant deficiencies or material weaknesses in internal controls noted by auditor
- Financial controls in place: monthly close process, documented accounting policies, segregation of duties in payment/approval
- Explanations for major variances: if OpEx is higher than prior year, the driver is documented (new hire, new vendor, etc.)
- Tax filings (1099, 1040, corporate returns) consistent with financial statements
- Bank statements and credit card statements available to reconcile to monthly cash flow

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- No financial statements; only "spreadsheet estimates" of revenue and expenses
- P&L with large unexplained variances year-over-year or month-to-month
- Balance sheet with suspiciously round numbers or lack of detail
- Cash flow statement that doesn't reconcile to bank statements (cash in/out don't match deposits/withdrawals)
- No audit or review; management accounts prepared by founders without third-party validation
- Large accrual entries (revenue or expenses) that are not supported by contracts or invoices
- Accounting principles changed year-to-year without explanation
- Major line items missing: no cost of goods sold breakdown, no R&D or Sales breakdown
- Red flags: founder unable to explain how specific line item was calculated, missing supporting invoices, delayed month-end close
- Multiple versions of "financials" circulating with different numbers

### Key Assessment Questions
1. Are financial statements audited, reviewed, or compiled by a third party? By whom?
2. What is the accounting framework (GAAP, IFRS, other)?
3. How long does month-end close take? Is it documented and repeatable?
4. Can you trace a significant P&L line item (e.g., sales commission expense, cloud infrastructure cost) to supporting documentation?
5. What is the accounts receivable balance? How much is collected, how much outstanding 30/60/90 days?
6. What is the accounts payable balance? How much is current, how much is deferred?
7. Are there any significant accruals or adjusting entries? Are they supported by contracts or agreements?
8. What is the deferred revenue balance? Over how many periods will it be recognized?
9. Are there any related-party transactions (founder loans, related-company purchases)? How are they disclosed?
10. What is the cash balance and how does it align with bank statements?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Most recent 2-3 years of audited or reviewed P&L, balance sheet, and cash flow statement
- Audit opinion or review letter from accounting firm (if applicable)
- Detailed revenue schedule: by customer, by product line, by geography, or by service type
- Detailed OpEx schedule: by department/function, by expense category, with major line items itemized
- Bank statements and cash flow reconciliation for most recent month
- Aging of accounts receivable and accounts payable
- Schedule of significant accruals and their supporting contracts
- Any material related-party transactions and their business rationale
- Copy of most recent tax return and evidence it reconciles to P&L

## Module 2: Revenue Projections

### Definition
Explicit forward revenue projections by stream (product, service, geographic market, customer segment, etc.) with driver-based assumptions. Revenue projections should be granular and defensible, not top-down extrapolations.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Revenue broken down by stream: B2B SaaS by product, marketplace by buyer and seller, commerce by category, etc.
- Bottom-up build: pipeline-driven (weighted sales opportunities), cohort-driven (new customer cohorts and retention), or unit-driven (units shipped or seats sold)
- For sales-driven revenue: explicit opportunity pipeline with win rates and sales cycle by stage and segment
- For SMB or product-led: cohort analysis showing new customer acquisition rate, churn rate, and expansion per cohort
- Clear driver assumptions: customer acquisition rate (CAC), customer lifetime value (LTV), average contract value (ACV), expansion rate
- Sensitivity analysis: how does revenue change if customer acquisition rate is 20% lower or if churn increases by 2 points?
- Comparisons to comparable public companies or industry benchmarks: is 50% YoY growth reasonable for the segment?
- Conservative relative to market opportunity: revenue projections don't assume 100% market capture or penetration
- Updated quarterly: projections updated as actual data comes in and assumptions are validated or revised
- Credibility signals: projections miss by <20%, not by 3-5x; team quickly adjusts if underlying assumptions prove wrong

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Revenue forecast is top-down: "large market, if we get 1% market share, that's $X revenue"
- No underlying drivers: just extrapolation of current growth rate forward (e.g., "if we grow 50% next year, revenue will be $10M")
- Pipeline projections with unrealistic win rates: 80%+ win rates on early-stage pipeline
- Projections not updated in 12+ months; initial forecast treated as gospel regardless of actual results
- No cohort or unit-level analysis for recurring revenue businesses
- Expansion revenue (upsell, cross-sell) projected with no data on historical expansion rates
- Projections include new products or segments with no pilot data or customer commitments
- Vast overestimation: projections consistently miss by 3-5x, suggesting fundamental flaws in assumptions
- No acknowledgment of churn: revenue projections assume zero customer loss
- Hidden assumptions: revenue driver not explicitly stated; requires reverse-engineering to understand

### Key Assessment Questions
1. How is revenue broken down? By what dimensions (product, segment, channel, geography)?
2. For each revenue stream, what are the key drivers (e.g., CAC, LTV, ACV, churn, expansion)?
3. How is new customer acquisition rate calculated? Is it based on pipeline, past conversion rates, or market estimate?
4. What is the historical customer churn rate, and how is it incorporated into projections?
5. For recurring revenue, what is the expected expansion rate (upsell/cross-sell) per customer cohort?
6. How do revenue projections compare to historical growth rates? Is the growth assumption accelerating, stable, or decelerating?
7. What is the largest customer concentration? What percentage of revenue is from top 3 customers?
8. Are there committed customers or pipeline that underpin revenue projections?
9. How have prior-year revenue projections compared to actual results? What was the variance?
10. What happens to revenue projections if customer acquisition cost increases by 50% or churn increases by 2 points?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Revenue projection model: 3-5 year forward revenue by stream, with assumptions clearly labeled
- Customer pipeline analysis: opportunity stage, close probability, sales cycle, contract value for deals in pipeline
- For recurring revenue: cohort analysis showing new customer acquisition rate, churn rate, expansion rate by cohort
- Historical revenue growth: actual revenue by month/quarter for past 2+ years
- Comparable company analysis: revenue growth rates of similar-stage or similar-model companies
- Sensitivity tables: how does year 3 revenue change if CAC is 20% higher, or churn is 2 points higher?
- Updated projections: evidence that projections are updated quarterly or annually as new data comes in
- Major customer contracts or letters of intent that support projected revenue

## Module 3: Cost and Margin Projections

### Definition
Explicit projections of cost of goods sold (COGS) and operating expenses (OpEx) by category, with driver-based assumptions. Assess the path to gross margin and EBITDA profitability, efficiency of expense scaling, and visibility into key cost levers.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- COGS broken down by component: cloud infrastructure, payment processing, cost of customer support, cost of goods shipped, etc.
- Gross margin trajectory: gross margin % improves from current state to target as scale increases (economy of scale, lower CAC blended rate)
- For cloud/SaaS businesses: COGS as low percentage of revenue (20-30%) with path to <20% at scale
- For hardware/commerce: gross margin target reasonable for segment (e.g., 40%+ for branded consumer hardware, 50%+ for marketplace)
- OpEx broken down by function: Sales & Marketing, R&D, General & Administrative, with sub-categories
- S&M efficiency improving over time: magic number (incremental revenue per $ S&M spent) of 0.75+ at maturity
- Headcount plan explicit: number of employees by function for each period, with ratios (S&M as % of revenue, R&D as % of revenue)
- Inflation and compensation assumptions stated: annual salary growth of 3-5%, inflation adjustment for cloud costs
- No "hockey stick" EBITDA improvement: margin improvement is gradual and reasonable, not sudden profitability at Year 3
- Cost assumptions benchmarked: how do S&M as % of revenue and R&D as % of revenue compare to public comps at similar revenue stage?

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- COGS estimates that don't align with actual product delivery model (e.g., claiming 10% COGS for software service with significant human labor)
- Gross margin improving from 20% to 80% overnight with no explanation of cost reduction strategy
- OpEx projections treated as uniform: same OpEx dollars in Year 1 and Year 5, suggesting no growth or wrong assumption
- S&M spending that stays flat while revenue grows 10x (impossible without changing go-to-market model)
- No breakdown of OpEx by function: single "OpEx" line item with no visibility into spend distribution
- Unrealistic efficiency ratios: S&M to revenue <10% when company is in customer acquisition mode
- Major cost categories missing: no allocation for G&A, no adjustment for tax, no working capital changes
- Headcount plans that don't align with revenue growth or functional needs (e.g., zero new hires while revenue doubles)
- EBITDA margin improvements not explained: should document how costs will scale sublinearly to revenue

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is current gross margin, and what is the target gross margin at scale? Over what timeline?
2. What are the major components of COGS, and how will each scale with revenue?
3. What is the current S&M spend as % of revenue, and how will this evolve as company scales?
4. What is the headcount plan by function for the next 3-5 years?
5. What are the key cost assumptions (salary growth, inflation, cloud pricing)?
6. When does the company break even on EBITDA? What profitability does the model show at exit (Year 5-7)?
7. What is the magic number (incremental revenue per $ S&M spend)? How does this improve over time?
8. How does the company's margin profile compare to public comps at similar revenue stage?
9. What are the main cost levers (areas where 20% savings would have outsized impact)?
10. How has the model for COGS evolved as actual product delivery experience accumulates?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- COGS and OpEx projections: 3-5 year forecast by major category, with assumptions documented
- Gross margin trajectory: current to target, with explanation of how margin will improve
- S&M spend by channel: how much is being spent on direct sales, digital marketing, partnerships?
- Headcount plan: number of employees by function and seniority for each year
- Compensation assumptions: salary ranges by role and expected annual salary growth
- Cost benchmarking: comparison of S&M/revenue, R&D/revenue, and other ratios to public companies at similar stage
- EBITDA path: projections showing when company reaches break-even and positive EBITDA
- Sensitivity analysis: how does EBITDA change if gross margin is 5 points lower, or S&M spend is 20% higher?

## Module 4: Cash Flow Projections

### Definition
Explicit projections of operating, investing, and financing cash flows, including working capital changes (receivables, payables, inventory). Cash flow is the ultimate driver of company survival and runway. A company can be profitable on accrual basis but burn cash if working capital needs are high.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Operating cash flow forecast: net income adjusted for non-cash items and working capital changes
- Working capital management explicit: assumptions for Days Sales Outstanding (DSO), Days Payable Outstanding (DPO), Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO)
- DSO and DPO aligned with business model: SaaS companies have negative working capital (cash upfront); B2B services have positive working capital
- Cash conversion cycle (CCC = DSO + DIO - DPO): if negative, company is cash generative; if positive, company consumes cash as it grows
- Capex explicitly modeled: server capacity, office buildout, equipment, etc., with timing
- Change in deferred revenue explicit: when customers pay upfront (SaaS), this is a source of cash; as revenue is recognized, it becomes a use
- Cash flow break-even forecast: when does the company stop burning cash and begin generating positive operating cash flow?
- Sensitivity to growth: what happens to cash burn if revenue is 20% lower than projection?
- Financing assumptions: when will the company raise next round? How much capital needed to reach cash flow positive?

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Cash flow forecast is not prepared; only P&L and balance sheet projections provided
- Working capital changes ignored: company with 60-day payment terms and growing receivables may need significantly more cash than P&L suggests
- Capex underestimated or omitted: especially problematic for capital-intensive businesses (hardware, real estate, infrastructure)
- Deferred revenue treated as P&L revenue without modeling cash impact
- Cash flow projections that assume instant cash conversion (no days sales outstanding) when customers typically pay 30-60 days
- Timing mismatches: large expense payment in Month 1 of projection but no corresponding revenue until Month 6
- No contingency or sensitivity: single-point forecast with no "what if growth is 20% lower?"
- Cash runway not calculated: company may run out of cash before next financing event but this not highlighted
- Negative cash flow assumed indefinitely: no plan or path to cash break-even

### Key Assessment Questions
1. When does the company reach positive operating cash flow? What is the timeline?
2. What is the working capital impact of the business model (days sales outstanding, days payable outstanding)?
3. How much capex is required in the next 3 years? When is it incurred?
4. If revenue is 20% lower than projections, how much longer will the company have runway?
5. How much change in deferred revenue is expected? When will this cash be recognized as revenue?
6. What is the current cash balance, and what is the runway (months of burn) at current burn rate?
7. How much capital is needed to reach cash flow positive?
8. Are there working capital opportunities (faster collections, extended payables) that could improve cash?
9. What is the timing of the next capital raise relative to runway?
10. How sensitive is cash runway to the key revenue and expense assumptions?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- 3-5 year cash flow forecast: operating cash flow, investing cash flow, financing cash flow, with ending cash balance by period
- Working capital assumptions: Days Sales Outstanding (receivable collection), Days Payable Outstanding (payable payment), Days Inventory Outstanding
- Capex schedule: timing and amount of capital expenditures by category
- Deferred revenue bridge: beginning balance, customer prepayments, revenue recognized, ending balance
- Current cash balance and runway calculation: months of cash at current burn rate
- Operating cash flow to EBITDA bridge: reconciliation showing how net income converts to operating cash flow
- Sensitivity analysis: cash runway if revenue is 20% lower or 20% higher
- Financing plan: amount and timing of next capital raise, use of proceeds, and how it extends runway

## Module 5: Balance Sheet Projections

### Definition
Explicit projections of assets (cash, receivables, equipment, intangibles), liabilities (payables, debt, deferred revenue), and equity over the projection period. Balance sheet projections reveal capital structure evolution, working capital trends, and return of capital to equity holders.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Assets clearly categorized: current assets (cash, receivables, inventory) and non-current (property, equipment, intangibles, goodwill)
- Liabilities clearly categorized: current (payables, accrued expenses, current portion of debt) and non-current (long-term debt, deferred revenue)
- Depreciation and amortization schedule explicit: timing and useful lives of asset write-downs
- Intangible assets: goodwill or capitalized software properly amortized if applicable
- Cap table clarity: equity split between founders, employees, and investors with implied ownership percentages
- Debt schedule if applicable: principal amount, interest rate, maturity, payment schedule
- Working capital trends analyzed: accounts receivable and payable balances align with revenue and OpEx growth
- Balance sheet constraints checked: current ratio (current assets / current liabilities) at least 1.0x; debt-to-equity aligned with stage
- No surprises in assumptions: balance sheet doesn't contain "plug" numbers or unexplained large balances

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Balance sheet totals don't balance: assets != liabilities + equity
- Major balance sheet categories listed but not explained (e.g., "intangibles $500K" with no explanation)
- Depreciation ignored: long-lived assets on balance sheet with zero assumption for depreciation or amortization
- Deferred revenue on balance sheet but not clearly explained or linked to revenue recognition policy
- Cap table unclear or contradictory: ownership percentages don't add to 100%, or cap table not updated for recent raises
- Debt terms vague or missing: amount of debt but no interest rate, maturity, or covenants stated
- Equity projections that don't account for future dilution from new funding rounds
- Balance sheet relationships that don't make sense: receivables growing 50% faster than revenue, suggesting collection problems
- No link to P&L: company projects $5M revenue in Year 2 but balance sheet shows $0 receivables, suggesting immediate cash collection

### Key Assessment Questions
1. Are current assets (cash, receivables) and current liabilities (payables, current debt) explicitly stated?
2. What is the working capital position (current assets minus current liabilities)?
3. What is the accounts receivable balance and aging? How many days of revenue is outstanding?
4. What is the accounts payable balance and aging? What is the company's payment schedule?
5. Are there accrued expenses (salary, bonuses, taxes)? What is the magnitude and timing?
6. Is there debt on the balance sheet? What is the principal, interest rate, and maturity?
7. What is the cap table? Who owns what percentage (founders, employees, investors)?
8. Are there options, warrants, or convertibles outstanding? How diluted is the fully-diluted equity?
9. What is the equity position (total assets minus total liabilities)? Is this positive and growing?
10. How will balance sheet change if next funding round occurs? How diluted will current holders be?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Current balance sheet: assets, liabilities, equity as of the most recent date
- 3-5 year projected balance sheets: showing how assets, liabilities, and equity evolve
- Asset schedule: detail on major asset balances (receivables, equipment, intangibles) and their projection
- Liability schedule: detail on payables, debt, deferred revenue, and how each evolves
- Cap table: cap table as of most recent date, showing founders, investors, employees, options
- Fully-diluted equity: cap table including all options, warrants, convertibles to show fully-diluted ownership
- Debt schedule: amount, interest rate, maturity date, payment schedule, and covenants
- Working capital analysis: accounts receivable, payable, inventory aging; cash conversion cycle
- Depreciation and amortization schedule: basis of fixed assets, useful lives, accumulated depreciation

## Module 6: Key Assumptions

### Definition
Explicit statement of every material assumption driving revenue, COGS, OpEx, cash flow, and balance sheet projections. Assumptions must be granular, defensible, and compared to historical data and industry benchmarks. Vague or hidden assumptions are a major red flag.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Assumptions document: single source of truth listing all assumptions with rationale and benchmarks
- Every assumption has a data source: "CAC of $500 based on YTD actual of $475, trending down as we optimize," not "estimated $500"
- Assumptions grouped by category: unit economics, headcount, cost inflation, working capital, financing
- Benchmarks provided: CAC assumption of $500 cited against public SaaS benchmarks of $300-700 for segment
- Historical validation: for recurring revenue, churn assumption of 5% per month based on YTD churn of 4.8%
- Sensitivity identified: document highlights the 3-5 assumptions that have outsized impact on outcomes
- Assumptions reviewed and updated regularly: quarterly or as new data comes in
- Drivers explicit: if revenue growth is 50%, the assumption should state "CAC $500 x 100k new customers x 2.5x expansion"
- Conservative where material: when uncertainty is high, assumptions bias toward conservative (lower revenue, higher expenses)

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- No assumptions document; financials presented without explanation of how numbers were derived
- Assumptions stated without support: "50% customer churn" with no data or comparison
- Hidden assumptions: have to reverse-engineer from financial statements to understand what was assumed
- Assumptions from outside sources not validated: "market research says market is $10B, so we'll get $100M" (0.1% share) with no defense
- Inconsistent assumptions: revenue model assumes 100K new customers at $500 ACV, but churn assumption implies stable customer base
- Spreadsheet errors: assumptions stated but financial projections don't match (e.g., stated CAC is $500 but implied CAC from revenue/budget is $750)
- No sensitivity identification: all assumptions treated as equally important; doesn't highlight which assumptions matter most
- Unrealistic assumptions: CAC = $0 (no customer acquisition cost), churn = 0% (no customer attrition), gross margin = 100%

### Key Assessment Questions
1. How is customer acquisition cost (CAC) calculated? Is it based on historical data or estimate?
2. How is customer lifetime value (LTV) calculated? What is the assumed customer lifespan and retention?
3. What is the churn assumption? Is it based on historical cohort data or industry benchmark?
4. What is the assumed expansion revenue per customer? Is it based on historical data?
5. What salary inflation is assumed? How does this compare to historical inflation and labor market trends?
6. What is the gross margin target, and how will it be achieved?
7. How is depreciation and amortization calculated? What are the useful lives assumed?
8. What is the tax rate assumption? How does this compare to statutory rates?
9. What discount rate (WACC) is used if doing NPV analysis or return forecasting?
10. Which 3-5 assumptions have the largest impact on outcomes (revenue, EBITDA, cash flow)?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Assumptions schedule or appendix: list of all material assumptions with values, rationale, and data sources
- Historical data: comparison of assumptions to actual historical performance by unit (CAC, LTV, churn, etc.)
- Benchmark data: comparison of assumptions to public company data, industry reports, or analyst estimates
- Sensitivity analysis: table showing impact on Year 3 revenue (or other key metric) if assumption varies by +/- 20%
- Key drivers identified: which 3-5 assumptions have outsized impact on business outcome
- Quarterly updates: evidence that assumptions are updated quarterly as new data comes in
- Spreadsheet validation: walk-through showing that stated assumptions are actually used in financial model

## Module 7: Scenario Analysis

### Definition
Projections across multiple scenarios (base case, upside, downside) with clear articulation of what happens in each case. Scenarios should vary the key assumption drivers (customer acquisition, retention, pricing, expansion) and show outcomes across a range of market or execution outcomes.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Three scenarios minimum: base case, upside case, downside case (could also include "management case" or "bull/bear" scenarios)
- Each scenario clearly described: what changes from base case? (e.g., "downside assumes CAC increases 40%, churn increases 3 points, pricing stays flat")
- Key metrics shown for each scenario: revenue at Year 3/5, EBITDA, cash flow to positive, IRR, MOIC
- Probability implied for each scenario (not always explicit, but reader should understand relative likelihood)
- Upside case plausible: not pure fantasy but aligned with market leader performance in category
- Downside case meaningful: not doomsday scenario but reflects realistic execution setbacks or competitive pressure
- Scenarios trigger different financing needs: base case might need two rounds; downside might need three rounds
- Return analysis by scenario: what multiple of invested capital is returned in each case?
- Sensitivity within scenarios: downside case further refined to show impact if downside assumptions are worse than expected

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Only base case provided; no scenario analysis
- Scenarios with no clear definition: "optimistic," "pessimistic" with no specifics on what changes
- Upside case assumes 100% market capture or 500% customer growth with no change in assumptions
- Downside case is more pessimistic than worst realistic outcome (e.g., revenue goes to zero, company shuts down)
- Scenarios don't impact financing: all scenarios show same amount of capital needed, suggesting assumptions aren't really different
- Return metrics not shown for each scenario: reader can't understand value outcome across scenarios
- Scenarios presented but no indication which is "most likely"
- Inconsistent assumptions: downside case has lower revenue but higher EBITDA margin (shouldn't happen; lower revenue usually means higher % OpEx)

### Key Assessment Questions
1. How is each scenario defined? What assumptions change from base case to upside/downside?
2. What is the base case revenue at Year 3? At Year 5?
3. What is the upside case revenue at Year 3? What would need to go right?
4. What is the downside case revenue at Year 3? What would need to go wrong?
5. How much capital is required in each scenario to reach cash flow positive?
6. What is the implied IRR or MOIC for investor in each scenario?
7. What is the probability attached to each scenario (implicit or explicit)?
8. Which scenario is most likely? Why does management believe that?
9. Are there "branch point" decisions where company pivots between scenarios (e.g., if initial TAM validation fails, shift go-to-market)?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Scenario definitions: clear description of what changes in upside, base, and downside cases
- Scenario financial projections: revenue, EBITDA, cash flow for each scenario at key years (Year 3, 5)
- Scenario outcomes: funding required, burn duration, break-even timing for each scenario
- Return analysis: IRR, MOIC, and exit value for each scenario
- Probability weighting: explicit or implicit indication of which scenario is most likely
- Waterfall showing capital requirements across scenarios: how much capital needed to reach cash flow positive?
- Sensitivity within scenarios: further analysis of "what if downside is even worse?"

## Module 8: Break-Even Analysis

### Definition
Explicit identification of unit break-even (when each customer generates more revenue than customer acquisition cost) and aggregate break-even (when company EBITDA becomes positive). Unit break-even is critical for recurring revenue models; aggregate break-even determines profitability and exit feasibility.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Unit economics clear: Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC), Customer Lifetime Value (LTV), and LTV:CAC ratio calculated
- LTV:CAC ratio of 3:1 or better at maturity: indicates sustainable unit economics
- Payback period explicit: how many months to recover CAC from customer revenue? (typically 12-18 months for healthy SaaS)
- Unit break-even already achieved or clearly on path: if not yet profitable per customer, timeline to unit profitability stated
- Aggregate break-even (EBITDA positive) forecast: year when company reaches positive EBITDA
- Contribution margin clarity: revenue minus COGS as % of revenue, showing high-margin per-unit economics
- Analysis of profitability path: how does company move from negative to positive EBITDA? What cost reduction or revenue expansion drives it?
- Gross margin per customer: if not already positive, gross margin trajectory shows path to positive

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- LTV:CAC ratio less than 3:1; suggests customer acquisition is expensive relative to lifetime value
- CAC payback period exceeding 24 months; customer may not generate sufficient lifetime revenue
- No unit economics analysis: company focuses on top-line revenue growth without regard to profitability per customer
- Negative gross margin: company loses money on every transaction; structural issue that scaling won't fix
- Aggregate break-even assumed to occur only after Series C or later: may require excessive capital relative to opportunity
- Break-even timeline vague: "will be profitable in 2-3 years" with no specific analysis of when
- Profitability dependent on unrealistic cost reduction: assumes cloud infrastructure costs to drop 80% with no catalyst
- Unit economics improving but timeline extending: LTV:CAC improves as company grows, but payback period extends (suggests pricing power erosion)

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the CAC for a new customer in each segment or channel?
2. What is the LTV for a customer cohort? Over how many years is this calculated?
3. What is the LTV:CAC ratio? Is it 3:1 or better?
4. What is the payback period for CAC? How many months until customer revenue exceeds acquisition cost?
5. What is gross margin per customer? Is it positive?
6. When does the company achieve unit profitability (per customer)?
7. When does the company achieve aggregate profitability (EBITDA positive)?
8. What assumptions drive the path to profitability? (margin improvement, reduced CAC, increased LTV)
9. How does this company's unit economics compare to public comps or industry benchmarks?
10. If unit economics never improve to 3:1 LTV:CAC, is the business model sustainable?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Unit economics calculation: CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC ratio by segment or channel
- Payback period: months from CAC spend to recovery from customer revenue
- Cohort analysis: showing unit economics improving (or degrading) by cohort over time
- Gross margin per customer: contribution after COGS, showing path to profitability per unit
- Aggregate break-even analysis: forecast of when EBITDA becomes positive
- Sensitivity of break-even to key assumptions: how does timeline change if CAC is 20% higher, or LTV is 10% lower?
- Comparison to public comps: benchmarking unit economics against similar-stage or mature companies

## Module 9: Debt Service Analysis

### Definition
For companies with debt (or planning to take debt), explicit analysis of debt service capacity, coverage ratios, and repayment schedule. Critical for evaluating financial risk and lender perspective on viability.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Debt schedule: principal amount, interest rate, maturity, payment frequency, and covenants
- Debt service coverage ratio (DSCR) forecast: operating cash flow / debt service; target of 1.25x or higher
- Interest coverage ratio: EBITDA / interest expense; typically required to be 1.5x or higher by lenders
- Covenant compliance: company remains compliant with all covenants (max debt/EBITDA, minimum current ratio, max capex)
- Repayment priority: debt service is first priority; projections show sufficient cash to service debt before distributions
- Refinancing plan: if debt matures before company reaches profitability, plan for refinancing or payoff articulated
- Multiple tranches: if multiple debt instruments, clear waterfall of repayment priority
- Stress test: what if EBITDA is 20% lower? Does company still service debt?
- No covenant breaches projected: projections show company staying within covenant thresholds

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Debt terms vague: amount and interest rate stated, but maturity and covenants not mentioned
- DSCR below 1.0x: company cannot service debt from operating cash flow in any scenario
- Covenant breaches: debt projections show company would violate covenants in base case or downside
- No repayment plan: debt assumed to somehow disappear or be refinanced without analysis
- Maturity cliff: significant debt principal due with no clear repayment or refinancing source
- Overlapping debt covenants: multiple creditors with conflicting requirements
- Amortization schedule not aligned with cash generation: company asked to pay down debt faster than it generates cash
- Interest expense treated as operational burden, not critical obligation

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the total amount of debt? What is the interest rate and maturity?
2. What is the debt service schedule (principal + interest payment by period)?
3. What is the projected Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)? Is it above 1.25x?
4. What are the covenants? What are the thresholds for max debt/EBITDA, min current ratio, max capex?
5. Will the company remain in compliance with all covenants in base, upside, and downside scenarios?
6. If debt matures before company reaches profitability, what is the refinancing plan?
7. What is the interest coverage ratio (EBITDA / interest expense)?
8. Is there a waterfall of debt repayment priority (senior vs. subordinated)?
9. What amount of equity cushion exists relative to debt obligations?
10. How sensitive is debt service to revenue or EBITDA assumptions?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Debt schedule: principal amount, interest rate, maturity date, repayment frequency, and covenants for each debt instrument
- Debt service projections: principal and interest payment by period (monthly or quarterly for near-term)
- DSCR and interest coverage analysis: showing compliance with typical covenant thresholds
- Covenant compliance forecast: showing company remains compliant in base case and downside
- Refinancing plan: if debt matures before cash flow positive, clear plan for repayment or replacement debt
- Debt waterfall: if multiple debt instruments, clear repayment priority and subordination
- Sensitivity analysis: DSCR and covenant compliance if EBITDA is 20% lower

## Module 10: Return Analysis

### Definition
Analysis of investor returns across exit valuation scenarios, including IRR (internal rate of return), MOIC (multiple on invested capital), and return distribution waterfall. Critical for understanding investor value creation across scenarios and evaluating investment quality relative to risk.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Return analysis across scenarios: IRR and MOIC for base, upside, and downside cases
- Exit value scenarios: analysis of returns at range of exit valuations (conservative to bull case)
- IRR targets: IRR of 25%+ for early-stage VC, 15-20% for growth-stage, <15% for mature companies typical market expectations
- MOIC targets: 3x minimum for early-stage VC (10-year horizon), 2x for growth-stage
- Waterfall showing capital allocation: how proceeds are distributed (preferred holders, common holders, debt holders)
- Fully-diluted analysis: shows ownership and returns accounting for all equity (founders, employees, investors)
- Exit timing: assumption about exit year (Year 5, 7, 10) and how it affects returns
- Sensitivity to valuation multiple: shows how IRR changes if exit multiple is 8x revenue vs. 6x revenue
- Return comparison to benchmarks: how do projected returns compare to sector/stage expectations?

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- No return analysis; investor only sees revenue and EBITDA forecasts
- IRR and MOIC calculated but not explained or contextualized
- Exit value assumed to be revenue multiple with no justification (e.g., "exit at 5x revenue" with no comparable basis)
- Return waterfall missing: unclear how proceeds are distributed among preference holders
- Fully-diluted equity not reflected: appears founders own 50% but actually own 30% after option pool and previous investors
- IRR assumes immediate exit at horizon; doesn't account for time value of money
- Return analysis assumes no down round or dilution from future raises
- Returns appear overstated: exit value seems inconsistent with path to profitability or comparable M&A transactions

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the projected exit valuation in base, upside, and downside cases?
2. What is the projected IRR and MOIC in each scenario?
3. What is the basis for exit valuation multiple (e.g., 5x revenue)? How does it compare to recent M&A transactions?
4. What year is exit assumed? How does IRR change with different exit timings?
5. How are proceeds distributed in the waterfall? What do each investor class receive?
6. What is the investor's expected ownership at exit after dilution from future rounds?
7. What is the fully-diluted cap table at exit? What percentage does investor own after all dilution?
8. How sensitive are returns to exit multiple? (e.g., 4x vs. 6x revenue)
9. How do projected returns compare to sector/stage benchmarks?
10. What scenarios would result in investor loss or sub-benchmark returns?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Return analysis: IRR and MOIC for base, upside, and downside scenarios
- Exit value scenarios: range of exit valuations and corresponding returns
- Valuation multiple basis: comparable transactions or public company multiples justifying exit multiple assumption
- Waterfall analysis: distribution of proceeds to each preference class and common shareholders
- Fully-diluted cap table: at entry, at exit, and how ownership dilutes with future raises
- Sensitivity table: how IRR and MOIC change with different exit multiples or exit timings
- Return benchmarking: comparison of projected returns to VC/growth equity market expectations for stage/sector
- Down-round or dilution scenarios: what happens to returns if company raises next round at lower valuation?

## Assessment Notes for Scoring Agent

Evaluate financial performance and projections systematically across the ten modules using this framework:

**Gather Evidence**
1. Obtain all historical financial statements (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow) for past 2-3 years.
2. Request financial model in Excel/Google Sheets showing all revenue, COGS, OpEx, cash flow, and balance sheet assumptions.
3. Identify the assumptions document or embedded assumptions in the model; trace key assumptions to data sources.
4. Calculate actual financial metrics from historical statements: gross margin %, S&M as % of revenue, payback period, burn rate.
5. Request scenario analysis showing base, upside, downside cases.
6. Obtain cap table and trace through historical funding rounds.
7. Review any debt documents or agreements if debt is present.
8. Compare financial metrics and assumptions to public company comparables and industry benchmarks.

**Score Each Module**

For each of the ten modules, assign a risk rating:
- **Green (Credible)**: Financial projections are detailed, supported by data or benchmarks, and appear achievable relative to comparable companies at similar stage. Assumptions are explicit and conservative where material. Unit economics are strong or on path to strong.
- **Yellow (Reasonable with Caveats)**: Financial projections are detailed but include some ambitious assumptions or gaps in support. Unit economics are adequate but not compelling. Company will likely need to hit projections to return investor capital.
- **Red (High Doubt)**: Financial projections are vague, lack supporting data, or contain inconsistencies. Key assumptions are not justified. Unit economics are weak or negative with unclear path to improvement. Major gaps in financial detail (no COGS breakdown, no working capital analysis, etc.).

**Synthesize Domain Score**

Roll up individual module scores to derive overall Domain 7 score:
- Count Green modules. If 6+ of 10 modules are Green, financial story is credible and detailed.
- Count Yellow modules. 2-3 Yellow modules are normal, especially for early-stage; more than 4 Yellow indicates material gaps.
- Count Red modules. Even 1 Red module requires deep investigation. 2+ Red modules suggest financial story is not credible or founder has limited financial discipline.

**Red Flags**
- No unit economics analysis; company focused on revenue growth without profitability per customer
- CAC payback period exceeding 30 months; customer may not return investment
- LTV:CAC ratio below 2:1; unit economics not sustainable
- Gross margin below 40% for software/SaaS; suggests structural cost issues
- EBITDA break-even projected to occur only after Series C or later (requiring excessive capital)
- Key financial metrics (revenue drivers, headcount, OpEx) not detailed or unexplained
- Scenario analysis missing; only base case provided with no upside/downside
- Historical revenue vs. projections show consistent miss (e.g., prior year missed by 30%+); forecasts lack credibility
- Debt covenant violations projected in base case
- Returns to investor appear overstated relative to market comparables or risk profile

**Green Flags**
- Detailed financial model with granular revenue drivers (pipeline, cohort, unit-based)
- LTV:CAC ratio of 3:1 or better; unit economics strong and sustainable
- Gross margin on trajectory to 70%+ at scale (typical for software)
- S&M efficiency improving (magic number 0.75+); customer acquisition becoming more efficient
- EBITDA break-even projected within 3-4 years; reasonable capital requirements
- Historical revenue vs. projections showing consistent delivery (miss <20%); forecasts credible
- Scenario analysis with plausible upside/downside; management has thought through outcome range
- Working capital clearly modeled; cash flow and EBITDA reconcile
- Debt metrics (DSCR, coverage ratio) strong; no covenant risk
- IRR and MOIC in line with market expectations for stage and risk profile

## Cross-Domain Dependency Notes

**Domain 7 feeds into:**
- **Domain 8 (Risk Profile)**: Financial Risk is directly derived from cash burn rate, runway, and path to profitability. Execution Risk depends on ability to hit revenue and cost targets.
- **Domain 9 (Capital Structure)**: Use of proceeds, valuation, and return analysis are all outputs of financial modeling. Debt service analysis informs debt terms negotiation.
- **Domain 6 (Management)**: Strong financial controls and accurate projections are indicative of operational discipline and financial acumen of management team. Conversely, weak financials suggest gaps in CFO capability (Domain 6, Module 2).

**Domain 7 depends on:**
- **Domain 6 (Management)**: CFO/Finance capability directly impacts quality of financial controls, accuracy of projections, and credibility of assumptions. A weak finance function produces weak financials.
- **Domain 10 (Legal/Governance)**: Cap table accuracy and historical fundraising (Domain 10) inform equity and dilution modeling in Domain 7.
