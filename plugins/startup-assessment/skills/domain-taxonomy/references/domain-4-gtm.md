# Domain 4: Go-to-Market Assessment Reference

## Domain Overview

The Go-to-Market domain evaluates the startup's strategy for reaching customers, acquiring them, and driving adoption. Go-to-market encompasses sales strategy, channel selection, marketing approach, customer acquisition cost and mechanics, retention, expansion, partnerships, and market entry sequencing. A strong go-to-market strategy is grounded in customer psychology and behavior, realistic about competitive dynamics, and aligned with the company's resources and product positioning.

A compelling opportunity and excellent product can be undermined by poor go-to-market execution. Conversely, a well-executed go-to-market strategy can overcome product limitations or a less-compelling opportunity. Your assessment here evaluates whether the team understands how to reach their customers efficiently, whether the acquisition costs are sustainable, and whether the company has a realistic path to scaling customer acquisition.

---

## Module 1: Sales Strategy

### Definition
The approach the startup will use to close customers—whether direct sales, inside sales, self-serve, account-based, or combinations thereof. Sales strategy must align with customer buying behavior, deal size, and customer needs (e.g., large deals with significant implementation needs require high-touch account-based sales; small deals can be self-serve).

### What Good Looks Like

- **Sales model clearly defined**: The submission specifies the sales approach. Example: "We use direct enterprise sales for deals >$50K ACV; inside sales for mid-market $10K–$50K ACV; self-serve for SMB <$10K ACV."
- **Sales model aligned with customer buying process**: The sales approach matches how customers prefer to buy. Example: "Enterprise customers require 4–6 month sales cycles with IT/procurement evaluation; we employ account-based sales with dedicated AE support. SMB customers prefer self-serve evaluation and rapid deployment; we optimize for frictionless onboarding."
- **Sales process articulated**: The submission describes the sales process—stages, typical deal progression, timeline. Example: "Discovery (2 weeks) → Evaluation (4 weeks) → Negotiation (2 weeks) → Deployment (2 weeks). Average deal cycle is 10 weeks; 30% of evaluations close."
- **Sales roles and compensation**: The submission specifies sales team structure and compensation model. Example: "Account executives (AE) focus on new customer acquisition; compensation is $75K salary + 20% variable commission on new ACV. Customer success managers (CSM) focus on retention and expansion; compensation is $70K salary + 5% variable on net revenue retention."
- **Sales efficiency metrics**: The submission articulates expected sales efficiency (quota attainment, sales cycle length, deal size, win rate). Example: "Year 1 target: 2 AEs close 20 enterprise deals at $50K ACV. Year 2 target: 5 AEs close 75 deals. This implies $2.5M quota per AE with 60% attainment in Year 1, 80% in Year 2."
- **Sales channel clarity**: The submission specifies whether sales are field sales, inside sales, or self-serve. Example: "Direct enterprise sales (AE + SDR model) for enterprise; inside sales for mid-market; self-serve signup + free trial for SMB."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Sales model undefined**: "We'll figure out how to sell" with no articulated sales strategy.
- **Sales model misaligned with customer buying process**: Using self-serve for customers who need 6-month enterprise sales cycles; or high-touch sales for customers who prefer self-serve.
- **Unrealistic sales team productivity**: Assumes AEs close $5M quota each, or expects 1 AE to close 100 deals per year.
- **No sales process defined**: Does not articulate typical deal progression or sales cycle length.
- **Sales roles not specified**: Does not explain who will do the selling (founders, dedicated AEs, VARs, etc.).
- **No sales compensation plan**: Does not articulate how sales team will be paid or motivated.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the primary sales model (direct, inside, self-serve, hybrid)? Why this model for these customers?
- What is the typical sales cycle length? What are the deal stages?
- What is the expected sales team size at different revenue milestones?
- What is the sales quota per AE? Is this realistic given customer type?
- How will sales team be compensated? Base vs. variable mix?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Sales model definition** (direct, inside, self-serve, account-based, etc.)
- **Sales process diagram or description** showing deal stages and typical cycle length
- **Sales team structure** (AEs, SDRs, CSMs) and headcount plan
- **Sales quota and attainment assumptions** (quota per AE, win rate, deal size)
- **Sales compensation plan** (base salary, commission structure, variable incentives)
- **Sales efficiency metrics** (sales cycle length, average deal size, quota attainment %)

## Module 2: Channel Strategy

### Definition
The specific channels through which the startup will acquire and serve customers—whether direct sales, resellers/partners, marketplaces, distribution partners, or combinations. Channel strategy must align with customer buying preferences and the startup's ability to serve customers through that channel efficiently.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Primary channels identified**: The submission identifies the 2–3 primary channels through which customers will be acquired. Example: "Primary channels: (1) Direct sales for enterprise and mid-market; (2) Self-serve digital/freemium for SMB; (3) Partner/integration channel (for customers already using [integrated system])."
- **Channel-specific unit economics**: The submission models unit economics by channel. Example: "Direct sales CAC: $20K, ACV $50K, LTV:CAC = 5:1. Self-serve CAC: $3K, ACV $10K, LTV:CAC = 7:1. Partner CAC: $5K, ACV $15K, LTV:CAC = 6:1."
- **Channel differentiation**: The submission articulates why each channel is attractive and which customer segments are served through each. Example: "Direct sales: high-touch, long sales cycle, complex implementation; Self-serve: low friction, fast adoption, high conversion; Partners: embedded in customer's existing workflow, low CAC, lower support burden."
- **Partner/integration strategy articulated**: If channels include partners, the submission specifies partner types (resellers, integrators, software integrations) and acquisition plan. Example: "We target 20 system integrators as partners; partner CAC is $10K (initial training and enablement); partners generate $500K revenue year 1, $2M by year 3."
- **Channel roadmap**: The submission specifies which channels launch when. Example: "Month 1: Direct sales launch. Month 6: Self-serve digital launch. Month 12: Partner program launch."
- **Channel efficiency**: The submission shows how CAC and sales cycle vary by channel. Example: "Direct sales: 16-week cycle, $20K CAC. Self-serve: 2-week cycle, $3K CAC. Partners: 4-week cycle, $5K CAC."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **No channel strategy**: "We'll use multiple channels" with no specificity.
- **Unrealistic channel assumptions**: Assumes resellers will generate meaningful revenue without investment; partners do not work unless there is compelling incentive alignment.
- **No channel economics**: Does not model unit economics by channel or compare channel efficiency.
- **Over-reliance on single channel**: Entire business depends on one channel; risk if that channel underperforms.
- **No partner/integration plan**: If claiming partnership is a channel, no concrete plan for acquiring partners.
- **Channel conflict ignored**: Does not address how to manage channel conflict if selling direct and through partners in same market.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What are the 2–3 primary channels the company will use to acquire customers?
- What is the unit economics (CAC, ACV, LTV, payback) for each channel?
- Which customer segments will be served through each channel?
- For partner channels, what is the partner acquisition and enablement plan?
- How does the company manage channel conflict (e.g., selling directly and through partners)?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Channel mix** (% revenue from direct, self-serve, partners) over time
- **Unit economics by channel** (CAC, ACV, sales cycle, payback, LTV:CAC)
- **Customer segment allocation** to channels (which segments buy from which channels)
- **Partner strategy** (partner types, partner CAC, partner enablement, revenue sharing)
- **Channel roadmap** showing when each channel launches and scales
- **Channel conflict management** plan

## Module 3: Marketing Strategy

### Definition
The approach the startup will use to build brand awareness, generate demand, and drive qualified pipeline to the sales team. Marketing strategy includes: positioning and messaging, marketing channels (digital, content, events, PR, etc.), demand generation tactics, and budget allocation. A strong marketing strategy is grounded in customer insights and delivers measurable ROI.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Positioning and messaging articulated**: The submission clearly defines how the product will be positioned and the key messages. Example: "Positioning: We're the most user-friendly invoice automation platform for mid-market companies. Key messages: (1) Reduces manual AP work by 90%; (2) Painless implementation (days, not months); (3) Works with your existing ERP."
- **Target audience clarity**: The submission specifies the persona and audience for marketing (not just the buyer, but the influencers and decision-makers). Example: "Primary audience: Accounts Payable Manager (pain owner, influencer); Secondary audience: CFO/Procurement Director (budget owner, final decision maker)."
- **Marketing channels specified**: The submission identifies the marketing channels and budget allocation. Example: "Digital marketing (40%): paid search, LinkedIn ads, webinars; Content marketing (30%): blog, case studies, webinars; Events (20%): tradeshows, roundtables; PR/partnerships (10%)."
- **Demand generation tactics**: The submission articulates specific demand generation tactics and expected ROI. Example: "Paid search: $10K monthly spend, 2% conversion to demo, average deal $40K. Expected LTV:CAC = 5:1. LinkedIn ads: $5K monthly spend, 0.5% conversion, expected LTV:CAC = 4:1."
- **Content strategy**: For content-driven channels, the submission articulates content strategy. Example: "We publish 2 blog posts/week (AP automation best practices, case studies), 1 webinar/month, white papers quarterly. Content is optimized for search ('invoice automation best practices', 'how to implement accounts payable automation')."
- **Customer research informing positioning**: The submission shows that positioning and messaging are grounded in customer research. Example: "Surveys of 50 AP managers show 68% cite ease of use as top-3 evaluation criteria; we position on user-friendliness based on customer feedback."
- **Marketing ROI modeled**: The submission shows expected ROI by channel. Example: "Paid search: $10K spend, 1M impressions, 100K clicks, 2K form submissions, 100 demos, 10 deals = $400K revenue. CAC: $1K. LTV:CAC = 4:1 (profitable)."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Generic positioning**: "We're a best-in-class SaaS solution for [market]" with no differentiation.
- **No marketing channels specified**: "We'll do marketing" with no specificity on channels or budget allocation.
- **No marketing budget**: Marketing strategy is articulated with no budget attached; signals unrealistic expectations.
- **No customer research informing positioning**: Positioning feels disconnected from customer needs.
- **Unrealistic conversion assumptions**: Assumes 10% of website visitors will convert to leads; B2B SaaS benchmarks are 1–3%.
- **No measurement or ROI tracking**: Does not articulate how marketing ROI will be measured or tracked.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the product positioning? What are the key messages? Is this grounded in customer research?
- Who are the primary and secondary audiences/personas for marketing?
- What marketing channels will be used? What is the budget allocation?
- What are the expected conversion rates and ROI by channel?
- How will marketing ROI be measured and optimized?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Positioning statement** with key messages grounded in customer insights
- **Target personas** (title, company size, pain points, decision-making authority)
- **Marketing mix** (channels, budget allocation, tactics)
- **Content strategy** (blog, case studies, webinars, white papers, events)
- **Demand generation tactics** with expected conversion rates and ROI by channel
- **Brand/awareness strategy** (PR, partnerships, thought leadership)
- **Marketing metrics/KPIs** (lead volume, conversion rate, CAC by channel, pipeline contribution)

## Module 4: Customer Acquisition Economics

### Definition
Detailed economics of customer acquisition—the cost, timeline, and efficiency of acquiring customers through different channels and tactics. Customer acquisition economics are fundamental to business model viability; if CAC is too high relative to LTV, the business cannot scale profitably.

### What Good Looks Like

- **CAC calculated by channel and segment**: The submission calculates CAC for each major channel/segment. Example: "Direct sales CAC: $20K (Year 1). Self-serve CAC: $3K. Partner CAC: $5K."
- **CAC calculation methodology transparent**: The submission specifies what is included in CAC (salaries, commissions, marketing spend, overhead allocation). Example: "Direct sales CAC includes: fully-loaded AE salary and commission ($300K / 15 closes = $20K); SDR salary allocation ($150K / 150 leads generated = $1K); marketing spend allocation. Total: $22K CAC."
- **CAC payback period calculated**: The submission estimates payback period (months to recover CAC through customer contributions). Example: "Mid-market CAC: $20K; ACV: $40K; gross margin: 70%; monthly contribution: $2,333. Payback: $20K / $2,333 = 8.6 months."
- **Sales cycle articulated**: The submission specifies the sales cycle timeline. Example: "Discovery: 2 weeks. Evaluation: 4 weeks. Negotiation: 2 weeks. Deployment: 2 weeks. Total: 10 weeks."
- **Win rate or close rate specified**: The submission estimates what percentage of leads/opportunities close. Example: "Demo-to-customer: 30%; lead-to-demo: 25%; inquiry-to-lead: 50%. Overall lead-to-customer: 3.75%."
- **CAC declining over time**: The submission models CAC declining as the company improves efficiency. Example: "Year 1 CAC: $20K; Year 2: $15K (improved sales efficiency); Year 3: $12K (brand maturity and referral growth)."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **CAC not calculated**: The submission does not estimate customer acquisition cost.
- **CAC understated**: CAC calculation omits significant costs (marketing, overhead allocation, implementation support).
- **No payback period calculated**: Does not show how long it takes for customer to pay back acquisition cost.
- **Win rate not articulated**: Does not specify what percentage of leads or opportunities close.
- **CAC static over time**: Assumes CAC will remain constant, not declining through operational improvements.
- **No CAC by segment**: Treats all customers as same CAC; does not segment by customer type or channel.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the CAC for each major customer segment/channel?
- How is CAC calculated? What costs are included (fully-loaded)?
- What is the payback period? (<12 months is excellent; >18 months is concerning)
- What is the win rate/close rate by sales stage (inquiry to lead, lead to demo, demo to customer)?
- How long is the sales cycle? How does this compare to competitors?
- How will CAC improve over time?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **CAC by segment/channel** with transparent calculation methodology
- **Sales funnel metrics** (inquiry volume, conversion rates by stage)
- **Sales cycle timeline** (months from first contact to deal close)
- **Win/close rates** at each stage (lead-to-demo %, demo-to-customer %)
- **Payback period** (months to recover CAC)
- **CAC improvement roadmap** showing how CAC will decline
- **CAC benchmarking** vs. industry comparables

## Module 5: Retention and Expansion Mechanisms

### Definition
Strategy for keeping customers and expanding revenue within existing customers. Retention is measured by churn rate and retention rate; expansion is measured by Net Revenue Retention (NRR). Both are critical to sustainable SaaS business models.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Churn target articulated**: The submission specifies target customer churn rate. Example: "Target annual churn: 10% (Year 1), declining to 5% by Year 3."
- **Churn drivers identified**: The submission identifies what drives churn and how it will be mitigated. Example: "Churn drivers: (1) Lack of engagement (mitigate with proactive CSM outreach); (2) Feature gaps (mitigate with rapid product iterations); (3) Competitive switching (mitigate with high switching costs and lock-in)."
- **Customer success strategy**: The submission articulates how the company will ensure customer success and retention. Example: "Dedicated CSM for enterprise (1 CSM: 10 customers); QBR (quarterly business reviews) to track ROI and identify expansion opportunities; NPS monitoring monthly; escalation process for at-risk customers."
- **Expansion revenue strategy**: The submission articulates how revenue will expand within customers. Example: "Expansion mechanisms: (1) Seat expansion as customer team grows (natural); (2) Module upsell (expense management module to invoice automation customers); (3) Usage-based expansion (customers who exceed usage thresholds at higher pricing tier)."
- **Net Revenue Retention target**: The submission specifies NRR target. Example: "Target NRR: 110% by Year 2, indicating 5% churn and 15% expansion revenue."
- **Expansion revenue as % of total**: The submission specifies what percentage of new revenue comes from expansion vs. acquisition. Example: "Year 1: 100% of revenue from new customers (no expansion yet). Year 2: 40% from expansion, 60% from acquisition. Year 3: 50% from expansion, 50% from acquisition."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Churn not addressed**: The submission does not articulate churn or retention strategy.
- **Unrealistic churn targets**: Claims 1% annual churn with no justification; most SaaS has 5–15% annual churn.
- **No expansion strategy**: Assumes all revenue comes from new customer acquisition; no expansion revenue.
- **No customer success plan**: No articulation of how customers will be helped to succeed and remain engaged.
- **Churn drivers not identified**: Does not explain why customers churn or how churn will be minimized.
- **NRR not modeled**: Does not calculate or target Net Revenue Retention.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the target annual churn rate? How does this compare to industry benchmarks (SaaS: 5–15%)?
- What drives churn? How will the company minimize it?
- What is the customer success strategy (CSM coverage, QBRs, support, onboarding)?
- How will expansion revenue be generated (seat expansion, module upsell, usage-based, professional services)?
- What is the target NRR? What assumptions (churn + expansion) underpin this target?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Churn rate target** (annual customer churn %) with justification
- **Churn driver analysis** identifying why customers churn and mitigation plans
- **Customer success roadmap** (CSM coverage, QBRs, health monitoring, escalation process)
- **Expansion revenue strategy** (mechanisms for upsell/cross-sell)
- **Net Revenue Retention target** and component assumptions (churn, expansion %)
- **Customer lifecycle strategy** (onboarding, adoption, engagement, expansion, renewal)

## Module 6: Partnership Strategy

### Definition
Plan for leveraging partnerships to accelerate customer acquisition, expand market reach, or improve customer success. Partnerships may include technology integrations, go-to-market partnerships, reseller relationships, or strategic alliances. Strong partnership strategies are grounded in mutual value (not just startup benefit) and have concrete execution plans.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Partnership types identified**: The submission identifies types of partnerships pursued. Example: "(1) Technology integrations (with [ERP vendor], [financial system]) to be embedded in customer workflows; (2) Reseller partnerships (with systems integrators) to access enterprise customers; (3) Marketing partnerships (with industry associations) for brand credibility."
- **Partner selection criteria articulated**: The submission specifies which potential partners to pursue. Example: "Target systems integrators: 50–500 employees, ERP implementation focus, customer base of 100+ mid-market manufacturers (our TAM). We've identified 15 qualified partners."
- **Partner value proposition clear**: The submission articulates why partners should engage. Example: "For system integrators, we offer: recurring revenue (20% commission on customer ACV), differentiation from competitors, reduced implementation time (pre-built integrations), and customer lock-in."
- **Partnership acquisition plan**: The submission specifies how partners will be acquired. Example: "Partner acquisition: Direct outreach to partnership leaders, ROI demonstration, revenue sharing negotiation, partner enablement (training, sales collateral, technical documentation)."
- **Partner economics modeled**: The submission models expected revenue from partnerships. Example: "Year 1: 5 partners, $100K revenue from partners. Year 2: 15 partners, $500K revenue. Year 3: 30 partners, $1.5M revenue (25% of total revenue)."
- **Integration/enablement requirements**: The submission specifies what is required to enable partners. Example: "Integration: 2 weeks engineering. Enablement: 2-day partner training, sales collateral, technical documentation. Ongoing: quarterly training and support."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Vague partnership strategy**: "We'll pursue strategic partnerships" with no specificity on partner types or selection.
- **No partner economics**: Does not model expected revenue from partnerships.
- **Unrealistic partner expectations**: Assumes partners will generate significant revenue without investment in partner enablement.
- **No partner value proposition**: Does not articulate why partners should engage or what value the partnership provides to partners.
- **Partner dependencies understated**: Does not acknowledge risks if key partners do not materialize.
- **No integration plan**: For technology partnerships, does not specify integration requirements or timeline.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What types of partnerships will the company pursue (technology integrations, resellers, marketing partners)?
- How many partners will be acquired? What is the revenue target from partnerships?
- Why will partners want to engage with the startup? What is the partner value proposition?
- How long does it take to onboard a partner and begin generating revenue?
- What percentage of revenue is expected from partnerships vs. direct sales?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Partnership strategy** (technology, reseller, marketing, strategic alliances)
- **Partner types and selection criteria** (company size, customer base, geographic focus)
- **Partner value proposition** (revenue opportunity, differentiation, time/cost savings)
- **Partner acquisition and enablement plan** (outreach, training, collateral, ongoing support)
- **Partner economics** (partner CAC, revenue per partner, lifetime value)
- **Revenue model** (revenue sharing %, commission structure, deal registration)

## Module 7: Market Entry Sequencing

### Definition
The strategy for prioritizing which markets, geographies, customer segments, or verticals to enter and in what order. Strong market entry sequencing focuses resources on segments where the company has competitive advantage and can achieve success quickly, building momentum for expansion.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Sequencing strategy articulated**: The submission specifies the sequence for market entry. Example: "Phase 1: Discrete manufacturing (automotive, aerospace) in North America (high pain, clear ROI, concentration). Phase 2: Expand to other manufacturing verticals (pharma, food). Phase 3: Expand internationally to EMEA and APAC."
- **Selection rationale**: The submission explains why this sequencing is optimal. Example: "We start with discrete manufacturing because: (1) High invoice volume drives strong ROI; (2) Concentrated customer base (10K facilities in North America); (3) Competitive advantage (we have manufacturing-specific expertise); (4) Customers actively evaluating solutions."
- **Geographic strategy**: The submission specifies geographic market entry. Example: "Year 1: US only (80% of TAM, English-speaking, established cloud infrastructure). Year 2: Canada and UK (English-speaking, similar regulations). Year 3: EMEA expansion (multi-language support required)."
- **Timeline for expansion**: The submission specifies when each market will be entered. Example: "Phase 1: Months 1–12 (50 customers in discrete manufacturing). Phase 2: Months 13–24 (expand to 3 manufacturing verticals). Phase 3: Months 25–36 (expand to 2 EMEA countries)."
- **Resource allocation**: The submission shows how resources will be allocated to different market expansions. Example: "Year 1: All resources on discrete manufacturing. Year 2: Allocate 30% of sales resources to new verticals. Year 3: Allocate 20% of resources to EMEA, 50% to North America expansion, 30% to new verticals."
- **Success metrics by market**: The submission articulates success metrics for market entry. Example: "Phase 1 success: 50 customers, $2M ARR, 80% NPS. If we hit these targets in 12 months, we expand to Phase 2."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **No market entry sequencing**: "We'll target all markets simultaneously" with no prioritization.
- **Simultaneous multi-market entry**: Attempts to enter 5+ markets in Year 1; dilutes focus and increases execution risk.
- **Sequencing not based on competitive advantage**: Enters markets where the company has no advantage or where competitors are entrenched.
- **No timeline for expansion**: Unclear when each market will be entered.
- **No success metrics**: Does not articulate what success looks like before expanding to next market.
- **Geographic expansion too aggressive**: Plans to enter China, Japan, and Brazil in Year 1 without multilingual team or international expertise.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the market entry sequencing? Which segments, verticals, or geographies first?
- Why is this sequencing optimal? What competitive or market factors support this order?
- What is the timeline for entering each market?
- What success metrics trigger expansion to the next market?
- What resources are required for each market entry?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Market entry sequence** (which segments/geographies in which order)
- **Sequencing rationale** (competitive advantage, TAM size, adoption readiness, resource requirements)
- **Timeline** for entering each market/segment
- **Success criteria** that trigger expansion to next market
- **Resource plan** showing staffing and investment required for each phase
- **Geographic strategy** (if applicable) with language and regulatory requirements

## Module 8: Pipeline and Backlog

### Definition
Evidence of near-term customer demand in the form of pipeline (qualified opportunities likely to close) and backlog (customers committed to buying but not yet fully onboarded). Pipeline and backlog are leading indicators of future revenue and validate that the go-to-market strategy is generating real customer interest.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Pipeline articulated**: The submission reports qualified sales pipeline. Example: "Current sales pipeline: $2M (prospects in evaluation or negotiation stage). Expected close rate: 30%. Expected revenue from pipeline: $600K over next 90 days."
- **Pipeline quality assessed**: The submission distinguishes between strong and weak pipeline. Example: "Strong pipeline: $1M (customers in negotiation or late-stage evaluation, 40% probability of close). Weak pipeline: $1M (early-stage prospects, 15% probability of close). Expected revenue: $400K + $150K = $550K."
- **Backlog reported**: The submission reports signed agreements not yet started or deployed. Example: "Current backlog: $500K (customers have signed agreements, awaiting implementation). Expected deployment in next 30–60 days."
- **Pipeline visibility**: The submission shows how much revenue is visible (in pipeline + backlog) vs. requires new outreach. Example: "Committed revenue (LOIs + backlog): $500K. Pipeline: $2M (30% close probability = $600K expected). Total visible: $1.1M. For $3M annual target, need to generate additional $1.9M in pipeline."
- **Pipeline growth tracked**: The submission shows how pipeline is growing month-over-month. Example: "Pipeline has grown 20% MoM for last 3 months, indicating strong demand generation."
- **Pipeline-to-revenue leading indicator**: The submission correlates pipeline to actual revenue. Example: "Historical conversion from 90-day pipeline to revenue: 35%. With current $2M pipeline, we expect $700K in next 90 days; prior quarter, $2M pipeline generated $650K, validating accuracy."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **No pipeline reported**: The submission does not report current sales pipeline.
- **Pipeline vague**: "We have strong pipeline" with no quantification.
- **Pipeline quality not assessed**: Pipeline is reported as one number with no differentiation of deal stage or probability.
- **No backlog**: Even at growth stage, there is no backlog of signed, not-yet-implemented customers, suggesting weak demand.
- **Pipeline not tracked**: No visibility into pipeline growth trends.
- **Unrealistic pipeline valuation**: Pipeline is reported as 10x annual revenue targets; either deal sizes are overstated or close rates are unrealistic.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the current sales pipeline? What is the expected value of pipeline at different close probabilities?
- What is the breakdown of pipeline by deal stage (discovery, evaluation, negotiation, final approval)?
- What percentage of pipeline is expected to close? (30–40% is typical for B2B SaaS)
- How much revenue is in backlog (signed but not yet deployed)?
- Is pipeline growing month-over-month? What is the growth rate?
- How accurate is pipeline-to-revenue conversion? (Do pipeline estimates match actual closes?)

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Current sales pipeline** ($XX million) with distribution by deal stage
- **Pipeline by close probability** (strong: >50% probability, weak: <20% probability)
- **Current backlog** (signed but not yet deployed customers and ACV)
- **Pipeline visibility** (pipeline as multiple of monthly/quarterly revenue targets)
- **Pipeline growth trend** (MoM growth rate)
- **Pipeline conversion accuracy** (historical close rates by deal stage)
- **Attribution** (which marketing/sales activities generated pipeline)

## Cross-Domain Dependencies

The Go-to-Market domain feeds into and is informed by:

- **Domain 1 (Market and Opportunity)**: Customer segmentation and competitive positioning drive go-to-market strategy. Demand validation proves customers are ready to buy.
- **Domain 2 (Solution and Product)**: Product capabilities and positioning drive messaging and differentiation. Sales cycle may depend on product maturity.
- **Domain 3 (Business Model)**: Go-to-market strategy drives CAC, which is fundamental to unit economics. Pricing must support customer acquisition costs.
- **Domain 5 (Traction)**: Early customer acquisitions validate go-to-market strategy. Customer acquisition costs, churn, and NRR prove the strategy is working.

Conversely, these domains inform GTM assessment:

- **Traction**: Actual customer acquisition costs and sales cycle lengths must align with projections.
- **Product**: Product quality and ease of use affect sales cycle length and customer success.
- **Business Model**: Unit economics and profitability depend on achieving projected CAC and LTV.

---

## Assessment Notes for the Scoring Agent

When assessing the Go-to-Market domain:

1. **Validate sales model alignment**: Does the sales model match the customer buying process? If selling long-cycle enterprise deals with self-serve, that's a mismatch.

2. **Test CAC realism**: Is CAC achievable given industry benchmarks and the company's positioning? Typical B2B SaaS CAC is 0.5–2x ACV; if CAC is 5x ACV, unit economics may be broken.

3. **Assess sales efficiency**: Are quota assumptions realistic? $5M quota per AE is achievable for enterprise SaaS, not for mid-market SaaS. Check industry benchmarks.

4. **Evaluate channel complementarity**: Do multiple channels complement each other (self-serve funnels into direct sales; partners generate leads for company), or do they compete?

5. **Challenge partnership assumptions**: Partnerships sound great but rarely deliver on early projections. Is the startup over-dependent on partnerships that may not materialize?

6. **Stress-test pipeline**: If all growth depends on current pipeline, what happens if pipeline closes at 50% of projected rate? Is there another source of demand?

7. **Assess market entry realism**: Is the market entry sequence ambitious but achievable, or impossible? Entering 5 verticals in Year 1 is likely impossible.

8. **Cross-reference with traction**: Do early customer acquisition costs align with projections? Is the company on track to hit pipeline/revenue targets?

## Summary: Good Go-to-Market Assessment

A strong Go-to-Market assessment demonstrates:

- **Clear sales strategy** aligned with customer buying process (direct, inside, self-serve, or hybrid)
- **Efficient customer acquisition** with realistic CAC, payback period, and LTV:CAC >3:1
- **Diversified channels** (direct, self-serve, partners) reducing dependency on any single channel
- **Strong marketing strategy** grounded in customer insights with clear positioning and demand generation
- **Retention and expansion** mechanisms driving NRR >100% and customer stickiness
- **Realistic market entry** sequencing focusing on segments where company has advantage
- **Current pipeline and backlog** validating customer demand and near-term revenue visibility

Go-to-market excellence converts a good opportunity and product into actual revenue and customer relationships. Pair this assessment with product and market opportunity for a complete picture of startup viability.
