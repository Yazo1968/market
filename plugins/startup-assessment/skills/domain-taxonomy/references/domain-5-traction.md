# Domain 5: Traction Assessment Reference

## Domain Overview

The Traction domain evaluates evidence that the startup's business model, product, and go-to-market strategy are working in practice. Traction encompasses customer acquisition, revenue growth, product adoption metrics, customer retention, and major commercial milestones. Traction is the validation of all prior hypotheses—proof that customers actually want the product, will pay for it, and will stay. A strong traction assessment demonstrates clear market validation and momentum.

Traction is the most objective of all domains. Assertions about market opportunity, product excellence, or business model viability can be debated; actual customer adoption and revenue are facts. Your assessment here evaluates whether the startup has credible evidence that its business model works, momentum is sustainable, and growth trajectory is realistic.

---

## Module 1: Revenue History

### Definition
Historical revenue data demonstrating the startup's ability to generate revenue from real customers. Revenue history includes: annual recurring revenue (ARR), monthly recurring revenue (MRR), total contract value (TCV), customer acquisition timeline, and revenue growth rate. For early-stage startups with minimal revenue, milestone-based evidence (pilots, LOIs, commitments) may substitute for revenue history.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Revenue trajectory reported**: The submission reports historical revenue with clear timeline. Example: "Q1 2024: $0 (no customers, beta phase). Q2 2024: $50K ARR (10 customers). Q3 2024: $150K ARR (25 customers). Q4 2024: $300K ARR (40 customers). Growth rate: 100% QoQ."
- **Revenue components transparent**: The submission breaks down revenue by source (subscription, services, other). Example: "Q4 2024 revenue: $300K ARR from SaaS subscriptions; $50K from implementation services; $250K recurring, $100K variable."
- **Growth rates calculated**: The submission specifies MoM, QoQ, and YoY growth rates. Example: "MoM growth rate (Oct–Dec): 30%; this represents acceleration from 20% MoM earlier in year, suggesting improving product-market fit."
- **Customer cohort metrics**: For startups with multiple cohorts, the submission shows cohort-level revenue and retention. Example: "Q1 2024 cohort: 10 customers, $50K ARR, 80% retention after 6 months. Q2 2024 cohort: 15 customers, $100K ARR, 85% retention at 3-month mark."
- **Revenue consistency**: Revenue growth is consistent and predictable, not lumpy. Example: "Revenue has grown 20% MoM for 6 consecutive months with <5% volatility; growth is consistent and not dependent on single large deals."
- **Milestone-based evidence (if revenue minimal)**: For startups with <$100K ARR, the submission provides evidence of near-term revenue commitment. Example: "Pilot revenue: $0 (pilots are paid after completion). LOI revenue: $500K (5 LOIs from enterprise customers for 2025). Backlog: $0. Near-term visibility: Strong pipeline of $2M over next 90 days."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **No revenue reported**: The submission does not report any revenue or revenue timeline.
- **Revenue not independently verified**: Revenue claims are not backed by any third-party verification; could be overstated.
- **Lumpy or inconsistent revenue growth**: Revenue is driven by one or two large deals; growth is unpredictable.
- **Service revenue over-represented**: Majority of revenue is from one-time services or implementation; recurring revenue is minimal.
- **Revenue trend unexplained**: Revenue grew 50% last quarter then flat this quarter; no explanation for change.
- **Milestone evidence unclear**: "We have strong early customer interest" without specific LOIs, pilots, or commitments.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the current ARR/MRR? What was it 3, 6, and 12 months ago?
- What is the MoM and YoY revenue growth rate? Is growth accelerating, steady, or decelerating?
- What percentage of revenue is recurring vs. one-time?
- How many customers are generating revenue? What is ACV?
- For early-stage startups, what LOIs or pilot commitments validate near-term revenue?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Revenue history** (ARR, MRR, TCV) with monthly or quarterly breakdown
- **Revenue growth rate** (MoM, QoQ, YoY) showing growth trajectory
- **Revenue composition** (% recurring, % services, % one-time)
- **Customer count** over time (cohort analysis if multiple cohorts)
- **Average Contract Value (ACV)** and how ACV has evolved
- **Customer acquisition rate** (new customers per month)
- **For early-stage**, LOIs, pilots, or backlog providing revenue visibility
- **Revenue recognition** methodology (SaaS typically uses monthly for recurring revenue)

## Module 2: Customer Metrics

### Definition
Metrics describing the startup's customer base—how many customers, customer composition, customer quality, and customer acquisition rate. Customer metrics are leading indicators of business health; a growing, stable customer base indicates sustainable momentum.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Total customer count reported**: The submission reports number of customers at specific points in time. Example: "Q1 2024: 10 customers. Q2 2024: 25 customers. Q3 2024: 40 customers. Q4 2024: 60 customers."
- **Customer growth rate**: The submission calculates MoM or QoQ customer growth rate. Example: "Customer growth: Q1–Q2: 150%; Q2–Q3: 60%; Q3–Q4: 50%. Growth rate is decelerating but remains strong."
- **Customer composition**: The submission breaks down customers by segment/type. Example: "60 customers: 5 enterprise (>$1M revenue), 15 mid-market ($100M–$1M), 40 SMB (<$100M). Blended ACV: $5K. Enterprise ACV: $50K, Mid-market ACV: $20K, SMB ACV: $2K."
- **Customer quality assessment**: The submission articulates customer quality (are customers ideal customers, or are they low-quality). Example: "80% of customers match our ICP (mid-market manufacturers); 20% are outside ICP. ICP customers have 85% 6-month retention; non-ICP have 60%."
- **Customer acquisition rate**: The submission calculates new customers acquired per month. Example: "October: 10 new customers. November: 15 new customers. December: 20 new customers. CAR (customer acquisition rate) is accelerating."
- **Customer concentration**: The submission discloses concentration (% of revenue/customers from top customers). Example: "Top 10 customers: 40% of revenue. Top 3 customers: 15% of revenue. No single customer is >8% of revenue. Appropriately diversified."
- **Payable customer rate**: For self-serve or freemium models, the submission reports rate at which free users convert to paying customers. Example: "Free users: 5,000. Paying customers: 60. Free-to-paid conversion rate: 1.2%. Estimated payoff timeline: 3 months to break even on acquisition cost."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Customer count not reported**: The submission does not report how many customers exist.
- **Customer concentration concealed**: Does not disclose what percentage of revenue comes from top customers; implies concentration issue.
- **Low-quality customers**: Customer base is primarily trial/freemium users with minimal intent to pay.
- **Declining customer growth**: Customer acquisition is slowing without explanation.
- **High customer churn**: Many customers are acquired but then lost; growth masks underlying problems.
- **Customer composition opaque**: Does not differentiate customer quality or segment composition.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- How many customers exist? What is the customer count growth rate (MoM, QoQ)?
- What percentage of customers match the ICP (ideal customer profile)? How does retention differ by segment?
- What is the new customer acquisition rate? Is this accelerating or decelerating?
- What is the customer concentration? Are there any large-customer dependencies?
- For freemium/trial models, what is the conversion rate from free to paid?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Customer count** at specific points (monthly or quarterly) with growth rate
- **Customer composition** by segment, size, or vertical
- **Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) fit** and retention by customer type
- **Customer acquisition rate** (new customers per month, accelerating/decelerating)
- **Customer concentration** (% revenue from top 10, top 1)
- **Customer quality indicators** (onboarding success rate, TTV—time-to-value, product adoption)
- **For freemium**, free-to-paid conversion rate and conversion funnel

## Module 3: Product Engagement

### Definition
Metrics describing how actively customers are using the product—daily/monthly active users (DAU/MAU), feature adoption, usage frequency, session length, and other behavioral indicators of engagement. High engagement indicates that customers value the product and are deriving real benefit; low engagement suggests product-market fit issues.

### What Good Looks Like

- **DAU/MAU or equivalent reported**: The submission reports daily or monthly active users. Example: "MAU: 500 (monthly active users). DAU: 150 (daily active users). DAU/MAU ratio: 30%, indicating regular engagement."
- **Usage frequency**: The submission articulates how often customers use the product. Example: "Median customer logs in 2x per week; median session length is 15 minutes. Power users log in daily; average session length 30 minutes."
- **Feature adoption**: The submission shows adoption rate of key features. Example: "Core invoice processing feature: 95% of customers use. Bulk import feature: 60% adoption. Advanced matching rules: 25% adoption. Engagement tier: high for core, medium for secondary features."
- **Engagement trend**: The submission shows that engagement is increasing or stable. Example: "Monthly active user rate has increased from 60% to 75% of customers over 6 months, indicating improving product adoption and stickiness."
- **Time-to-value (TTV)**: The submission estimates how long it takes customers to gain value from the product. Example: "Average TTV: 2 weeks. 80% of customers process their first batch of invoices within 2 weeks of deployment. This enables rapid onboarding and reduces implementation risk."
- **Product health metrics**: The submission includes health metrics indicating product quality. Example: "System uptime: 99.95%. Mean time to resolution (MTTR) for defects: 4 hours. Customer-reported critical bugs: 1 per quarter (industry average: 3–5 per quarter)."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Engagement not measured**: The submission does not report DAU, MAU, usage frequency, or other engagement metrics.
- **Low engagement rate**: MAU is <50% of customers; implies many customers are not actively using the product.
- **Declining engagement**: Engagement has decreased over time; indicates declining product-market fit.
- **Feature adoption low**: Core features are adopted by <70% of customers; suggests feature is not valuable or usability issues.
- **Long TTV**: Takes >6 weeks for customers to gain value; long TTV increases churn and customer dissatisfaction.
- **Product quality issues**: High defect rates, frequent outages, or slow incident response; indicates poor product quality.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the DAU/MAU ratio or equivalent engagement metric? Is engagement increasing, stable, or declining?
- How often do customers use the product? What is typical session length and frequency?
- What is adoption rate for key/core features? Are features being used as intended?
- What is time-to-value (TTV)? How quickly do customers gain benefit from the product?
- What is product uptime/reliability? What is the defect rate or incident response time?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Daily/Monthly Active Users** (DAU/MAU) with trend over time
- **DAU/MAU ratio** (30%+ is strong for SaaS)
- **Usage frequency** (sessions per user per month, session length)
- **Feature adoption** (% of customers using each feature)
- **Engagement cohort analysis** (how engagement changes with customer tenure)
- **Time-to-value (TTV)** (how long until customer gains value)
- **Product health metrics** (uptime, defect rate, MTTR, NPS, support ticket volume)
- **Engagement benchmarking** vs. industry comparables

## Module 4: Commercial Milestones

### Definition
Major business achievements that validate product-market fit, competitive positioning, and business model viability. Commercial milestones include: product launch, first customer, first enterprise customer, first $100K ARR, profitability, partnerships, awards, or media recognition. Milestones should be concrete and achievement-based, not timeline-based.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Milestones listed with dates**: The submission lists key milestones with achievement dates. Example: "Jan 2024: Beta launch (5 beta customers). May 2024: GA launch (25 customers, $50K ARR). Aug 2024: First enterprise customer ($50K ACV). Oct 2024: $300K ARR milestone. Dec 2024: 60 customers, $300K ARR."
- **Milestone types diverse**: Milestones span product, customer, revenue, and partnership achievements. Example: "Product: GA launch (May). Customers: First enterprise (Aug). Revenue: $300K ARR (Dec). Partnerships: Reseller partnership with [company] (Nov)."
- **Milestone selection strategic**: Milestones highlight achievements demonstrating progress toward business goals. Example: "Milestones show: (1) Rapid initial adoption (5 customers in beta → 25 at GA); (2) Revenue growth ($0 → $300K ARR in 6 months); (3) Customer quality improvement (first enterprise customer demonstrates enterprise sales capability)."
- **Milestone momentum**: Milestones show accelerating progress. Example: "Time from GA launch to first enterprise customer: 3 months. Time from GA to $300K ARR: 7 months. Pace of achievement is accelerating."
- **Planned milestones**: The submission includes planned near-term milestones. Example: "Q1 2025 milestones: $500K ARR, 80 customers, first international customer, API launch."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **No milestones reported**: The submission does not articulate key achievements.
- **Trivial milestones**: Lists "hired first employee" or "raised seed round" as major milestones, rather than customer/revenue milestones.
- **Timeline-based rather than achievement-based**: "Month 6 target: $100K ARR" (not yet achieved) presented as a milestone.
- **Declining pace of milestones**: Milestones are spaced further apart over time; suggests momentum is slowing.
- **Unsubstantiated milestones**: Milestones are claimed without supporting evidence.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What are the key product, customer, and revenue milestones achieved to date?
- What do these milestones demonstrate about product-market fit and business model?
- Is momentum accelerating (milestones closer together over time) or decelerating?
- What are the planned milestones for next 6–12 months?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Chronological milestone list** with dates and achievement descriptions
- **Milestone types** (product launches, customer milestones, revenue milestones, partnerships)
- **Milestone progression** showing acceleration or deceleration of progress
- **Customer quality milestones** (first enterprise customer, first customer in target segment)
- **Revenue milestones** ($50K ARR, $500K ARR, profitability, etc.)
- **Planned near-term milestones** (6–12 months ahead)

## Module 5: Pilot and Proof of Concept Results

### Definition
Evidence from pilot deployments or proofs of concept demonstrating that the product solves the customer problem and delivers promised value. Pilot results validate product-market fit at a smaller scale before scaling. Strong pilot results show: measurable impact, customer satisfaction, and commitment to broader deployment or purchase.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Pilot structure clear**: The submission describes the pilot scope, duration, and success criteria. Example: "Pilot program: 5 mid-market customers, 8-week pilot, success criteria: (1) 85% of invoices processed automatically, (2) Reduction of AP processing time by 50%, (3) NPS >40, (4) Customer commitment to 3-year contract post-pilot."
- **Pilot results quantified**: The submission reports specific results against success criteria. Example: "Pilot results: (1) 91% automation rate (target: 85%, exceeded); (2) AP processing time reduced 58% (target: 50%, exceeded); (3) NPS 58 (target: 40, exceeded); (4) 5/5 customers signed 3-year contracts post-pilot (commitment 100%)."
- **Pilot learnings documented**: The submission articulates learnings from pilots. Example: "Key learnings: (1) Customers need GL integration more urgently than anticipated; (2) Training time is longer for less-digitally-mature customers; (3) Implementation approach should be phased by invoice type rather than all-at-once."
- **Product iteration from pilots**: The submission shows that pilot feedback drove product improvements. Example: "Pilot feedback: Customers struggled with exception handling UI. Based on feedback, we redesigned exception handling interface; post-redesign, NPS improved 10 points."
- **Customer conversion post-pilot**: The submission shows that pilot customers convert to paying customers. Example: "5 pilot customers: 5 converted to GA customers; 100% conversion rate. Pilot customers have higher retention (95% at 6 months) than non-pilot customers (82%), validating that pilots drive customer commitment."
- **Pilot ROI demonstrated**: The submission quantifies customer ROI realized in pilot. Example: "Pilot customer: $50K annual AP processing cost reduced to $21K (58% reduction). Annual customer savings: $29K. Our product cost: $12K/year. Customer ROI: 240% annually."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **No pilots conducted**: The submission does not reference any pilot deployments or PoCs.
- **Pilot results not quantified**: "Pilot customers were happy" without specific metrics.
- **Pilot success criteria not met**: Pilot customers did not achieve stated objectives; results were disappointing but are not addressed.
- **No learnings from pilots**: Does not articulate what was learned or how feedback drove product changes.
- **Pilot customers not converted**: Pilot customers did not sign contracts post-pilot; suggests they did not see sufficient value.
- **Pilot results not replicated**: Only 1 of 5 pilot customers actually achieved the stated benefits; questions generalizability.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What pilot programs have been conducted? With how many customers, for how long?
- What were the success criteria? Were they met?
- What specific results were achieved (quantified impact, customer satisfaction, commitments)?
- What product changes resulted from pilot feedback?
- Did pilot customers convert to paying customers? What is the conversion rate?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Pilot description** (customer count, duration, scope, success criteria)
- **Pilot results** quantified against success criteria
- **Customer impact metrics** (cost savings, time savings, quality improvements)
- **Customer satisfaction** (NPS, testimonials, feedback)
- **Customer commitment post-pilot** (% of pilots converting to contracts)
- **Learnings from pilots** and product iterations based on feedback
- **Replication success** showing pilots are repeatable with new customers

## Module 6: Independent Validation

### Definition
Evidence of third-party validation of the startup's product, market fit, or business model. Independent validation may come from: industry awards, analyst recognition, media coverage, customer testimonials, third-party reviews (G2, Capterra), or customer case studies published by customers. Independent validation builds credibility and reduces investor perception of risk.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Awards and recognition**: The submission reports industry awards or accolades. Example: "Won [Industry Name] Product of the Year 2024 (out of 200 nominees); finalist in [Category] Innovation Award."
- **Media coverage**: The submission cites reputable media coverage. Example: "Featured in [Publication] as 'Emerging AI Leaders for Finance'; coverage reached 500K readers."
- **Analyst recognition**: The submission references third-party analyst coverage. Example: "Named as 'Strong Performer' in Gartner's 2024 [Category] report; noted for customer satisfaction and ease of use."
- **Customer testimonials and case studies**: The submission includes customer testimonials and published case studies. Example: "[Customer] published case study showing 40% reduction in AP costs; [Customer] testimonial: 'Best AP automation solution we've evaluated.'"
- **Third-party review platforms**: The submission cites customer reviews from G2, Capterra, or similar. Example: "G2 Rating: 4.6/5 (50 verified customer reviews). Capterra rating: 4.7/5. Highest-rated solution in [Category] on both platforms."
- **Customer logos**: The submission includes customer logos/names (with permission) establishing credibility. Example: "Customers include: [Enterprise Company 1], [Enterprise Company 2], [Mid-market Company 1]."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **No independent validation**: The submission does not cite any awards, media coverage, analyst recognition, or third-party reviews.
- **Internal validation only**: Testimonials come from company-selected customers or investor-connected customers, not independent sources.
- **Trivial awards**: Awards are from niche award programs with low credibility.
- **No customer logos**: Company name-drops customers but does not provide verifiable customer references.
- **Negative reviews ignored**: Available third-party reviews are negative or mediocre, but are not addressed.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What industry awards or recognition has the startup received?
- What reputable media coverage or analyst recognition exists?
- What is the startup's rating on third-party review platforms (G2, Capterra)?
- What customer testimonials or case studies validate the product?
- Are there customer logos or verifiable customer references?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Award list** with source and credibility
- **Media coverage** (publication name, date, relevance, reach)
- **Analyst recognition** (Gartner, Forrester, industry analysts)
- **Customer testimonials** (attributed to named customers, with permission)
- **Published case studies** from customers (with metrics)
- **Third-party review platform ratings** (G2, Capterra, TrustRadius)
- **Customer logos or references** for verification

## Module 7: Failure and Adaptation Record

### Definition
Honest assessment of challenges encountered and how the startup has adapted. Rather than claiming flawless execution, a strong traction assessment acknowledges setbacks and demonstrates learning. Setbacks are normal; inability to acknowledge and learn from them is concerning. This module is also about adaptability—how quickly the startup pivots or iterates in response to feedback.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Challenges acknowledged**: The submission candidly discusses challenges encountered. Example: "Initial go-to-market strategy focused on large enterprise; we realized sales cycles were 12+ months and CAC was prohibitive. We pivoted to mid-market, which has 6-month sales cycles and $20K CAC."
- **Learning from setbacks**: The submission articulates what was learned and how approach changed. Example: "Beta customers complained about onboarding complexity; we discovered our documentation was insufficient and onboarding process was not intuitive. We redesigned onboarding, added guided workflows, and published video training. Post-redesign, TTV dropped from 6 weeks to 2 weeks, and NPS improved 15 points."
- **Product iterations**: The submission shows rapid iteration based on customer feedback. Example: "Launched with rule-based matching engine; customers said it required too much configuration. We pivoted to ML-based matching (8-week development); new customers now require 1 week setup instead of 3 weeks."
- **Pricing iterations**: The submission discusses pricing experimentation. Example: "Started with per-transaction pricing; customers preferred predictable SaaS subscription pricing. We changed to per-user subscription pricing; same customer adoption timeline but higher ACV and better revenue predictability."
- **Go-to-market pivots**: The submission discusses pivots in customer targeting or channels. Example: "Initially focused on all manufacturing verticals; after 6 months, realized automotive customers were highest-NPS and fastest to purchase. We focused resources on automotive; focused go-to-market has improved CAC by 30%."
- **Timeline implications**: The submission acknowledges how pivots affected timeline. Example: "Pivoting to mid-market delayed $1M revenue target by 3 months, but improved long-term unit economics significantly."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Flawless execution claimed**: The submission asserts that everything has gone according to plan with no setbacks.
- **Challenges not acknowledged**: Major pivots are presented without acknowledging why they were necessary.
- **No learning articulated**: Challenges are mentioned but no explanation of how the startup learned or changed.
- **Slow adaptation**: Challenges were identified early but the startup took 6+ months to respond.
- **Repeated failures**: The startup made the same mistake multiple times, suggesting poor learning.
- **Blame external**: All challenges are blamed on external factors (market timing, competitor moves) with no acknowledgment of internal execution issues.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What major challenges or setbacks has the startup encountered?
- How did the startup learn from these challenges?
- What product, go-to-market, or strategy pivots resulted from feedback?
- How quickly did the startup adapt to challenges?
- What changes were made, and what was the impact?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Challenges articulated** (product, market, go-to-market, team-related)
- **Learning and adaptation** showing how feedback drove changes
- **Specific pivots or iterations** with timeline and impact
- **Speed of adaptation** (how quickly the startup responded to feedback)
- **Impact of changes** (customer feedback, metrics improvements, business impact)
- **Retrospective honesty** about what did not work and why

## Module 8: Sustainability of Traction

### Definition
Assessment of whether the startup's traction is sustainable or if it masks underlying problems. For example, a startup may have grown quickly by acquiring low-quality customers with high churn, making growth unsustainable. Or growth may depend on a single customer, making it fragile. Sustainability evaluation assesses whether momentum will continue or if fundamental problems exist.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Churn rate healthy**: The submission reports low customer churn. Example: "Annual churn: 10%; this is below industry average (15–20%) and indicates product-market fit. Churn is declining over time (12% in Month 1, 10% by Month 6) as product improves and customer satisfaction increases."
- **Retention improving**: The submission shows that customer retention is increasing. Example: "6-month retention: 85% (Cohort 1), 88% (Cohort 2), 90% (Cohort 3). Improving retention indicates stronger product-market fit and operational execution."
- **Unit economics improving**: The submission shows that CAC is declining and/or LTV is increasing. Example: "CAC declining: $25K (Year 1) → $18K (Year 2), driven by improving sales efficiency. LTV increasing: $140K (Year 1) → $165K (Year 2), driven by lower churn and expansion revenue."
- **Revenue diversification**: The submission shows that revenue is not dependent on few customers. Example: "Top 10 customers: 35% of revenue. No single customer >8%. Diversified customer base indicates revenue is not fragile."
- **Growth repeatable**: The submission shows that growth is not dependent on one-off events. Example: "Customer acquisition is consistent and repeatable: 10 customers/month for 6 consecutive months. Growth is not lumpy or dependent on single large deals."
- **Product-market fit signals**: The submission shows indicators of strong product-market fit (high NPS, low churn, strong retention, high engagement). Example: "NPS: 58 (strong for SaaS). Engagement: 75% MAU. Retention: 90% at 6 months. These signals indicate genuine product-market fit, not hype-driven adoption."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **High churn masks growth**: Company has acquired 100 customers but lost 70; growth is not sustainable.
- **Unit economics worsening**: CAC is increasing or LTV decreasing over time; growth is becoming less profitable.
- **Revenue concentration**: Top 3 customers are 50% of revenue; if any leaves, revenue collapses.
- **Engagement declining**: DAU/MAU declining over time; suggests customers are becoming less engaged.
- **Growth dependent on one-time events**: All growth is from a single large partnership or competitor exit; unlikely to repeat.
- **Execution quality declining**: Defect rates increasing, support volume increasing, or NPS declining; product quality issues undermine sustainability.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the customer churn rate? Is churn stable, declining, or increasing?
- What is customer retention at different tenure points (6-month, 1-year, 2-year retention)?
- Are unit economics (CAC, LTV) improving or worsening over time?
- Is revenue concentrated in few customers, or is it diversified?
- Is growth repeatable, or is it dependent on one-time events?
- What are the key indicators of product-market fit (NPS, engagement, retention)?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Churn rate** (monthly or annual) with trend over time
- **Retention cohort analysis** showing % of customers retained at 6, 12, 24 months
- **Unit economics trend** (CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC) over time
- **Revenue concentration** (% from top customers, concentration index)
- **Customer acquisition sustainability** (repeatable vs. one-off)
- **Product-market fit indicators** (NPS, engagement, retention benchmarks)
- **Execution quality metrics** (defect rate, support volume, uptime, CSAT)

## Cross-Domain Dependencies

The Traction domain informs and validates all other domains:

- **Domain 1 (Market and Opportunity)**: Traction validates that the market opportunity is real and the demand validation was accurate. Customer acquisition rates prove market demand exists.
- **Domain 2 (Solution and Product)**: Traction (engagement, retention, NPS) validates that the product delivers promised value and achieves product-market fit.
- **Domain 3 (Business Model)**: Traction (actual CAC, LTV, churn, pricing) validates business model assumptions. Actual unit economics must align with projections.
- **Domain 4 (Go-to-Market)**: Traction (customer acquisition rate, sales cycle, channel performance) validates that go-to-market strategy is working.

Conversely, traction is informed by:

- **All domains**: Traction is the ultimate validation of the entire investment thesis. Strong traction across customer acquisition, revenue, engagement, and retention suggests all other domains are well-executed.

---

## Assessment Notes for the Scoring Agent

When assessing the Traction domain:

1. **Weight actual metrics over projections**: Traction is objective fact. A startup with $300K ARR has traction; a startup claiming "$10M ARR by Year 2" without customers does not. Judge on actual metrics, not projections.

2. **Assess growth sustainability**: Is growth accelerating or decelerating? Is growth dependent on quality customers or low-quality acquisition? Does churn and engagement support the growth story?

3. **Validate revenue quality**: High revenue growth with high churn is not good traction. NRR >100% with low churn is excellent traction.

4. **Check for vanity metrics**: Some startups report vanity metrics (users, signups) while hiding real metrics (paying customers, revenue, churn). Look for comprehensive metrics, not selective reporting.

5. **Cross-reference metrics**: Does revenue align with customer count and ACV? Do engagement metrics support low churn claims? Do financial metrics reconcile?

6. **Assess timeline credibility**: If the startup claims $1M ARR in 12 months, does this align with customer acquisition rate and sales cycle? Do the numbers add up?

7. **Evaluate milestone pace**: Is the startup maintaining momentum? Are milestones spaced closer together (accelerating) or further apart (decelerating)?

8. **Look for red flags**: Declining engagement, increasing churn, declining NPS, or worsening unit economics all signal underlying problems despite apparent growth.

---

## Summary: Good Traction Assessment

A strong Traction assessment demonstrates:

- **Real revenue** with healthy growth rate (20%+ MoM for early stage; 10%+ for more mature)
- **Sustainable unit economics** with improving CAC and/or increasing LTV over time
- **Healthy customer retention** with low churn and high NRR (>100%)
- **Strong engagement** with high DAU/MAU ratio and consistent usage
- **Quality customer base** with appropriate customer concentration and customer quality indicators
- **Product-market fit signals** (high NPS, low churn, high retention, strong engagement)
- **Repeatable growth** driven by consistent customer acquisition and expansion
- **Clear momentum** with accelerating milestones and improving metrics

Traction is the ultimate validator of all prior hypotheses. Strong traction transforms a promising pitch into a proven business model and significantly reduces investor risk.
