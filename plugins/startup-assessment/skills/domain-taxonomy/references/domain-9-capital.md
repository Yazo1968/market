# Domain 9: Capital Structure and Transaction Terms

## Domain Overview

Capital Structure and Transaction Terms define the ownership, rights, and return potential of the investment. This domain assesses the company's current capitalization, the structure and terms of the current funding round, the allocation of proceeds, the valuation basis, investor protections, and the expected waterfall of returns across shareholders. Understanding capital structure is essential because it determines:
- Probability-weighted return to each investor class
- Alignment of incentives between founders, employees, and investors
- Ability to raise future capital without excessive dilution
- Control and governance rights of investors
- Exit mechanisms and negotiation dynamics

This domain directly influences investment attractiveness because it determines both the upside (what return multiple is possible) and downside (how protected is capital in low-outcome scenarios). Poor capital structure alignment or unfavorable terms can eliminate substantial value even in successful companies.

## Module 1: Current Capital Structure

### Definition
Documentation of all outstanding equity ownership, including founder and employee options, previous investor rounds, debt obligations, and any other claims on equity (warrants, convertibles). The cap table must be complete, updated, and reconcilable to actual shareholder records.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Cap table current as of recent date (within 30 days) with clear update history
- All shareholders listed with share count, percentage ownership, and vesting schedule
- Founder equity clearly documented: vesting schedule (typically 4 years with 1-year cliff), any acceleration triggers
- Employee option pool documented: total shares reserved, strike price, vesting terms, exercises to date
- Previous funding rounds detailed: investor name, investment amount, round size, share price, and valuation
- Board seat allocation clear: which investors have board seats, observer rights, or information rights
- All debt obligations listed: principal amount, interest rate, maturity, lender identity, and material covenants
- Convertible instruments listed: convertible notes or SAFEs with conversion terms, discount rates, and valuation caps
- Fully-diluted cap table: cap table including all options and convertibles assuming full dilution
- No cap table surprises: all securities accounted for; no undisclosed shareholders or claims on equity

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Cap table out of date: last updated 6+ months ago; employee option exercises not recorded
- Cap table incomplete: major equity holders missing, option pool size unclear, or fully-diluted percentage not calculated
- Founder equity vesting unclear: vesting schedule not documented, or shares already fully vested with no forfeiture if founder leaves
- Employee options terms inconsistent: different employees have different strike prices, vesting terms, or acceleration provisions
- Hidden equity: undisclosed convertibles, SAFEs, or warrant agreements that would dilute existing shareholders
- Debt terms vague: debt amount and lender clear, but interest rate, maturity, and covenants not documented
- Board seat allocation misaligned: investors without clear decision rights; board structure not documented
- Cap table arithmetic errors: shares don't add up to 100%; percentages don't reconcile to share counts
- No distinction between common and preferred: preference structure and liquidation priority unclear
- Unclear full-dilution: company unable to calculate fully-diluted ownership impact of pending option grants

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the current cap table? Who owns what percentage?
2. How many founder shares are vesting? What is the vesting schedule and cliff?
3. How many shares are reserved in the option pool? What percentage of fully-diluted cap table is this?
4. How many options have been granted? At what strike price and with what vesting?
5. What previous funding rounds has the company raised? What were the valuations and price per share?
6. Are there any convertible instruments (SAFEs, convertible notes)? What are the conversion terms?
7. How much debt does the company have? What is the repayment schedule and interest rate?
8. Are there any warrant agreements? How many shares do warrants represent?
9. Who has board seats and observer rights? What are information and approval rights?
10. What is the fully-diluted cap table if all options are exercised and convertibles convert?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Cap table spreadsheet: current cap table with all shareholders, shares, percentages, and vesting schedules
- Fully-diluted cap table: showing impact of all options and convertibles
- Stock ledger: share issuances and exercises documented with dates and amounts
- Founder equity documents: stock purchase agreement or equity grant agreement for founders with vesting terms
- Option plan: company's stock option plan document with key terms (vesting, strike price, exercise procedures)
- Equity grants: documentation of all option grants made, with grant date, strike price, vesting terms, and grant amount
- Board resolution: board or stockholder resolution approving option grants and pricing
- Convertible instruments: SAFE agreements, convertible note agreements, or other convertible instruments with terms
- Previous funding rounds: term sheets or stock purchase agreements for all previous funding rounds
- Debt agreements: loan agreements, promissory notes, or debt instruments with full terms

## Module 2: Funding Instrument

### Definition
Characterization and full terms of the securities being purchased in the current round, including investment type (equity, debt, convertible, preferred stock, etc.), price per share or valuation basis, rights and preferences, and any unusual terms.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Investment structure clear: equity, preferred stock, senior debt, convertible note, or hybrid instrument clearly identified
- Price per share well-documented: share price for equity rounds, valuation cap for convertibles, interest rate for debt
- Preference structure transparent: if preferred stock, dividend rights, liquidation preferences, anti-dilution, and redemption terms clearly stated
- Investment terms align with stage: Series A typically 10-15% dilution, Series B 15-25%, later rounds 10-20%
- Terms market-standard for stage: anti-dilution provisions, board seat allocation, information rights typical for stage
- No unusual terms: no offensive covenants (e.g., revenue targets triggering investor control), no blocking rights that prevent future financing
- Clear use of proceeds: investor knows how capital will be deployed
- Founder alignment: investment structure and terms aligned with founder incentives to build long-term value

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Unclear instrument: term sheet describes "investment" without clarity on equity vs. debt vs. convertible
- Valuation opaque: share price not documented, or price calculation based on vague methodology
- Onerous terms: anti-dilution too harsh (weighted average rather than broad-based), redemption clauses, conversion discounts
- Over-dilutive: investment terms imply >25% dilution in growth stage round; suggests high pressure on valuation or weak fundraising power
- Misaligned incentives: terms favor investors over founders; founders discouraged from staying long-term
- Control concentrated: single investor or investor class has excessive control (>50% board seats, broad blocking rights)
- Unusual instruments: convertible with aggressive discount rates, SAFE with cap but no discount, or SAFEs with pro-rata rights
- Debt-like returns: equity with dividend requirements, return preferences, or exit obligations designed to mimic debt
- Terms conflict with future fundraising: current terms make future fundraising difficult (e.g., broad anti-dilution makes investors expensive to add to later rounds)

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What security type is being purchased (equity, preferred, debt, convertible)?
2. What is the valuation (pre-money and post-money for equity; valuation cap for convertibles)?
3. What is the price per share or investment amount and resulting ownership percentage?
4. If preferred stock, what are the liquidation preferences (participating, non-participating)?
5. If preferred stock, what is the anti-dilution protection (broad-based, narrow-based, weighted average)?
6. What board seats or governance rights do investors receive?
7. What information rights do investors have?
8. Are there any redemption clauses, put rights, or mandatory conversion triggers?
9. Are there any unusual covenants or restrictions (revenue targets, spending caps, key person insurance)?
10. How do the terms compare to typical venture capital terms for this stage?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Term sheet: investor-signed term sheet or executed investment agreement clearly describing security type and material terms
- Capitalization table showing post-money valuation and post-money ownership for all shareholders
- Preference structure document: details of liquidation preferences, dividend rights, anti-dilution, redemption for any preferred stock
- Comparison to market terms: benchmarking against typical venture capital terms for stage (dilution %, board seats, information rights)
- Full investment agreement: final executed investment documents with all operative terms

## Module 3: Use of Proceeds

### Definition
Clear allocation of capital by activity, mapped to milestones and deployment timeline. Strong companies have explicit plan for how capital will be deployed, with accountability for capital efficiency.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Use of proceeds document: clear breakdown of capital allocation by major activity (product development, sales/marketing, hiring, infrastructure)
- Milestone mapping: capital allocation tied to specific milestones (product launch, revenue target, geographic expansion)
- Deployment timeline: when each bucket of capital will be deployed (immediately, quarterly, phased)
- Accountability assigned: owner identified for each capital bucket (VP Engineering for product capital, VP Sales for go-to-market capital)
- Contingency plan: what happens if key milestone is missed or market changes; how would capital be reallocated?
- Capital efficiency visible: burn rate and capital runway calculated; company demonstrates capital discipline
- Runway extension: deployment plan extends runway to next funding milestone (typically 18-24 months)
- No capital hoarding: company is deploying capital efficiently, not accumulating unnecessary cash reserves

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Vague use of proceeds: "general corporate purposes" or broad categories without specificity
- No timeline: allocation stated without clarity on deployment schedule
- No accountability: allocation stated but no owner identified for each bucket
- Misalignment with strategy: capital allocation doesn't support stated go-to-market strategy or product roadmap
- Too much cash hoarded: company burning at high rate but accumulating cash, suggesting capital raising exceeded realistic needs
- Milestone-agnostic: capital deployed regardless of business milestones or market conditions
- No contingency: no plan to adjust capital allocation if strategy or market changes
- Over-investment in one area: >50% of capital in one bucket (e.g., 70% to sales) leaving other areas underfunded

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the planned capital allocation by major bucket? (product/engineering, sales/marketing, operations, infrastructure, capital reserve)
2. What percentage of capital is allocated to each major activity?
3. How will the capital be deployed over time? (quarterly, phased, immediately)
4. Who is accountable for each capital bucket?
5. How does capital allocation support the revenue and growth plan?
6. If a major milestone is missed, how would capital allocation be adjusted?
7. What is the planned runway with the new capital? (months to cash flow positive or next funding event)
8. Is the company being conservative or aggressive with capital deployment?
9. What are the key hiring plans enabled by the new capital?
10. What infrastructure or capability investments will the capital support?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Use of proceeds breakdown: capital allocated by major category with dollar amounts and percentages
- Deployment timeline: quarterly or phased deployment plan showing when capital will be spent
- Milestone mapping: connection between capital deployment and key business milestones
- Accountability: owner identified for each capital category
- Runway calculation: months of runway at projected burn rate with new capital
- Contingency plan: how would capital be reallocated if strategy or market changes

## Module 4: Valuation Basis

### Definition
Methodology for deriving the current round valuation, supported by comparable company analysis, precedent transactions, DCF analysis, or other defensible approaches. Valuation must be justified relative to stage, metrics, and market comparables.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Valuation based on comparable companies: revenue or EBITDA multiples benchmarked against public companies or recent M&A at similar stage and segment
- Multiple methodologies triangulated: valuation derived from two or more approaches (comparables, DCF, revenue multiples) with similar result
- Transparency on assumptions: assumptions underlying valuation clearly stated (exit multiple, discount rate, terminal growth rate)
- Conservative relative to market leaders: valuation doesn't imply outcome (e.g., 50x revenue at 10x public comp revenue multiple)
- Growth justifies valuation: revenue growth rate, TAM expansion, or unit economics improvements justify revenue multiple
- Comparable transactions cited: recent M&A or funding rounds in same category at similar stage support valuation
- Board/investor consensus: valuation endorsed by board and appears to be negotiated fairly (not one-sided)
- Reasonable dilution: post-money valuation implies reasonable dilution (10-25% for growth stage)

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Valuation based on founder intuition: "we think we're worth $50M" with no supporting analysis
- Valuation methodology unclear: not stated how valuation was derived
- Comparable analysis missing: no revenue or EBITDA multiple benchmarking to similar companies
- Outlier valuation: company valued at 100x revenue when similar public companies trade at 8x; significant gap unexplained
- Assumptions unrealistic: revenue multiple assumes 5-year exit at $500M revenue when realistic exit is $100M
- Insufficient growth to justify valuation: company growing 20% YoY but valued at 15x revenue (implies exit at 30x); multiple doesn't justify growth
- No comparable transactions: recent M&A or funding comparables not cited
- Board skepticism: board or investor push back on valuation; suggests valuation not well-supported
- Excessive dilution: post-money valuation implies 30%+ dilution in growth-stage round; suggests overvaluation or weak fundraising

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the pre-money and post-money valuation? What is the implied ownership percentage for this investment?
2. What revenue multiples are comparable public companies trading at? What is our company's revenue multiple?
3. What recent M&A transactions are comparable? What multiples did acquirers pay?
4. What is the DCF valuation if you project to exit in 5-7 years at reasonable exit multiple?
5. How fast is the company growing revenue? Does growth justify the valuation multiple?
6. What are the key assumptions in the valuation (exit year, exit multiple, discount rate)?
7. How does the valuation compare to other recent funding rounds in the same category?
8. If company misses revenue targets by 20%, how much does valuation decrease?
9. What would be the exit multiple implied by this valuation at different exit revenue levels?
10. Is the valuation reasonable if company only reaches $50M revenue at exit (vs. $200M implied)?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Valuation summary: pre-money, post-money, price per share, and resulting ownership
- Comparable company analysis: public company multiples and recent M&A transactions benchmarking revenue, EBITDA, or other multiples
- Valuation models: DCF or comparable company analysis supporting the valuation
- Assumption detail: key assumptions (exit revenue, exit multiple, discount rate, terminal growth) documented
- Sensitivity analysis: valuation across range of assumptions (exit revenue 30-200%, exit multiple +/- 20%)
- Board/investor endorsement: evidence that valuation was negotiated and endorsed by board and investors
- Comparable precedent: other venture or growth capital rounds in category with valuation and terms compared

## Module 5: Investor/Lender Protections

### Definition
Contractual and legal protections for investors including covenants, security interests, liquidation preferences, anti-dilution rights, and information rights. Protections vary by investor class and stage, but should be balanced to protect downside while preserving founder incentives.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Anti-dilution provisions appropriate: weighted average broad-based (standard for venture) rather than full ratchet (aggressive)
- Liquidation preferences clear: for preferred stock, participating vs. non-participating and preference amount (1x vs. 2x) appropriate to stage
- Information rights balanced: quarterly financials and annual audit, but no excessive reporting burden
- Board representation balanced: investor representation proportional to ownership; not excessive control for minority stake
- Protective provisions appropriate: vetoing rights for major decisions (asset sales, related-party transactions, new debt) but not operational control
- No onerous covenants: no revenue targets, no key person requirements, no spending caps that would constrain management
- Drag-along/tag-along rights: standard provisions allowing majority to force sale or minority to participate in exit
- Anti-dilution not punitive: anti-dilution applies only to down rounds, not to all future rounds

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Anti-dilution overly aggressive: full ratchet anti-dilution (gives investor additional shares if any down round) heavily dilutes founders
- Redemption clauses: investor has right to force company to buy back shares (creates liability risk)
- Onerous covenants: company required to hit revenue targets, maintain ratio of burn/capital, or restrict spending
- Excessive control: investor has approval rights for hiring, customer contracts, or operational decisions
- Liquidation preferences punitive: 3x non-participating preferred (investor gets 3x capital back before anyone else benefits)
- Blocking rights excessive: investor can veto future fundraising, changes to strategy, or normal business decisions
- Key person clause: founder departure triggers investor control change or buyback
- Dividend or interest requirements: preferred shares have dividend requirement or annual interest accrual
- Put/call rights: investor has right to force exit at certain return multiple or company has obligation to buy back
- Clawback provisions: investor can force founder to forfeit gains if company underperforms

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the anti-dilution protection? (weighted average vs. full ratchet; broad-based vs. narrow-based)
2. What are the liquidation preferences? (participating, non-participating, and preference multiple)
3. What board seats or observer rights does the investor receive?
4. What information rights does the investor have?
5. What protective provisions give the investor blocking rights?
6. Are there any revenue targets or covenants that could be breached?
7. Is there a key person clause that triggers investor control if founder leaves?
8. Are there any redemption clauses or put rights?
9. Are there drag-along and tag-along rights?
10. How do these terms compare to standard venture capital terms for this stage?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Investor rights schedule: detailed list of board representation, information rights, and protective provisions
- Liquidation preference analysis: illustrative waterfall showing investor returns at exit valuation
- Anti-dilution mechanics: explanation and illustrative example of how anti-dilution would apply in down round
- Governance rights: board seat allocation, committee participation, observer rights, and approval thresholds
- Covenants summary: list of any covenants, including thresholds and consequences of breach
- Comparison to market terms: benchmarking investor protections against typical venture capital terms for stage

## Module 6: Governance Rights

### Definition
Control and decision-making authority, including board representation, approval thresholds, veto rights, and governance structures. Governance rights determine whether founders retain operational control or investors have veto power over major decisions.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Board composition balanced: founders control majority (3+ of 5 seats typically) or board is balanced at parity
- Founder/operator control preserved: management retains authority over day-to-day operations and strategy execution
- Investor voice appropriate: investor/independent board members have input on strategy but don't control operations
- Protective provisions reasonable: investor can veto strategic decisions (exit, major M&A, related-party deals) but not operational decisions
- Committee structure clear: audit or compensation committees established if stage warrants (typically Series B+)
- Decision rights documented: which decisions require board approval, which are management authority
- Tied voting: common and preferred shareholders vote together on most matters (not separate preferred votes on routine items)
- Amendment provisions: changes to governance or terms require reasonable thresholds (typically 66-75% investor vote)
- No governance gridlock: board structure doesn't create deadlock or prevent management action

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Investor control: investor holds board majority or controlling share of seats (problematic unless investor is primary capital source)
- Excessive veto rights: investor can veto hiring, customer contracts, or other operational decisions
- Tied voting not used: preferred shareholders vote separately on routine matters; creates friction and governance paralysis
- Governance structure unclear: board members and their appointment process not documented
- No independent directors: all directors are founders or investors; no independent perspective
- Governance changes at investor direction: board/investor can unilaterally change governance or restrict founder authority
- Related-party veto absent: no protection for minority shareholders if founder tries to take company private or sell to related party
- Amendment provisions too loose: governance can be changed by simple majority; allows investor to amend terms at any time
- No founder protection: if investor becomes hostile, founder has no recourse or blocking rights

### Key Assessment Questions
1. How is the board composed? How many directors and how are they appointed?
2. Who appoints board seats for founders, investors, and any independent directors?
3. What decisions require board approval vs. management authority?
4. What protective provisions give investors veto rights?
5. How are governance amendments made? What is the threshold for changes?
6. Are there any investor committees (audit, compensation) and how are they composed?
7. If investors become hostile, what protection does founder have?
8. How frequently does the board meet and how are decisions documented?
9. Are there any restrictions on founder actions (hiring caps, customer contract approvals, etc.)?
10. How would governance change if there is a follow-on round with new investor?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Board composition and director bios: current board members, how appointed, background, and role
- Board seat allocation for future rounds: how will board change if new investors join
- Protective provisions: detailed list of decisions requiring investor approval
- Governance structure: decision-making authority for routine decisions, major decisions, and emergency decisions
- Meeting cadence and documentation: how frequently board meets, how minutes are documented
- Amendment procedures: how governance can be changed and what thresholds apply
- Related-party transaction provisions: protections for minority shareholders vs. founder self-dealing
- Founder protections: founder blocking rights, anti-dilution, or other protections if investor becomes hostile

## Module 7: Exit Mechanisms

### Definition
Contractual rights and procedures governing eventual exit (IPO, trade sale, secondary, or buyback). Exit mechanisms determine shareholder liquidity rights, timing, and potential disputes over exit proceeds.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Exit rights clear: drag-along rights allow majority to force sale; tag-along rights allow minority to exit with majority
- IPO process defined: if IPO is contemplated, underwriter role and registration process outlined
- Trade sale process clear: if acquirer emerges, process for negotiating and closing acquisition documented
- Secondary/buyback path: if exit timeline extends, company can conduct secondary sales or founder buybacks
- Dissenter rights balanced: minority shareholders have right to dissent and seek appraisal, with reasonable procedures
- Earnout structure: if acquisition involves earnouts, mechanics and dispute resolution clear
- Tax efficiency: exit structure minimizes tax leakage to shareholders (stock vs. asset sale, Section 338 elections, etc.)
- Precedents documented: past exits by founder or investor referenced; expected timeline for this company's exit
- No blocking rights: single shareholder cannot block exit desired by majority (unless rare special circumstance)

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Exit rights vague: drag-along and tag-along rights not documented; unclear who can force exit
- No liquidity path for minority: if founder wants to exit, minority investors stuck with illiquid shares
- Earnout disputes inevitable: earnout terms structured to invite disputes over achievement of targets
- Excessive dissenter rights: any shareholder can block exit by dissenting; governance paralysis
- No secondary path: if IPO or exit delayed, minority shareholders have no liquidity mechanism
- Founder blocking rights: founder can prevent exit even if majority investors want to exit
- Tax inefficient: exit structure results in unfavorable tax treatment (double taxation, excess capital gains)
- Founder payouts excessive: if acquisition, earnout structure allows founder to pocket majority of deal value
- No buyback right: company cannot buy back investor shares if investor wants to exit pre-IPO

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What are the drag-along and tag-along rights? Can a majority force the minority out?
2. What is the expected exit path (IPO, trade sale, secondary)?
3. What is the expected timeline for exit (5 years, 7 years, longer)?
4. Are there any blocking rights that would prevent the founder from exiting?
5. If there is an earnout in a sale, how are the targets set and disputes resolved?
6. What are the dissenter/appraisal rights?
7. Does the company have a secondary or buyback right to create liquidity pre-exit?
8. If there is an IPO, are all shareholders free to sell at IPO or are there lock-up periods?
9. How would exit proceeds be distributed (waterfall analysis)?
10. What tax structure is contemplated for exit (stock vs. asset sale, Section 338 elections)?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Drag-along and tag-along rights documentation
- Expected exit timeline and path (IPO, trade sale, secondary)
- Earnout mechanics if acquisition is contemplated (targets, measurement, timing, dispute resolution)
- Dissenter/appraisal rights procedures
- Secondary or buyback mechanisms for pre-exit liquidity
- Lock-up periods and restrictions on founder/employee sales post-exit
- Exit proceeds waterfall: illustrative distribution at exit at $100M, $300M, $500M+ valuations
- Tax planning for exit (legal structure, potential capital gains tax treatment)

## Module 8: Dilution and Waterfall Analysis

### Definition
Analysis of how existing shareholder ownership dilutes with current and future funding rounds, and how exit proceeds are distributed across shareholders at various exit valuations. Critical for understanding expected return to all shareholders.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Dilution path modeled: ownership percentages for founders and investors at current round and projected future rounds
- Future round dilution: if Series B, C, D contemplated, dilution from each round estimated
- Full waterfall analysis: at exit (5-year horizon), distribution of proceeds to each preference class and common shareholders
- Sensitivity to exit valuation: waterfall shown at conservative, base, and bull case exit valuations
- All security classes included: common, preferred, options, and any convertibles included in waterfall
- Founder economics transparent: founder return at different exit scenarios clearly shown
- Investor economics transparent: IRR and MOIC for each investor class calculated
- Path to profitability: if profitable before exit, waterfall reflects dividend or other return mechanism
- No mathematical inconsistencies: waterfall adds up to total proceeds; ownership percentages reconcile to cap table

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- No dilution analysis: company unable to articulate how ownership will evolve
- No waterfall analysis: shareholder returns not analyzed or modeled
- Waterfall assumptions hidden: waterfall doesn't disclose exit multiple, financing structure, or key assumptions
- Mathematical errors: waterfall ownership doesn't match cap table, or proceeds don't reconcile
- Single waterfall: only shows exit scenario; no sensitivity to exit valuation or structure
- Founder dilution excessive: founder ownership drops from 45% to 5% by exit due to over-dilution from future rounds
- Downside scenario missing: no analysis of return if exit valuation is disappointing (50% of base case)
- Employee option pool not modeled: waterfall shows fully-diluted ownership, but impact of option exercises not clear
- Liquidation preference mechanics ignored: waterfall assumes all proceeds distributed pro-rata, ignoring preferences
- Return expectations misaligned: founder expects 100x return, but waterfall shows 3x realistic return

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the current ownership percentage for each shareholder class?
2. If the company raises Series B at 30% dilution, what will ownership be?
3. If the company raises Series C at 20% dilution, what will ownership be at exit?
4. What is the fully-diluted ownership after all options are exercised?
5. At exit at $100M valuation, what is the distribution to each shareholder?
6. At exit at $500M valuation, what is the distribution to each shareholder?
7. What is the IRR for early investors at different exit valuations?
8. What is the founder's return at different exit valuations?
9. How do liquidation preferences impact the waterfall?
10. What return assumptions underpin the valuation (implied exit multiple and exit revenue)?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Cap table evolution: projected ownership after current round and future funding rounds
- Dilution analysis: ownership percentage for each shareholder at current round, Series B, Series C, and exit
- Exit waterfall: distribution of proceeds at exit at 2-3 different exit valuations (conservative, base, bull case)
- Fully-diluted analysis: waterfall including impact of all options and convertibles assuming full exercise and conversion
- Investor return analysis: IRR and MOIC for each investor class at different exit scenarios
- Founder return analysis: founder return at different exit scenarios
- Preference waterfall mechanics: liquidation preference applied correctly in waterfall

## Assessment Notes for Scoring Agent

Evaluate capital structure and transaction terms systematically across the eight modules using this framework:

**Gather Evidence**

1. Request cap table spreadsheet: obtain current cap table with all shareholders, shares, percentages, and vesting schedules.
2. Obtain executed term sheet or investment documents: review offering memorandum, stock purchase agreement, or other investment documents.
3. Analyze valuation basis: trace valuation to comparables, DCF analysis, or other support. Calculate implied exit valuation.
4. Map investor protections: review full list of investor rights, covenants, and protective provisions.
5. Model waterfall analysis: at current cap table, model proceeds distribution at range of exit valuations.
6. Assess board composition: review board structure and any recent or anticipated board changes.
7. Review prior funding: understand prior funding rounds and how valuation and ownership evolved.
8. Calculate fully-diluted impact: model ownership dilution from future funding rounds.

**Score Each Module**

For each of the eight modules, assign a quality rating:
- **Green (Fair and Aligned)**: Capital structure and terms are balanced between investor protection and founder incentives. Valuation is defensible. Ownership and return calculations are clear and mathematically sound.
- **Yellow (Acceptable with Caveats)**: Capital structure is reasonable but has some concerns (excessive dilution, aggressive anti-dilution, or limited founder upside). Valuation defensible but with ambitious assumptions.
- **Red (Problematic)**: Capital structure significantly favors investors over founders, or is unfavorable to one shareholder class. Valuation appears inflated or unjustified. Governance structure creates conflict or operational constraints.

**Synthesize Domain Score**

Roll up individual module scores to derive overall Domain 9 score:
- Count Green modules. If 6+ of 8 modules are Green, capital structure is fair and aligned, and investment terms are favorable.
- Count Yellow modules. 2-3 Yellow modules are normal; more than 3 Yellow indicates material misalignment or unfavorable terms.
- Count Red modules. Even 1 Red module suggests significant structural issue (overvaluation, excessive dilution, unfair terms, or governance conflict).

**Red Flags**
- Valuation unjustified: company valued at 50x revenue when comps trade at 8x; no credible basis for premium
- Excessive dilution: this round dilutes founders by >30%; future rounds could dilute to <20% ownership
- Anti-dilution punitive: full ratchet anti-dilution or other mechanisms that significantly penalize founders or future investors in down rounds
- Governance conflict: investor has majority board seats but is minority shareholder; or founder has blocking rights for all decisions
- Covenants problematic: company must hit revenue targets, limit spending, or restrict hiring; covenants likely to be breached
- Liquidation preferences excessive: 3x non-participating preferences, or preferences that give investors outsized returns in down scenarios
- Return expectations misaligned: founder expects 100x, but waterfall shows realistic 3x; expectations will clash if outcomes disappoint
- Cap table confusion: cap table incomplete, outdated, or mathematical errors; can't determine actual ownership
- Hidden securities: convertibles, SAFEs, or other securities discovered post-closing that materially dilute shareholders
- Exit mechanics unclear: no clarity on how company will exit, who can force exit, or how proceeds will be distributed

**Green Flags**
- Valuation supported: pre-money backed by comparable companies or precedent transactions; post-money reasonable for round
- Moderate dilution: this round dilutes by 15-25%; reasonable dilution trajectory for growth-stage company
- Anti-dilution fair: weighted average broad-based standard; applies only to down rounds, not all future rounds
- Governance balanced: founders retain operational control; investor has board representation proportional to ownership
- No problematic covenants: company has freedom to run business; no revenue targets or spending caps
- Liquidation preferences standard: 1x non-participating or similar; doesn't distort returns in any outcome scenario
- Return expectations aligned: founder, investor, and board aligned on return expectations and exit scenario
- Cap table clean: current, complete, mathematically sound; all securities documented
- Clear exit path: drag-along/tag-along rights, expected timeline, and exit process documented
- Shareholder alignment: all shareholders aligned around long-term value creation

## Cross-Domain Dependency Notes

**Domain 9 feeds into:**
- **Domain 7 (Financial Performance)**: Dilution and waterfall analysis depend on financial projections (exit revenue, timeline). Return analysis uses financial models as input.
- **Domain 8 (Risk Profile)**: Investor protections and liquidation preferences (Domain 9) are risk mitigation mechanisms for investor downside. Capital raise timing depends on runway and cash flow forecasts (Domain 7).

**Domain 9 depends on:**
- **Domain 7 (Financial Performance)**: Valuation and exit analysis depend on financial projections (revenue, EBITDA, exit multiple). Waterfall analysis uses financial trajectory as input.
- **Domain 6 (Management)**: Governance rights and board structure (Domain 9, Module 6) are influenced by investor confidence in management team (Domain 6).
