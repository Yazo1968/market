# Domain 6: Management and Organizational Capability

## Domain Overview

Management and Organizational Capability is the human capital foundation upon which all strategic and operational execution depends. This domain assesses whether the founding and leadership team possesses the demonstrated capability, relevant experience, and organizational discipline to navigate the company from its current stage to exit while building a sustainable, scalable operating system.

In venture assessment, management quality is often the primary risk mitigant when technology is proven or market validation is nascent. A strong team with relevant domain experience and a track record of building or scaling operations within their sector can materially improve outcome probability. Conversely, a first-time founder team without relevant functional expertise or prior exits faces substantially higher execution risk, even with promising technology or market opportunity.

This domain directly influences:
- Probability of hitting revenue and profitability milestones
- Ability to attract subsequent capital rounds and senior talent
- Speed of product-market fit iteration
- Resilience during market downturns or competitive pressure
- Exit readiness and acquisition appeal

## Module 1: Founder and Leadership Profile

### Definition
Assessment of the founding team's and CEO's relevant experience, domain expertise, functional expertise, track record of execution, prior exits, and demonstrated ability to build organizations. Include tenure in relevant sectors, previous leadership roles, industry credentials, and measurable outcomes from prior ventures.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- CEO with 10+ years relevant domain experience and prior successful exit (IPO, acquisition, or PE secondary)
- Founding team with complementary functional expertise (engineering, commercial, operations, finance)
- Prior experience at high-growth or scale-stage companies (e.g., Series B+ or equivalent scale)
- Demonstrated pattern of execution: shipped products on time, hit sales targets, managed P&L, scaled teams
- Domain expertise that uniquely positions them to solve the identified problem (e.g., founder of supply chain optimization software previously worked in logistics operations)
- Track record of capital deployment: raised growth capital and deployed it efficiently
- Evidence of learning from failure: founders acknowledge setbacks and articulate what was learned
- Industry recognition: speaking engagements, published thought leadership, network credibility

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Founders new to the industry or sector with no direct operating experience
- CEO without prior P&L ownership or team leadership at scale
- Founding team with gaps in critical functional areas (no CFO or financial discipline if capital-intensive; no CTO if highly technical)
- No articulated connection between prior experience and current opportunity
- Previous ventures that failed or were wound down with unclear reasons
- Founders still operating earlier ventures or maintaining day jobs outside the startup
- No evidence of industry network or relationships that provide unfair advantage
- Resume gaps or unexplained career transitions

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the CEO's specific prior operating experience in this domain or adjacent sectors?
2. Has the founding team successfully navigated scaling challenges before (Series A to B, B to C, etc.)?
3. What is each founder's functional expertise, and are critical gaps mitigated by hires or advisors?
4. How many funded ventures have the founders previously exited? At what outcome and over what timeline?
5. Does the team have "battle scars"—evidence of navigating downturns, competitive pressure, or operational crises?
6. What specific relationships or networks from prior experience do they bring to this venture?
7. Have founders previously worked together? If yes, what was the outcome?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- LinkedIn profiles with complete employment history and endorsements from prior peers
- Specific examples of prior accomplishments with metrics (e.g., "grew revenue from $2M to $15M in 3 years," "recruited and scaled engineering team from 3 to 25")
- Evidence of staying current with the domain: certifications, board memberships, advisor roles
- References from prior board members, investors, or colleagues that speak to execution capability
- Narrative explanation of why this founding team is uniquely positioned for this problem
- Clear articulation of how prior experience directly applies to current venture stage and challenges

## Module 2: Functional Coverage

### Definition
Assessment of whether critical business functions are staffed at appropriate seniority and capability to support current stage and near-term growth plans. For early-stage startups, this may be founders wearing multiple hats; for growth-stage companies, this requires dedicated leadership in finance, commercial, product, and operations.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- CEO/Founder Role: Clear separation of duties; CEO focused on strategy, capital, key accounts, not day-to-day execution
- CTO/VP Engineering: Senior technologist responsible for technical roadmap, architecture, security, and engineering culture
- CFO/VP Finance: Individual with audit, SEC, or FP&A experience; able to manage cap table, fundraising, and financial control
- VP Sales or Commercial Lead: Prior success selling in the target segment with relevant relationships and pipeline
- VP Product: Product-market fit expertise; track record of shipping products and iteration velocity
- VP Operations: Operational discipline, process design, vendor management; especially critical in capital-intensive or regulated segments
- Each role filled by individual with 5+ years relevant experience at similar or larger-scale organizations
- Evidence of functional area performance (e.g., sales team hitting targets, engineering shipping on cadence, finance controls in place)
- Clear decision-making authority and accountability for each functional area

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- CEO attempting to simultaneously manage product, engineering, sales, and finances
- CFO role unfilled; financial statements prepared by accountant or consultant without regular review by founder
- VP Sales hired without prior relevant sector experience or track record
- Functional leaders with titles but no P&L accountability or span of control
- Org chart that does not align with stated go-to-market strategy (e.g., product-led growth model but no product leader)
- Recent high turnover in functional areas with unclear replacement plans
- Functions outsourced entirely to agencies or consultants with no internal accountability owner
- Title inflation: founders titled CEO, President, COO, and CTO simultaneously

### Key Assessment Questions
1. For each critical function (engineering, sales, finance, product, operations), who owns it and what is their prior background?
2. What is the span of control for each functional leader? Do they have direct reports and clear accountability?
3. If a function is missing, how is it being managed and when is it planned to be filled?
4. What are the performance metrics for each function, and is the leader accountable to them?
5. Are there documented processes, handbooks, or operating systems in place for each function?
6. What is the planned functional org structure for 18 and 36 months out?
7. How are compensation, promotion, and role progression decisions made for functional teams?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Organizational chart showing clear functional accountability (product, engineering, sales, operations, finance)
- Brief bios of each functional leader with 2-3 relevant prior roles and outcomes
- For unfilled critical functions, a clear 6-12 month hiring plan with role specifications and search timeline
- Evidence of functional performance: sales velocity, engineering deployment frequency, financial close timeline
- Documentation of functional responsibilities and decision rights
- Plan for how functions will evolve as company scales

## Module 3: Team Depth

### Definition
Assessment of bench strength below the leadership layer and risk of key-person dependency. Evaluate whether the organization has redundancy, development paths for emerging leaders, and resilience if a single critical person departs.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Engineering team with clear technical depth: senior engineers, mid-level architects, and junior developers on learning paths
- Sales organization with multiple hunters who have closed deals independently; not reliant on single relationship
- Product team with multiple product managers or owners; product decisions not made by single person
- Finance function with at least two people; CFO has controller or finance manager reporting in
- Evidence of mentorship and development: junior people being coached by seniors toward leadership roles
- Succession plans for critical roles; identified successor candidates for CEO, CTO, CFO, VP Sales
- Low turnover in high-performing teams; retention metrics above industry benchmarks
- Cross-functional projects where junior people lead and gain exposure to broader business
- Team members with cross-functional knowledge: not siloed to single function
- Org chart showing 3-4 levels of hierarchy with clear reporting lines

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Founder/CTO as only engineer, or all engineering capability dependent on one senior engineer
- Single sales person responsible for majority of pipeline and closed deals
- Product strategy determined solely by founder; no product management structure
- "Two-person finance": founder and outside accountant with no internal deputy
- High turnover: more than 20% annual voluntary attrition in key functions
- No documented succession plans or development paths
- Org chart with only 1-2 levels; founder reporting structure unclear
- Senior people operating in isolation; no cross-functional collaboration or knowledge sharing
- New hires with low retention: people joining but leaving within 12 months
- Critical relationships or vendor connections owned by single person

### Key Assessment Questions
1. Who would be the immediate successor if the CEO left? Is that person being actively developed?
2. In engineering, who would own technical direction if the CTO left? Can they articulate the roadmap?
3. In sales, what percentage of pipeline and closed deals depends on any single salesperson?
4. What is the voluntary attrition rate in the last 12 months for key functions?
5. Are there clear development plans for high-potential junior people?
6. Which key relationships (customers, partners, vendors) are owned by single individuals?
7. How is knowledge shared across the team? Are there regular communication forums (all-hands, skip-levels, cross-functional syncs)?
8. What is the ratio of managers to individual contributors? Is it aligned with stage and complexity?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Org chart showing multiple levels of management with clear reporting relationships
- For each leadership role, identify the identified successor (even if internal development)
- Turnover data: voluntary departures and tenure distribution for key functions
- Evidence of junior talent development: promotions, project leadership, training investments
- Cross-functional team compositions on major initiatives
- Retention mechanisms: vesting schedules, equity packages, team incentives
- Internal communication cadence: weekly syncs, monthly all-hands, quarterly planning

## Module 4: Advisory and Board Composition

### Definition
Strategic value delivered by board members and advisors beyond capital deployment. Assess depth and credibility of business, technology, and domain expertise; quality of relationships that can be leveraged for customer acquisition, partnerships, recruiting, or capital; and governance rigor.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Board members with recent operating experience at scale-stage companies in related sectors (not just investors or past entrepreneurs)
- At least one independent board member with audit, legal, or governance expertise
- Advisors with deep domain expertise, significant customer relationships, or technology credibility (e.g., prior CTO at large competitor)
- Advisors and board members who have made warm introductions to customers, partners, or subsequent investors
- Board meeting cadence of monthly for early-stage, quarterly for growth-stage companies
- Evidence of board influence on strategy: recorded advice that influenced major decisions
- Advisory board compensation is meaningful (equity grants with 4-year vesting) indicating serious engagement
- Board composition with diversity of background, demographics, and perspective
- Clear engagement model: advisors are accessible and responsive, not nominal
- Board materials prepared and reviewed in advance of meetings

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Board composed entirely of investors with no independent directors or operational expertise
- Advisors listed on website but unable to articulate specific advice given or relationships leveraged
- Board meetings infrequent or cancelled frequently; no documented minutes or action items
- Board members drawn entirely from founder's prior network at same company (homogenous backgrounds)
- Advisor equity grants with immediate cliff or expired vesting; not on cap table
- Advisors hired but never contacted; no evidence of engagement
- Board composition that underrepresents women, minorities, or perspectives outside founder's background
- No independent audit committee or finance/accounting expertise represented
- Board members with conflicting investments or business interests

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What specific decisions or pivots has the board influenced in the last 6 months?
2. Which board members or advisors have made introductions to customers, partners, or subsequent investors? Track the outcomes.
3. Does each board member or advisor have relevant domain expertise or relationship network for this stage?
4. How frequently does the board meet, and are minutes maintained?
5. What is the structure of board committees (audit, compensation, governance)?
6. Are there independent directors, or is the entire board composed of investors and founders?
7. What percentage of advisor equity is currently unvested, indicating ongoing engagement?
8. Who would founders call if they needed to recruit a VP Sales, find a new customer, or navigate a legal dispute?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Board and advisor roster with names, titles, affiliation, area of expertise, and equity grants
- For each board member/advisor, 2-3 sentence bio emphasizing relevant expertise or network
- Documented board meeting cadence and topics covered in recent meetings
- Specific examples of board/advisor influence: customer introductions made, strategic advice given, relationships leveraged
- Board composition diversity data: gender, age, racial, professional background diversity
- Governance documents: board charter, committee assignments, meeting schedule
- Evidence of advisor engagement: email exchanges, meeting notes, or customer/partner referrals attributed to advisor introductions

## Module 5: Organizational Structure

### Definition
Clarity of reporting lines, decision-making authority, and org design aligned with go-to-market strategy and current stage. Assess whether structure scales, whether decisions can be made quickly, and whether accountability is clear.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Org chart with clear reporting relationships (no ambiguity about who reports to whom)
- Decision rights documented: who decides pricing, product roadmap, hiring, customer contracts, financial strategy?
- Structure aligned with go-to-market model: if direct sales, dedicated VP Sales with sales team; if PLG, dedicated product and growth team
- Span of control appropriate to stage: early-stage CEO can manage 8-12 direct reports; growth-stage function heads manage 4-8
- Cross-functional teams: marketing reports to commercial leader, not separately; product roadmap influenced by sales and customer success
- Clear escalation paths: what decisions are made at function level, what escalate to leadership team, what to board?
- Flexibility in structure: willingness to reorganize as strategy evolves (e.g., from product-led to direct sales)
- Flat enough for speed: 3-4 levels of hierarchy for Series A/B stage; minimal handoffs and approval chains
- Remote-friendly structure: clear communication protocols, async decision-making, timezone considerations if distributed

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- No org chart or chart that contradicts actual reporting relationships
- Ambiguous ownership: two people report on same area, or nobody reports on critical function
- Org structure misaligned with go-to-market strategy: claiming direct sales model but no VP Sales
- Very deep org chart for stage: 5+ levels at Series A indicates over-management
- Functional silos: product doesn't talk to sales, sales doesn't talk to customer success
- Decision rights unclear: strategy made ad-hoc, contradictory decisions by different leaders
- Structure rigid: resistance to reorganizing when strategy changes
- No documented process for cross-functional decision-making
- Org chart not updated for recent hires or departures

### Key Assessment Questions
1. Describe the reporting structure: who reports to the CEO, and who does each of those report to?
2. For major decisions (pricing changes, product roadmap, major hires, customer contracts), who has authority and at what spend/impact level?
3. How are cross-functional decisions made (e.g., new feature tradeoff between sales requests and product vision)?
4. What is the maximum span of control for any individual? Is it appropriate to stage and complexity?
5. How is the org designed to scale? What's the planned structure at 2x headcount and 5x revenue?
6. What formal and informal communication structures exist (all-hands, leadership team meetings, guild meetings)?
7. Are there clear roles and responsibilities documented, or is the structure understood informally?
8. How quickly can the org reorganize if strategy pivots? Is there resistance or flexibility?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Current org chart clearly showing reporting relationships and team spans
- Document outlining decision rights: who approves what, at what spending threshold
- Go-to-market strategy document that justifies the current org structure
- Planned org structure at next 2 milestones (e.g., Series B, profitability)
- Description of how cross-functional teams or projects are structured
- All-hands or team communication cadence and documentation
- Roles and responsibilities document for each major function and team lead

## Module 6: Talent Strategy

### Definition
Deliberate approach to recruiting, compensation, retention, equity allocation, and career development. Assess whether the company has competitive talent acquisition processes, aligned incentives, and a clear plan to attract and retain the senior talent needed to execute the business plan.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Written talent and compensation strategy aligned with stage and capital raised
- Competitive compensation benchmarking: salary at 50th-75th percentile for stage; equity grants meaningful (0.1-1% for director-level)
- Structured recruiting: dedicated hiring, clear candidate specifications, interview process for senior roles
- Strong conversion rates on offers: 70%+ offer acceptance rate; not losing offers to competitors
- Equity vesting with meaningful cliffs: 4-year vest with 1-year cliff standard; all employees on same structure
- Clear career paths: documented progression from IC to lead to manager to director
- Retention bonuses for key people; equity refresh grants at vesting milestones
- Diversity and inclusion in hiring: diverse candidate pipelines; pay equity analysis
- Onboarding structure: documented process, mentorship, 30-60-90 day outcomes tracked
- Active alumni network or referral program; 25%+ of hires from employee referrals

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- No documented compensation philosophy or strategy
- Salaries significantly below market for stage; candidates losing to competitors on comp
- Inconsistent equity grants: different people in same role with vastly different packages
- Equity with short cliffs or unusual vesting: 2-year vest, no cliff, or immediate vesting (suggests desperation or lack of retention thinking)
- Ad-hoc recruiting: hiring through connections, not structured process
- Low offer acceptance rate; frequent candidate rejections late in process
- No career progression framework; people unclear how to advance
- High turnover in first 12 months for new hires; onboarding gaps
- No retention mechanisms; all equity vests early and people leave soon after
- Narrow hiring pipeline; not actively sourcing diverse candidates

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the compensation philosophy (market-competitive, premium, lean to grow)?
2. For a director-level role, what is the typical salary range and equity grant at this stage?
3. What is the offer acceptance rate for senior positions in the last 6 months?
4. What is the equity allocation strategy: what percentage of cap table is reserved for employees?
5. What is the employee referral rate? What percentage of hires are from existing employee networks?
6. How is equity refreshed or adjusted as value increases? Are early employees incentivized to stay?
7. What is the voluntary turnover rate? Where is attrition highest?
8. Are there documented career progression frameworks? How do ICs advance to leads and managers?
9. What diversity and inclusion goals are set for hiring?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Compensation philosophy document: salary ranges, equity strategy, total comp positioning relative to market
- Equity grant schedule: what percentage is granted to founders, employees, option pool, future grants?
- Sample offer letters or compensation packages for 2-3 levels (IC, lead, manager)
- Hiring plan: roles to be filled in next 12 months with seniority, comp range, and timeline
- Onboarding documentation: new hire checklist, 30-60-90 day frameworks, mentorship assignments
- Diversity data: hiring from past year broken down by role level and demographic categories
- Turnover analysis: separations by tenure, reason, and role; voluntary vs. involuntary
- Career progression framework: how does IC become lead, manager, director? What criteria?
- Referral program details: incentives, historical conversion rate

## Module 7: Cultural and Execution Evidence

### Definition
Tangible indicators of operational discipline, cultural cohesion, consistency in hitting milestones, and team stability. Culture is not just values statements; it is evidence of decision-making patterns, accountability, and how the organization navigates stress.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Consistent execution: hitting revenue targets, product milestones, hiring plans in 80%+ of quarters
- Documented planning cadence: quarterly OKRs, annual strategy, milestones mapped to funding
- Transparent communication: all-hands meetings (monthly or quarterly) with open discussion of wins and setbacks
- Accountability culture: misses are discussed openly, root causes identified, adjustments made
- Cross-functional collaboration: product, sales, and customer success aligned on goals and metrics
- Customer obsession: regular customer interaction by leaders, feedback loops to product, NPS or CSAT tracking
- Operational discipline: meetings have agendas and owners; decisions are documented; follow-up items are tracked
- Learning from failure: documented post-mortems on major setbacks; lessons captured
- Team cohesion: low drama departures; when people leave, they are positive about the experience; recruitment from competitors brings team members with them
- Speed of iteration: product shipped regularly, A/B tests run, customer feedback incorporated in sprint cycles
- Data-driven decision-making: metrics tracked, dashboards maintained, decisions informed by data not gut

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Missed revenue targets or product milestones in multiple quarters
- No documented planning process; strategy evolved ad-hoc
- Poor communication: team unaware of company direction or recent decisions
- Accountability absent: misses not discussed, same mistakes repeated
- Functional silos: sales and product misaligned on customer needs, marketing disconnected from sales pipeline
- Reactive to customer issues: no proactive customer development or feedback loops
- Meeting chaos: meetings overrun, decisions not captured, action items not tracked
- Failure hidden or blamed on external factors: no learning culture
- High-drama departures: people leave with bad feelings, departures kept quiet, remaining team demoralized
- Slow iteration: features take months to ship, customer feedback ignored, A/B testing not practiced
- Gut-driven decisions: "the CEO said so" rather than data-informed

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What percentage of quarterly goals were achieved in the past 4 quarters?
2. How is strategy set and communicated? What is the planning cadence (annual/quarterly)?
3. How are major decisions made and communicated? Are minutes/documentation kept?
4. How does the team respond to a missed milestone? What is the post-mortem process?
5. How frequently does the team interact with customers? Who collects feedback and how is it acted upon?
6. What metrics are tracked and reviewed regularly by leadership?
7. How are cross-functional conflicts resolved (sales vs. product, marketing vs. sales)?
8. What is the attrition rate and why do people leave? Are there patterns by role or tenure?
9. When was the last time a major decision was reversed based on new information?
10. How is the team rewarded for hitting goals? Are incentives aligned across functions?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- OKR documents from past 2-3 quarters showing goals and actual achievement rates
- Quarterly business review decks or strategy documents outlining goals and rationale
- Evidence of regular all-hands meetings: agendas, notes, attendee counts
- Customer interaction schedule: how often do product/engineering talk to customers?
- Major customer research or NPS tracking: data on satisfaction and engagement
- Product roadmap and shipping cadence: what was shipped in each month of past quarter?
- Metrics dashboard or scorecard showing key metrics tracked by leadership
- Cross-functional goal alignment: sales goals, product roadmap, customer success retention linked
- Examples of decisions made and then revisited based on new data
- Team feedback or engagement survey results

## Module 8: Identified Gaps

### Definition
Self-awareness of capability or structural gaps that currently exist, and credible plan and timeline to close them. Strong teams acknowledge gaps and have concrete plans to address them. Denial of gaps is a red flag.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Founders articulate specific skills they lack (e.g., "we are product and engineering founder-led; we need VP Sales in next 6 months")
- For each gap, a concrete hiring plan: role specification, search strategy, timeline, interim solution
- Realistic assessment: gaps acknowledged are actual gaps, not overclaimed (e.g., "we need all senior hires" suggests lack of realism)
- Interim coverage: while gap exists, a documented workaround (e.g., board advisor handling finance until CFO hired)
- Timeline reflects urgency: critical gaps being addressed immediately; less critical gaps have 6-12 month plans
- External validation: board or investors have confirmed the gap and the plan to address it
- Learning mindset: team recognizes where they are not experts and is actively developing or recruiting expertise
- Gaps do not suggest fundamental flaws: gaps in execution discipline are concerning; gaps in specific domain skills are normal and manageable

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Denial of obvious gaps: "we don't need a CFO," yet financial controls and projections are weak
- Vague gap acknowledgment: "we could use better sales management" with no plan or timeline
- Gaps that should have been filled by this stage: at Series B, still no dedicated financial person or CFO
- No interim solution: gap identified but nothing done to mitigate while permanent hire made
- Gap acknowledgment but no credible way to fill it: "we need Stanford PhD in chemistry" for 2-person early-stage biotech
- Founders dismissing feedback about gaps: investor or advisor suggests missing capability; founder pushes back
- Gaps in core competencies: founder saying they don't understand unit economics or customer LTV
- Overstated capabilities: founder claiming to handle 3-4 full-time functions when clearly stretched

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What skill gaps or structural gaps does the founding team acknowledge?
2. For each gap, what is the specific plan to close it? Who will be hired or recruited?
3. What is the timeline to fill each gap? Does timeline align with company milestones?
4. What is the interim solution while the permanent hire is being recruited?
5. Has the board confirmed that the gap is real and the plan is credible?
6. Are there advisors or interim contractors supporting the gap area while permanent hire is recruited?
7. What would be different if this gap were filled? How would it change execution or outcomes?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Specific statement of 2-3 key capability gaps (product leadership, financial control, GTM expertise, etc.)
- For each gap: role description, required background, timeline to hire, and interim coverage plan
- Evidence that gap is being actively addressed: recruiting outreach, advisor support, interim contractors
- Board or investor feedback validating that the gap is real
- Plan to onboard and integrate new hire once recruited
- Realistic assessment of how long gap will persist (not claiming gap will be filled in 2 months if it's hard to hire)

## Assessment Notes for Scoring Agent

Evaluate management and organizational capability across the eight modules above using the following systematic approach:

**Gather Evidence**
1. Review founder bios, LinkedIn profiles, and prior career histories. Cross-reference stated experience with verifiable employment and outcomes.
2. Map the actual org chart based on employee directory or stated reporting relationships. Compare to founder claims about structure.
3. Obtain commitment of the last 4 quarterly OKRs and track achievement rate. Anything below 70% suggests execution risk.
4. Request turnover data (voluntary departures by function) and calculate annual attrition rate. 20%+ is elevated; 5-10% is healthy for stage.
5. List all board members and advisors with their specific expertise, equity grants, and most recent contribution or introduction.
6. Request sample offer letters or compensation data to validate market competitiveness for role level and stage.
7. Examine board meeting minutes or notes from past 2-3 meetings to assess governance rigor and decision-making.
8. Solicit specific examples of how the team executed during setbacks, missed milestones, or market changes.

**Score Each Module**

For each of the eight modules, assign a risk rating:
- **Green (Strength)**: Strong evidence of capability, track record, or plan. Founder team confidence is justified. This area unlikely to be limiting factor in execution.
- **Yellow (Acceptable)**: Adequate evidence of capability or credible plan to build it. Minor gaps exist but are acknowledged and being addressed. Some execution risk remains but is manageable.
- **Red (Risk)**: Significant gaps in capability, experience, or evidence. Weak or absent plan to address. High probability this will constrain execution or require external rescue (board/investor intervention).

**Synthesize Domain Score**

Roll up individual module scores to derive overall Domain 6 score:
- Count Green modules. If 6+ of 8 modules are Green, the team is strong and can execute plan with reasonable probability.
- Count Yellow modules. 2-3 Yellow modules are normal and manageable; more than 3 Yellow indicates uneven team composition.
- Count Red modules. Even 1 Red module is concerning and should trigger deep-dive. 2+ Red modules suggest team may not be ready for the amount of capital being raised.

**Red Flags**
- Founder team with no relevant domain experience
- CEO without prior P&L ownership or scaling experience
- CFO or VP Finance role unfilled at Series B or later
- High turnover (>25%) in key functions in past 12 months
- Board composed entirely of investors; no independent expertise
- Denial of obvious capability gaps
- Org chart with conflicting information or major functions unowned

**Green Flags**
- Founder team with successful exits (IPO, acquisition at >$100M valuation)
- CEO with prior experience at unicorn-scale company
- Functional leaders hired from obvious competitors or category leaders
- Healthy voluntary attrition (5-10%); long tenure of core team
- Strong evidence of hitting milestones and executing disciplined plan
- Board members who have made warm customer introductions or meaningful strategic input
- Self-aware identification of gaps with concrete hiring plans in flight

## Cross-Domain Dependency Notes

**Domain 6 feeds into:**
- **Domain 7 (Financial Performance)**: Strength of finance and operational leadership directly impacts accuracy and credibility of financial projections, ability to manage burn rate, and likelihood of hitting milestones.
- **Domain 8 (Risk Profile)**: People Risk is directly informed by team depth, succession planning, and attrition rates assessed in Domain 6. Execution Risk correlates with track record and discipline.
- **Domain 9 (Capital Structure)**: Investor confidence and valuation often correlate with founder pedigree and track record. Use of proceeds and execution timeline is driven by team capability.
- **Domain 10 (Legal/Governance)**: Board composition (Domain 4) feeds into overall governance framework assessed in Domain 10.

**Domain 6 depends on:**
- **Domain 7**: Financial management and control are inputs to assessing talent strategy and compensation competitiveness (Module 6).
- **Domain 10**: Cap table and equity allocation (Domain 10) inform equity strategy and retention mechanisms discussed in Module 6.
