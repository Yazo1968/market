# Domain 2: Solution and Product Assessment Reference

## Domain Overview

The Solution and Product domain evaluates the completeness, feasibility, and viability of the proposed solution. This domain assesses whether the startup has a clear product strategy, realistic understanding of the technical challenge, and a compelling value proposition tailored to customer needs. A strong product assessment demonstrates that the team understands what to build, has mapped a realistic development roadmap, and has anticipated key technical, regulatory, and competitive challenges.

Product excellence is necessary but not sufficient for market success. A mediocre product in a large, favorable market can still generate revenue; an excellent product in a weak market will struggle. However, product gaps, unclear value propositions, or unrealistic technical timelines will derail execution and consume investor capital. Your assessment here evaluates the team's execution capability and product-market fit risk.

---

## Module 1: Solution Description

### Definition
Clear, articulate description of what the startup is building—the core features, capabilities, and user experience of the proposed product. A strong solution description explains not just what features the product has, but how those features translate to customer value and how the user experience enables that value delivery.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Clear feature description**: The submission describes the core features of the product in concrete terms. Example: "The product automates invoice-to-cash workflows by: (1) extracting invoice data using OCR, (2) matching invoices to purchase orders and receipts, (3) flagging discrepancies for manual review, (4) initiating ACH payment transfers post-approval."
- **User experience clarity**: The submission describes how customers interact with the product—the interface, workflows, and decision points. Example: "Users access the platform via web dashboard. Accounts payable teams configure matching rules once; thereafter, the system processes 95% of invoices automatically, flagging exceptions for manual review with <2 minute review time per exception."
- **Scope clarity**: The submission is explicit about what the product does and does not do. Example: "The product handles invoice processing and payment initiation; it does not manage general ledger integration or AR functions."
- **Customer-centric framing**: Features are described in terms of customer value, not technical implementation. Rather than "uses ML-based NLP for field extraction," the submission says "reduces manual data entry time by 90% through intelligent field recognition."
- **Differentiated approach**: The description articulates how the product's approach differs from competitors. Example: "Unlike competitors that require rule-based configuration, our solution learns matching patterns from historical data, reducing setup time from weeks to days."
- **Version/roadmap clarity**: The submission distinguishes between what is built now (MVP or current product), what is near-term (next 6–12 months), and what is longer-term (18+ months).

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Vague or generic product description**: "A platform that helps businesses be more efficient" without specificity on features or capabilities.
- **Technical jargon without translation to value**: "Uses advanced ML algorithms" without explaining what problems this solves or how it benefits users.
- **Feature creep or unclear scope**: The product is described as addressing 10+ different use cases or customer problems; scope is unfocused.
- **No differentiation in approach**: The description could apply to any competitor in the space; there is no articulation of unique approach.
- **User experience unclear**: How customers interact with the product is not described; it's unclear whether the UX is web, mobile, API, integration, or something else.
- **Unrealistic scope for stage**: An early-stage startup claims to have built a product with 50+ features; more likely the product is narrower and less complete.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What are the 3–5 core features or capabilities of the product? Describe each in plain language.
- How do customers interact with the product? Is it web dashboard, mobile app, API, integration with existing tools, or something else?
- What does the product do, and what is explicitly out of scope? Where are the boundaries?
- How does the product's approach differ from competitors? What is the differentiated mechanism?
- What version of the product exists today (MVP, beta, full commercial)? What features are planned for next 6–12 months?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Feature list** describing core capabilities in plain language, not technical jargon
- **User workflow description** showing how customers interact with the product and what decisions they make
- **Scope definition** explicitly stating what the product does and does not address
- **Differentiated approach** articulating what makes the product's mechanism or design unique vs. competitors
- **Product stage clarity** distinguishing between current version and planned features
- **Value translation** connecting features to customer benefits (time savings, cost reduction, risk mitigation, etc.)

## Module 2: Value Proposition

### Definition
Clear articulation of the customer benefit the product delivers. A strong value proposition translates features into customer outcomes and articulates the compelling reason why a customer should pay for this product versus the status quo (current workaround or alternative solution). Value propositions may be economic (cost reduction, revenue increase), operational (time savings, efficiency), strategic (capability, risk reduction), or some combination.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Customer-centric framing**: The value proposition is articulated from the customer's perspective and in their language. Example: "Reduces accounts payable costs by 40% while eliminating invoice processing errors" rather than "Uses machine learning for invoice processing."
- **Quantified benefit**: The value proposition includes quantifiable benefits when possible. Example: "Reduces time-to-hire by 30 days (from 60 days to 30 days), enabling earlier productivity and reducing recruitment costs by $15K per hire."
- **Multiple value vectors**: The value proposition includes more than one category of benefit. Example: "Reduces content moderation costs (efficiency), improves customer safety (risk mitigation), and enables 24/7 moderation (scalability)."
- **Specificity to customer segment**: Different customer segments may value different benefits; the submission acknowledges segment-specific value when relevant. Example: "For small agencies, primary value is cost reduction (from $5K/month to $1K/month); for enterprise, primary value is compliance and audit trail."
- **Clear ROI linkage**: The value proposition connects to tangible ROI that customers can model. Example: "If CFO spends 20% of time on close process (40 hours/month at $150/hour loaded cost = $6K/month), and our product reduces this to 5 hours/month, ROI is 5x on annual software cost of $15K."
- **Differentiation from alternatives**: The submission explains why this product delivers better value than competitors or the status quo. Example: "Delivers 90% accuracy at 50% of competitors' cost because we use [specific approach]."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Feature-focused rather than benefit-focused**: "The product has invoice automation" without articulating customer benefit (cost savings, time savings, etc.).
- **Unquantified benefits**: "Saves time" or "Improves efficiency" without quantification.
- **Generic or vague value**: "Helps businesses work better" or "Improves collaboration" without specificity on how or for whom.
- **Value not validated with customers**: The value proposition is asserted without evidence that customers recognize or prioritize this benefit.
- **Over-promised value**: Claimed benefits are implausible relative to the problem. Example: "Reduces sales cycle from 6 months to 1 week" (unrealistic) or "Increases revenue by 500%" (overstated).
- **No ROI clarity**: It's unclear how customers would calculate ROI or what the payback period would be.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the primary customer benefit (cost reduction, time savings, revenue increase, risk mitigation, capability)? Quantify it.
- Does the claimed benefit align with what customers say is their priority pain? (Cross-reference to market research.)
- How does the value proposition differ from competitors or the status quo? Why would a customer switch?
- Can a customer calculate ROI based on the value proposition? What is the payback period?
- Are there segment-specific value propositions, or does the value proposition apply uniformly?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Primary value proposition** articulated in customer language with quantified benefit
- **Multiple value vectors** (cost, time, quality, risk, capability) when applicable
- **Segment-specific positioning** if different customers value different benefits
- **ROI modeling** showing how customers can calculate return on investment
- **Competitive or alternative comparison** showing why this product delivers better value than alternatives
- **Customer validation** that customers recognize and prioritize this benefit
- **Payback period** and break-even timeline for customer investment

## Module 3: Development Stage

### Definition
Candid assessment of the product development stage—what exists, what is under development, and what is planned. Accurate representation of development stage is critical for assessing execution risk and capital requirements. Misrepresentation of development stage is a significant credibility risk.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Honest stage labeling**: The submission uses standard stage terminology accurately. (Pre-product/Conceptual → Prototype/MVP → Beta → General Availability → Mature.) The stage matches the evidence.
- **Feature completion clarity**: For beta or GA products, the submission explicitly lists which features are complete, which are in development, and which are planned. Example: "Core invoice processing and payment initiation are GA; multi-entity consolidation is in beta; GL integration is planned for Q3 2025."
- **Customer usage evidence**: For products claimed to be beyond MVP, there is evidence of real customer usage—not just internal use or friends-and-family beta. Example: "5 customers in beta, processing 50K invoices/month cumulatively, with average product utilization of 70%."
- **Quality and stability indicators**: Beta or GA products include evidence of quality—uptime metrics, defect rates, customer NPS, or similar. Example: "99.9% uptime in beta; average response time <500ms; 3 critical issues identified and resolved in first month of beta."
- **Known limitations disclosed**: The submission discloses known limitations, which builds credibility. Example: "Current version supports English-language invoices; non-Latin character support is planned for Q2 2025."
- **Realistic timeline estimates**: For in-development features, timelines are specific and realistic. Example: "GL integration is estimated at 8 engineer-weeks; we have bandwidth available Q2 2025."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Vague stage labeling**: Product is claimed to be "ready to go live" but upon closer inspection is incomplete or untested with real customers.
- **Missing feature detail**: The submission does not clearly itemize which features are complete vs. planned.
- **No customer usage evidence**: Beta or GA products are claimed without evidence of real customer usage or adoption.
- **No quality/stability data**: No uptime, defect rate, or quality metrics are provided.
- **Omitted limitations**: The submission does not disclose known limitations or edge cases, implying the product is more complete than it actually is.
- **Unrealistic timeline estimates**: Estimated timelines for major features are optimistic; estimates lack engineering credibility.
- **Feature scope creep in timeline**: Claimed near-term features are numerous (20+ features in next 6 months); implausible given team size.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the honest development stage of the product? Is it MVP, beta, or GA? What evidence supports this claim?
- Which core features are fully complete and in production? Which are in development? Which are planned?
- If beta or beyond, how many real customers are using the product? What is the usage volume and frequency?
- What quality/stability metrics exist (uptime, defect rate, customer satisfaction, NPS)? What are the numbers?
- What are the known limitations or edge cases not yet addressed?
- For in-development features, what is the realistic timeline and resource requirement?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Clear stage labeling** (Conceptual, MVP, Beta, GA) with supporting evidence
- **Feature completion matrix** showing which features are complete, in development, and planned
- **Customer usage data** (number of beta customers, usage volume, feature adoption, session frequency) for beta+ products
- **Quality metrics** (uptime, latency, defect rate, customer satisfaction, NPS) for beta+ products
- **Known limitations list** demonstrating transparency about what's not yet complete
- **Timeline estimates** for major in-development features with engineering credibility
- **Product demo or screenshot** showing actual product UI and user experience

## Module 4: Technology Architecture

### Definition
Assessment of the technical architecture underlying the product—the technology choices, scalability approach, infrastructure, and dependencies. A strong technology architecture is appropriate for the problem, scalable to expected customer volumes, and uses proven technologies rather than unproven approaches.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Architecture described at appropriate level**: The submission describes the key technical components and how they interact without unnecessary complexity. Example: "Web frontend (React) communicates via REST API (Node.js/Express) to a microservices backend. OCR processing (Tesseract) runs asynchronously; matching logic uses rule engine (Drools); data is persisted in PostgreSQL with S3 for document storage."
- **Technology choices justified**: Key technology choices are justified by problem requirements, not by "coolness" or team familiarity. Example: "We chose PostgreSQL over NoSQL because transaction consistency and ACID guarantees are critical for accounting workflows; the schema is highly relational."
- **Scalability and performance**: The submission articulates how the system will scale to expected customer volumes. Example: "Current architecture handles 1M invoices/month on $2K/month infrastructure (4 EC2 instances + RDS). We've modeled to 100M invoices/month and identified that queue-based async processing and database partitioning will enable 50x scaling at proportional cost."
- **Infrastructure approach**: The submission is explicit about infrastructure (cloud vs. on-premise, hosting provider, deployment model). Example: "We deploy on AWS; customers access via SaaS web portal. All customer data is encrypted at rest and in transit."
- **Known technical risks disclosed**: For cutting-edge or unproven technical approaches, the submission acknowledges risks. Example: "We're using [new ML model] for field extraction; current accuracy is 88%; we have contingency plan to fall back to rule-based extraction if accuracy does not improve to 95% by June."
- **Third-party dependencies managed**: The submission identifies critical third-party dependencies and mitigation plans. Example: "We depend on [OCR vendor] for document processing; we have evaluated fallback providers and could transition in 4 weeks if needed."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **No architecture described**: The submission provides no technical architecture details, making it difficult to assess feasibility or scalability.
- **Over-engineered or unnecessary complexity**: The architecture uses cutting-edge or overly complex technology without justification.
- **Unproven technologies**: The approach relies on new, unproven technologies without fallback or contingency.
- **Scalability not addressed**: No discussion of how the product will scale to 10x or 100x customer volume.
- **Single points of failure**: The architecture has critical dependencies without redundancy or fallback plans.
- **Infrastructure costs not modeled**: No discussion of how infrastructure costs will scale with customer volume.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is the high-level technical architecture? What are the key components and how do they interact?
- Why were the primary technologies chosen (programming languages, databases, frameworks)? Are choices justified by problem requirements or just team preference?
- How will the system scale to 10x, 100x current customer volume? What are the scaling limits and how will they be addressed?
- What is the infrastructure approach (cloud, on-premise, hybrid)? What are the hosting costs per customer?
- What are the critical third-party dependencies? What is the fallback plan if a critical dependency becomes unavailable?
- What are the known technical risks? How will these be mitigated or tested?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Architecture diagram or description** showing key components and interactions
- **Technology stack list** with justification for key choices (languages, databases, frameworks, libraries)
- **Scalability analysis** showing how system scales with customer volume, with cost modeling
- **Infrastructure approach** (cloud provider, hosting model, data residency, security)
- **Performance metrics** (latency, throughput, uptime targets) and how these will be achieved
- **Third-party dependency list** with criticality assessment and fallback plans
- **Technical risk assessment** identifying unproven approaches and mitigation plans

## Module 5: Intellectual Property

### Definition
Assessment of intellectual property protections—patents, trade secrets, proprietary technology, data advantage, or defensible technology moats. Strong IP assessment acknowledges what is defensible and what is not; weak assessment oversells IP or ignores non-defensible aspects.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Honest IP assessment**: The submission is candid about what is actually defensible IP vs. what is not. Example: "Our core matching algorithm is proprietary; however, most of the value comes from domain expertise in accounting rules and invoice formats, which competitors can eventually build."
- **Patent strategy articulated**: If patents are relevant, the submission describes filing strategy and estimated defensibility. Example: "We have filed provisional patents on [specific innovations]; we plan to file full patents in Q2 2025 once technology is stabilized."
- **Trade secret protection plan**: For algorithms or processes that are defensible as trade secrets, the submission describes how these will be protected. Example: "Our training dataset and feature engineering pipeline are core IP; we maintain strict access controls and non-disclosure agreements with all employees."
- **Data advantage acknowledged**: If the product's defensibility depends on data, the submission articulates how data will be accumulated and protected. Example: "Each customer deployment builds our training dataset; after 50 customers, we will have historical invoices representing $2B in transactions, enabling unsupervised learning that competitors lack."
- **Defensibility timeline**: The submission acknowledges that IP advantage may be temporary. Example: "Our current NLP approach provides 18–24 month advantage before competitors can replicate; our longer-term defensibility depends on data moat and brand/customer switching costs."
- **Documentation and protection**: The submission describes policies for documenting and protecting IP (NDAs, code repositories, access controls, etc.).

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Overselling IP**: The startup claims strong patent protection or defensibility without basis. "Our patent will prevent competitors from entering" is rarely accurate.
- **No IP strategy**: The submission does not describe patents, trade secrets, or how IP will be protected.
- **Conflating implementation with innovation**: The team assumes that their specific implementation is defensible IP when the underlying approach is not novel.
- **Ignoring non-defensible aspects**: The submission focuses on defensible IP while ignoring that much of the value is replicable (UI/UX, integrations, customer service).
- **No timeline for IP advantage**: No acknowledgment of how long the IP advantage will persist.
- **Overconfidence in secrecy**: Assumes trade secrets will remain secret indefinitely; no plan for reverse-engineering or employee departures.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What is truly defensible IP in this product? Patents, trade secrets, data advantage, brand, customer lock-in, or something else?
- Are there filed or planned patents? What is the scope and defensibility?
- For trade secrets, how will these be protected (access controls, NDAs, employee agreements)?
- If defensibility depends on data, how will the data advantage be built and protected?
- How long will the IP advantage persist before competitors can replicate?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **IP portfolio assessment** identifying patents (issued, pending, or planned), trade secrets, and other defensible IP
- **Patent filing strategy** with technology description and estimated scope
- **Data advantage analysis** if applicable, showing how data will be accumulated and used
- **Protection mechanisms** (NDAs, code security, access controls, employee agreements)
- **Defensibility timeline** acknowledging how long the advantage will persist
- **Competitor replication analysis** identifying which aspects are defensible and which are replicable

## Module 6: Product Roadmap

### Definition
Clear articulation of the planned evolution of the product over the next 12–24 months. A strong product roadmap is grounded in customer feedback, prioritizes features by customer impact and business value, and is realistic given team size and resource constraints.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Sequenced prioritization**: Features are sequenced by priority (typically 6-month horizons). Example: "Phase 1 (Months 1–6): Core invoice processing + GL integration. Phase 2 (Months 7–12): Multi-entity support + API access for integrations. Phase 3 (Months 13–18): Audit trail and compliance reporting."
- **Customer-driven roadmap**: Features are prioritized based on customer feedback, not just team ideas. Example: "Top-3 requests from beta customers are: [1] GL integration (5/5 customers requested), [2] audit trail (4/5), [3] multi-entity support (3/5)."
- **Business-value linkage**: Roadmap features are connected to business goals. Example: "GL integration is the top-requested feature; customers indicate this is deal-closing feature for 60% of evaluators; shipping this feature will accelerate sales cycle by 2 months and reduce churn."
- **Resource realism**: The roadmap is achievable given current and planned team size. Example: "Months 1–6 roadmap requires 4 engineers and 1 PM; we have 3 engineers now and are hiring 2 more."
- **Risk and contingency**: The roadmap acknowledges technical risks and includes contingency. Example: "GL integration depends on successful completion of data sync module (currently 50% done); if this slips, Phase 1 timeline extends to Month 8."
- **Milestone clarity**: Roadmap includes specific milestones (GA, specific feature launch) with target dates. Example: "Beta launch Q2 2025; GA launch Q4 2025; API release Q2 2026."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Unrealistic scope for timeline**: Roadmap includes 50+ features in 12 months for a small team.
- **Not customer-driven**: Roadmap appears to be based on team ideas or guesses rather than customer feedback.
- **No prioritization rationale**: Features are listed but no justification is provided for why they're prioritized in this order.
- **Unrealistic timelines**: Estimated timelines lack engineering credibility.
- **No risk or contingency**: Assumes everything will go according to plan; no acknowledgment of risks or slippage.
- **Disconnected from business goals**: Roadmap features don't clearly link to business goals or customer outcomes.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What are the top 3–5 features planned for the next 12 months? In what sequence and why?
- What customer feedback drove these roadmap priorities? How many customers requested each feature?
- What is the business value of each roadmap feature? (Does it enable upsell, reduce churn, accelerate sales, expand addressable market?)
- Is this roadmap achievable given current team size? If not, what hiring is needed?
- What technical risks could delay the roadmap? What are contingency plans?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Phased roadmap** with timeline (6–12 months out typical) and feature sequencing
- **Customer feedback** justifying prioritization (feature request frequency, customer impact, blocking deals)
- **Business value articulation** for each major feature (upsell, churn reduction, sales acceleration, market expansion)
- **Resource plan** showing engineering capacity and any needed hiring
- **Risk and contingency** identifying technical risks and mitigation plans
- **Milestone calendar** with target dates for beta, GA, and major feature releases

## Module 7: Defensibility Assessment

### Definition
Evaluation of how defensible the product is against competitive threats and commoditization risk. A defensible product has barriers to competitive entry or feature replication; a commoditized product is easily replicated by better-capitalized competitors. Defensibility may come from IP, network effects, customer lock-in, brand, data advantage, or the nature of the product's positioning.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Multi-dimensional defensibility**: The product has defensibility across multiple dimensions (not just one). Example: "We have IP (algorithm), data advantage (historical invoices), customer lock-in (tight integrations), and brand (strongest NPS in category)."
- **Specific barriers articulated**: The submission identifies concrete barriers to competitive entry. Example: "Our data advantage requires 50 customer deployments and 18 months to build equivalent historical dataset; our customer switching cost is high due to integrations with customer's ERP; our brand/NPS provides 30% discount advantage vs. new entrant."
- **Defensibility timeline**: The submission acknowledges that defensibility evolves over time. Example: "In years 1–2, defensibility is primarily from team expertise and early customer relationships; by year 3–4, defensibility is from data advantage and customer lock-in; by year 5, commoditization risk increases and defensibility depends on brand and service."
- **Competitive response anticipated**: The submission acknowledges how competitors may respond and why this response is limited. Example: "Large incumbent vendors could build this capability, but cannot do so without cannibalizing existing revenue; greenfield players could enter, but lack the domain expertise; so our 18-month window allows us to build strong customer base and data advantage."
- **Commoditization risk acknowledged**: The submission is realistic about long-term commoditization risk. Example: "By year 5–7, core invoice processing will likely commoditize; we must evolve the product to higher-value services (process optimization, predictive analytics, workflow intelligence) to maintain defensibility."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Assumption of permanent defensibility**: The submission implies the product will remain defensible indefinitely against any competitor.
- **Single dimension of defensibility**: Defensibility depends entirely on one factor (e.g., only IP, or only customer lock-in), which is fragile.
- **No competitive response analysis**: Does not acknowledge how incumbents or new entrants could replicate the product.
- **Ignoring commoditization risk**: Does not acknowledge that many software products tend toward commoditization over time.
- **Vague defensibility claims**: "Our technology is proprietary" without articulating what specifically is proprietary and why competitors cannot replicate it.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What are the 2–3 primary barriers to competitive entry or feature replication?
- How long will these barriers persist? What is the timeline for competitive convergence?
- What could a well-capitalized competitor do to negate the startup's defensibility?
- How does defensibility change as the product matures (year 1 vs. year 5)?
- What must the startup do to evolve the product and maintain defensibility as it commoditizes?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Multi-dimensional defensibility assessment** (IP, data, lock-in, brand, team, positioning)
- **Specific barriers to entry** with timeline (how long until a competitor could replicate?)
- **Competitive response analysis** showing how incumbents or new entrants would compete
- **Defensibility evolution** showing how defensibility changes as the product matures
- **Commoditization risk** acknowledging that defensibility may erode and planning for evolution
- **Investment in defensibility** (hiring data scientists, building lock-in features, brand building)

## Module 8: Regulatory and Compliance Profile

### Definition
Assessment of regulatory, compliance, and legal requirements applicable to the product. Products in regulated industries (financial services, healthcare, security) face additional complexity, longer sales cycles, and ongoing compliance obligations. Products in unregulated categories may still face important compliance considerations (data privacy, accessibility, consumer protection).

### What Good Looks Like

- **Regulatory landscape mapped**: The submission identifies all applicable regulations (SOC 2, HIPAA, GDPR, PCI-DSS, industry-specific regulations, accessibility requirements, etc.) and assesses applicability.
- **Compliance roadmap articulated**: The submission outlines the roadmap for achieving compliance, with realistic timelines. Example: "SOC 2 Type II audit planned for Q4 2025; HIPAA compliance planned for Q2 2026; estimated cost is $150K for audit and compliance engineering."
- **Compliance cost modeled**: The submission explicitly models the cost of compliance (legal, audit, engineering, ongoing monitoring). Example: "Initial compliance cost: $150K; ongoing annual cost: $30K."
- **Compliance as sales blocker or enabler**: The submission assesses whether compliance is a sales blocker or enabler. Example: "40% of enterprise prospects require SOC 2 before signing; compliance certification will eliminate this blocker."
- **Customer indemnification**: The submission acknowledges and plans for customer indemnification, insurance, or liability management. Example: "We will carry cyber liability insurance and include standard indemnification in customer agreements."
- **Regulatory change planning**: For heavily regulated domains, the submission addresses how regulatory changes will be monitored and incorporated. Example: "We have regulatory counsel monitoring EU AI Act developments; we plan to assess impact Q2 2025."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Regulatory landscape ignored**: The submission does not identify or acknowledge relevant regulations or compliance requirements.
- **Compliance assumed to be easy or free**: "We'll be SOC 2 compliant" without realistic understanding of effort or cost.
- **No compliance roadmap**: The submission does not articulate when compliance will be achieved or the cost.
- **Compliance not considered for sales**: Does not acknowledge that lack of compliance will block enterprise sales.
- **Liability and indemnification not addressed**: No discussion of how the startup will manage liability or indemnify customers.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What regulations or compliance standards are applicable (SOC 2, HIPAA, GDPR, PCI-DSS, industry-specific, accessibility)?
- What is the timeline for achieving each compliance certification? What is the estimated cost?
- Will lack of compliance be a sales blocker for target customers? (For enterprise SaaS, SOC 2 is typically table-stakes.)
- What are the ongoing compliance obligations and costs (annual audits, monitoring, updates)?
- How will the startup manage customer indemnification and liability?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Regulatory landscape assessment** identifying all applicable regulations and compliance requirements
- **Compliance roadmap** with timelines and cost estimates for each certification
- **Sales impact analysis** showing which compliance certifications are sale-blockers vs. nice-to-have
- **Compliance cost model** (initial and ongoing) built into financial projections
- **Liability and indemnification** plan, including insurance coverage and customer contract language
- **Regulatory monitoring** for heavily regulated domains (AI, healthcare, finance, security)

## Cross-Domain Dependencies

The Solution and Product domain feeds into and is informed by:

- **Domain 1 (Market and Opportunity)**: Market insights and customer validation inform product requirements and prioritization. Competitive positioning claims must be validated by actual product capabilities.
- **Domain 3 (Business Model)**: Product architecture and feature set drive unit economics (CAC, LTV, pricing power). Compliance requirements affect cost structure.
- **Domain 4 (Go-to-Market)**: Product capabilities and positioning drive messaging, sales processes, and channel strategy.
- **Domain 5 (Traction)**: Product metrics (engagement, NPS, churn) validate that the product delivers promised value.

Conversely, these domains inform product assessment:

- **Traction**: Early customer feedback and NPS validate that the product delivers value.
- **Business Model**: Unit economics constraints may require simplifying the product roadmap or narrowing scope.
- **Go-to-Market**: Sales and support limitations may require simplifying product complexity.

---

## Assessment Notes for the Scoring Agent

When assessing the Solution and Product domain:

1. **Validate product existence**: For products claimed to be beyond MVP, verify that real customers are using it. Internal use or friends-and-family beta is not sufficient evidence of a viable product.

2. **Assess value proposition credibility**: Does the claimed value proposition align with customer priorities from market research? If market research shows customers care about X, but the product focuses on Y, that's a risk signal.

3. **Check development stage honesty**: Misrepresentation of development stage is a major credibility risk. If the startup claims "GA" but features are incomplete or untested, downgrade the assessment significantly.

4. **Evaluate technical feasibility**: Does the technical architecture support the claimed product roadmap and customer volumes? Are technology choices justified?

5. **Assess execution risk**: Is the product roadmap achievable given team size and skill set? Do product timelines align with capital runway? (If you've run out of money before shipping, the product fails.)

6. **Weigh defensibility realistically**: Most software products eventually commoditize. Is the startup planning for evolution beyond the initial defensible product?

7. **Assess compliance planning**: For regulated domains, is compliance being treated seriously? Regulatory slippage is a common failure mode.

8. **Cross-check with traction**: Early product adoption, NPS, and customer feedback should align with the product assessment. If customers are churning or report low NPS, the product assessment should be skeptical.

---

## Summary: Good Solution and Product Assessment

A strong Solution and Product assessment demonstrates:

- **Clear, differentiated product** with specific features and user experience that translate to customer value
- **Honest development stage** validated by real customer usage (if claimed to be beyond MVP)
- **Realistic technical architecture** that is scalable and uses proven technologies
- **Viable defensibility** through multiple dimensions (IP, data, lock-in, brand, positioning)
- **Customer-driven product roadmap** that is realistic given team and capital constraints
- **Compliance planning** that treats regulatory/legal requirements seriously

Product excellence is necessary but not sufficient. Pair this assessment with market opportunity, business model, and traction to form a complete picture of startup viability.
