# Domain 10: Legal, Compliance, and Corporate Governance

## Domain Overview

Legal, Compliance, and Corporate Governance assesses the company's legal entity structure, ownership clarity, contractual obligations, intellectual property ownership, litigation exposure, regulatory compliance, and corporate governance infrastructure. This domain is foundational because legal issues can derail transactions, render assets unenforceable, trigger liability, or create operational constraints.

Investors and lenders require legal diligence to ensure they are acquiring clean, enforceable equity and that material liabilities don't exist. Defective incorporation documents, founder disputes, unclaimed IP, litigation, or regulatory violations can materially reduce enterprise value and create transaction risk. Strong governance infrastructure also supports scalability and investor confidence.

This domain directly influences:
- Confidence that equity ownership is clear, enforceable, and not subject to third-party claims
- Likelihood of transaction closing without litigation or challenge
- Regulatory risk and compliance burden
- Data security and privacy obligations
- Access to capital (many investors won't invest if legal issues exist)

## Module 1: Corporate Structure

### Definition
Documentation of all legal entities that comprise the company, including jurisdiction of incorporation, ownership chain, holding structures, and any subsidiaries or branches. Corporate structure must be clear and properly documented so that an investor acquiring equity knows exactly what assets and liabilities they own.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Single entity company: straightforward Delaware C-corporation or similar, with clean incorporation
- Incorporation documents filed: articles of incorporation, bylaws, and any amendments current and compliant with state law
- Registered agent appointed: Delaware registered agent (or equivalent) current and in good standing
- No complex structures: if Delaware holding company structure, rationale clear and unnecessary complexity avoided
- All prior entity conversions documented: if company converted from LLC to C-corp, conversion properly effectuated
- Subsidiary structure clear: if foreign subsidiaries, purpose and ownership structure documented
- No dormant entities: all subsidiaries active and accounted for; no abandoned entities creating liability
- Corporate governance current: board resolutions, shareholder approvals, and major decisions properly documented
- Tax ID and licenses: EIN and all necessary business licenses obtained and current
- No structural defects: company not operating without proper incorporation or with expired filings

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Multiple entities with unclear purpose: company has 3-4 entities but purpose and relationship unclear
- Complex structure: holding company, operating company, subsidiary structure with no clear rationale
- Incorporation documents outdated: bylaws from 5 years ago with no amendments; articles not reflecting current structure
- Registered agent missing or lapsed: Delaware registered agent slot unfilled or agent resigned; company not properly served
- Prior conversions incomplete: company was LLC, converted to C-corp, but conversion documents not filed or recorded
- Dormant subsidiaries: company has inactive subsidiaries that were created but never used; liability exposure unclear
- Tax ID issues: company unable to produce EIN; or multiple EINs for single entity
- Missing licenses: business licenses not obtained or are expired in key jurisdictions
- Structural defects: company operating in California but incorporated in Nevada with no California qualification; potential tax exposure

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the legal entity through which the company operates?
2. In what jurisdiction is the company incorporated? Why that jurisdiction?
3. Are there any subsidiaries, branches, or other entities? What is their purpose and ownership structure?
4. Has the company undergone any prior conversions (LLC to C-corp, merger, etc.)? Are these properly documented?
5. What is the registered agent for the company? Is the agent current and in good standing?
6. What is the company's federal tax ID (EIN)? Does it match the incorporation?
7. What business licenses or permits are required for the company's operations? Are they current?
8. If the company operates in multiple jurisdictions, is it qualified to do business in each?
9. Are there any dormant or inactive subsidiaries? Why do they exist and are they creating liability?
10. When are the articles and bylaws next due for renewal or amendment?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Certificate of incorporation and bylaws: current articles and bylaws for main operating entity
- Incorporation location and registration: Delaware certification of good standing or equivalent from incorporation state
- Corporate amendments: documentation of any amendments to articles or bylaws with board resolutions
- Entity structure chart: diagram showing all entities, ownership relationships, and purpose
- EIN documentation: federal tax ID matching incorporation documents
- Business licenses: list of all licenses, permits, and registrations required and current status of each
- Registered agent designation: current designation of registered agent and proof of acceptance
- Subsidiary documentation: for any subsidiaries, incorporation documents and ownership structure
- Conversion documents: if any prior entity conversions, documentation that conversion was properly effectuated
- Tax status: state tax ID(s) and confirmation of tax filing status (not delinquent)

## Module 2: Ownership and Cap Table

### Definition
Complete and accurate capitalization table showing all equity ownership, including founder equity, employee options, and investor equity. Cap table must be current, mathematically sound, and reconcilable to corporate records (stock ledger, board resolutions).

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Cap table current and detailed: updated within 30 days, showing all shareholders with share counts and percentages
- Reconciliation to stock ledger: cap table reconciles to actual stock certificates and board resolutions
- Founder equity documented: founder equity purchase agreements with clear vesting schedules and strike prices
- Employee options documented: stock option plan with all grants recorded, including grant date, strike price, and vesting terms
- Fully-diluted calculation correct: cap table includes all options, warrants, and convertibles in fully-diluted calculation
- No mathematical errors: percentages add to 100%, shares reconcile to total issued, no duplicates or gaps
- Prior rounds documented: all previous funding rounds documented with investor names, investment amounts, and pricing
- Equity reserves cleared: option pool reserved shares are accounted for; no unexpected dilution from future grants
- Equity clawbacks addressed: if any equity forfeited from early departures, clawback properly recorded
- Clean audit trail: cap table can be traced back to issuances with documentation (board resolutions, purchase agreements)

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Cap table out of date: last updated 6+ months ago; recent grants or departures not recorded
- Cap table incomplete: major shareholder missing, option pool size unclear, or fully-diluted calculation not done
- Mathematical inconsistencies: percentages don't add to 100%, share counts don't reconcile, or duplicates exist
- No stock ledger: company unable to produce list of shares issued with certificate numbers and holders
- Founder vesting unclear: founder equity fully vested with no cliff, or vesting schedule not documented
- Options not tracked: company unable to produce list of all option grants, exercises, or expirations
- Convertibles unrecorded: SAFEs, convertible notes, or other convertibles not reflected in cap table
- Warrant agreements: warrants outstanding but not recorded in cap table or fully-diluted calculation
- Equity clawbacks: equity forfeited from early departures not documented or not removed from cap table
- Prior round pricing: previous round price per share unclear, making subsequent financing calculation problematic

### Key Assessment Questions
1. Is the cap table current as of what date? When was it last updated?
2. How many total shares have been issued? To whom and at what price?
3. How many shares are reserved for the option pool? How many have been granted and exercised?
4. How many fully-diluted shares are there (including options and convertibles)?
5. What is the founder ownership percentage (fully-diluted)? What is the vesting schedule?
6. For each previous funding round, what was the price per share and how many shares were issued?
7. Are there any warrants or convertible instruments outstanding? How are they recorded in the cap table?
8. Have any shares been forfeited or clawed back? How are these recorded?
9. Does the cap table reconcile to the stock ledger and board resolutions?
10. What is the option strike price for each grant? How is strike price determined (FMV, 409A)?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Cap table spreadsheet: current and complete showing all shareholders, shares, percentages, vesting schedules
- Fully-diluted cap table: separate tab showing all options and convertibles included
- Stock ledger: detailed record of all share issuances with certificate numbers, holder, and date
- Founder stock purchase agreement: documentation of founder equity with vesting schedule and cliff
- Stock option plan: company's equity incentive plan document setting terms for options
- Option grants: documentation of all option grants with grant date, strike price, vesting terms, and grant amount
- Board resolutions: board resolutions approving equity grants and option strike prices (409A validation)
- Warrant agreements: for any warrant agreements, full terms and impact on fully-diluted cap table
- Convertible instruments: SAFE agreements, convertible notes, or other convertibles with terms and conversion mechanics
- Prior funding rounds: documentation (term sheets, stock purchase agreements) from previous funding rounds with pricing

## Module 3: Material Contracts

### Definition
Identification and summary of all material contracts that are essential to company operations or create significant obligations, including customer contracts, supplier agreements, partnership agreements, employment agreements, and licensing agreements.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Material contracts documented: list of all major contracts (customer, supplier, partner, employment) with parties, terms, and status
- Customer contracts clear: for top customers (>5% of revenue), contracts documented with revenue terms, termination provisions, and renewal status
- Supplier contracts clear: for critical suppliers, contracts documented with pricing, delivery terms, and termination provisions
- Partnership agreements: for key partnerships (channel, technology, integration), agreements documented with revenue sharing and term
- Employment agreements: offer letters and confidentiality/IP assignment agreements in place for key employees
- No undisclosed liabilities: review of contracts doesn't reveal hidden obligations or liabilities
- Terms acceptable: review of contracts doesn't reveal onerous terms that would constrain operations
- Renewal status tracked: contract renewal dates tracked; critical contracts being renewed or replaced
- Change of control provisions: contracts reviewed for change of control provisions that might be triggered by sale
- No personal guarantees: company not personally guaranteed by founders for corporate obligations

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Material contracts not documented: company unable to produce copies of key customer or supplier contracts
- Customer contracts vague: top customer contract terms unclear, or contract includes termination for convenience
- Supplier lock-in: supplier contract has very high termination costs or minimum volume requirements
- Partnership agreements missing: key partnerships exist but no written agreement or agreement is vague
- Key customer personal relationship: revenue dependent on relationship with individual customer contact with no contractual protection
- No employment agreements: key employees have no offer letter or IP assignment agreement
- Hidden liabilities: contracts reviewed and material obligations discovered (e.g., indemnification, earnout obligations)
- Onerous terms: customer contracts have price escalation provisions, vendor contracts have volume minimums
- Change of control triggers: contracts include change of control provisions that would require customer/supplier consent for sale
- Personal guarantees: founder has personally guaranteed company debt or customer contracts; personal liability exposure

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What are the top 5-10 material contracts for the company?
2. For top customers, what are the key contract terms (pricing, termination, renewal)?
3. For critical suppliers, what are the terms (pricing, delivery, termination)?
4. Are there any partnerships or channel agreements? What are the key terms?
5. Who has signed employment agreements? What are the key provisions (confidentiality, IP assignment)?
6. Are there any non-compete or non-solicitation agreements in place?
7. Are there any licenses or rights agreements (software, patents, data, IP)?
8. Do any contracts include change of control provisions that would be triggered by sale?
9. Do any contracts include indemnification or earn-out obligations?
10. Are there any personal guarantees by founders for company obligations?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Contracts matrix: list of all material contracts with party, type (customer, supplier, partner), key terms, and status
- Customer contracts: for top 5-10 customers, copies of contracts showing pricing, termination, and renewal terms
- Supplier contracts: for critical suppliers, copies showing pricing, delivery terms, and termination provisions
- Partnership agreements: agreements with key partners (technology, channel, integration) showing revenue sharing and term
- Employment agreements: offer letters, confidentiality agreements, and IP assignment agreements for key employees
- License agreements: software licenses, IP licenses, data licenses, or patent licenses with material terms
- Change of control summary: review of contracts for change of control provisions that could impede sale
- Indemnification and liability summary: summary of any indemnification obligations or liability caps in contracts
- Personal guarantee documentation: any personal guarantees by founders or identification that none exist

## Module 4: Intellectual Property Ownership

### Definition
Confirmation that all material intellectual property (patents, trademarks, copyrights, trade secrets, domain names) is owned by the company and not by founders or third parties. IP ownership is critical because products, brands, and technology are the core assets of the business.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- IP ownership documented: company has clear ownership of all material IP through assignment agreements or work-for-hire
- Founder IP assignment: all founders have assigned prior IP and agreed to assign future IP to company
- Employee IP assignment: all employees have signed agreements assigning IP created during employment to company
- Contractor IP assignment: all contractors have signed agreements assigning IP created for company to company
- Patents filed: patents filed in company name with company listed as applicant and inventor agreements signed
- Trademarks filed: key trademarks filed in company name with company as applicant
- Domain names owned: all critical domain names registered in company name (or with clear assignment)
- Copyright registrations: key copyrighted works (software, marketing materials) registered or covered by copyright notices
- Trade secrets protected: process, algorithm, or proprietary information protected by confidentiality and assignment agreements
- Licenses in place: all required licenses (open source, third-party IP) properly documented and compliant

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- IP ownership unclear: company unable to document who owns key patents or source code
- Founder IP not assigned: founders retain rights to pre-company IP or prior projects; not clearly assigned to company
- No IP assignment agreements: employees and contractors don't have IP assignment agreements
- Patents in wrong name: patents filed in founder personal name rather than company name; ownership unclear
- Open source compliance risk: company using open source without proper attribution or license compliance
- Third-party IP infringement: company using third-party IP without clear license or permission
- Domain names not owned: critical domain names registered in founder personal names, not company
- Copyright ownership unclear: key software or materials copyright unclear or not registered
- Trade secret protection weak: confidential information not protected by confidentiality agreements
- Contractor IP disputed: contractors claim ownership of IP created during engagement; potential dispute

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What is the company's core IP (patents, software, trademarks, domain names)?
2. Who owns the company's core IP? Is it documented that the company owns this IP?
3. Are there any founder IP assignments? Have all founders assigned prior IP to the company?
4. Do all employees have IP assignment agreements in their offer letters or employment agreements?
5. Do all contractors have IP assignment agreements before work is performed?
6. Are there any patent applications filed? In whose name (company or founder)?
7. Are there any trademark registrations? In the company name?
8. Does the company use any open source software? Is the company in compliance with license terms?
9. Are there any third-party licenses for IP used by the company (software, patents, data)?
10. Has the company's IP been reviewed by outside counsel for infringement or ownership issues?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- IP ownership documentation: assignment agreements showing company ownership of all material IP
- Founder IP assignment: assignment agreements from all founders assigning prior and future IP to company
- Employee IP assignment: sample employee offer letter or IP assignment agreement covering all employees
- Contractor IP assignment: copy of contractor agreement with IP assignment language
- Patent documentation: list of patent applications and issued patents with filing date, patent number, and status
- Trademark documentation: list of trademarks registered with registration date, registration number, and status
- Domain name documentation: list of critical domain names with registration and ownership confirmation
- Copyright documentation: copyright registration or copyright notice for key works
- Open source inventory: list of open source software used with license type and compliance confirmation
- IP infringement review: legal counsel opinion letter on IP ownership and non-infringement of third-party rights
- Third-party licenses: copies of material software, patent, or data licenses with key terms

## Module 5: Litigation and Contingent Liabilities

### Definition
Disclosure and assessment of any active or threatened litigation, including lawsuits, arbitrations, regulatory investigations, or contingent liabilities (guarantees, indemnities, disputed contracts) that could create financial or operational risk.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- No active litigation: company has no pending lawsuits, arbitrations, or regulatory investigations
- Clean history: company has had no prior material litigation
- Routine employment matters: only routine employment claims or disputes that are immaterial and well-covered by insurance
- D&O insurance: appropriate director and officer liability insurance in place covering $2M+ in potential liability
- General liability insurance: appropriate general liability and product liability insurance covering operational risks
- No personal guarantees: founders have not personally guaranteed company debt or obligations
- No indemnities: company has not guaranteed or indemnified other parties for potential liabilities
- Contingent liabilities minimal: no contingent liabilities disclosed in financial statements; clean P&L and balance sheet
- No regulatory investigations: company has no history of regulatory investigations or enforcement actions
- Clean background: comprehensive background check on company and founders shows no material issues

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Active litigation: company has pending lawsuits with material financial exposure (>$100K)
- Litigation history: company has had multiple lawsuits or arbitrations, suggesting operational or customer relations issues
- Employment litigation: employment claims pending, suggesting human resources or culture issues
- IP infringement suit: company being sued for patent, trademark, or copyright infringement
- Customer disputes: customer litigation or regulatory complaints from customers
- Regulatory investigation: company under investigation by SEC, FTC, EPA, or other regulator
- Personal guarantees: founder has personally guaranteed company debt; personal liability exposure
- Indemnities: company has indemnified customer, partner, or investor for potential liabilities
- Insurance gaps: company under-insured (D&O coverage <$1M, general liability gaps)
- Contingent liabilities material: financial statements disclose material contingent liabilities or off-balance-sheet obligations

### Key Assessment Questions
1. Are there any pending lawsuits or arbitrations? If yes, what is the nature and potential financial exposure?
2. Has the company been sued in the past 5 years? What was the outcome?
3. Are there any regulatory investigations or inquiries pending? If yes, what is the status and potential exposure?
4. Are there any employment disputes or claims pending?
5. Are there any customer complaints or disputes?
6. Has the company been involved in IP infringement disputes (as plaintiff or defendant)?
7. Does the company have D&O insurance? What is the coverage amount and deductible?
8. Does the company have general liability insurance? What is the coverage amount and deductible?
9. Are there any personal guarantees by founders? If yes, for what obligations?
10. Are there any material contingent liabilities disclosed in the financial statements?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Litigation summary: list of any pending or threatened litigation with description, damages alleged, and status
- Settlement documents: copies of any settlement agreements for prior disputes
- Insurance certificates: proof of D&O, general liability, and other key insurance with coverage amounts and terms
- Regulatory correspondence: any correspondence with regulatory agencies (SEC, FTC, state regulators) regarding investigations
- Employment disputes: summary of any employment claims, complaints, or disputes
- Customer disputes: summary of customer complaints or disputes
- IP disputes: summary of any IP infringement claims (as plaintiff or defendant)
- Personal guarantee documentation: any personal guarantees by founders with copy of obligation
- Indemnification documentation: any indemnification agreements with customers, partners, or investors
- Clean background check: background check on company and principals showing no material issues
- Contingent liability summary: disclosure of any contingent liabilities or off-balance-sheet obligations

## Module 6: Regulatory Compliance Status

### Definition
Assessment of compliance with applicable laws and regulations, including licenses, permits, certifications, and ongoing compliance obligations. Regulatory compliance varies by industry (healthcare, finance, privacy, data protection, environmental) and jurisdiction.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Licenses and permits in place: all required licenses, permits, and registrations obtained and current
- License renewal process managed: renewal dates tracked and renewals completed before expiration
- Compliance roadmap clear: if company operates in regulated industry, compliance requirements identified and plan in place
- Certifications obtained: relevant certifications (SOC 2, ISO 27001, HIPAA, PCI-DSS, etc.) obtained or on timeline
- Compliance officer designated: if needed by complexity or size, compliance officer or counsel overseeing compliance
- No regulatory violations: company has not received any regulatory violations, fines, or enforcement actions
- Regulatory counsel engaged: company has retained regulatory counsel to advise on compliance obligations
- Compliance budget allocated: sufficient resources (budget, staff) allocated to compliance
- Regulatory filings current: SEC filings, state filings, tax filings all current and accurate
- Environmental compliance: if applicable, environmental permits and compliance status current

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- Material licenses missing: company operating without required licenses or permits
- Expired licenses: licenses have expired and have not been renewed; company operating without current licenses
- Compliance obligations unclear: company uncertain what regulations apply and what is required
- No certifications: relevant certifications (SOC 2, HIPAA) not obtained and no timeline to obtain
- Regulatory violations: company has received fines, cease-and-desist orders, or enforcement action
- Compliance violations hidden: compliance issues discovered during due diligence not disclosed
- No regulatory counsel: company relying on internal resources without outside regulatory expertise
- Compliance budget inadequate: compliance is afterthought, not allocated adequate resources
- Regulatory filings delinquent: SEC, state, or tax filings late or inaccurate
- Environmental violations: environmental fines or violations, especially in manufacturing or chemical companies

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What industry/jurisdiction regulations apply to the company's business?
2. What licenses, permits, and registrations are required? Are all current?
3. When are license renewals due? What is the renewal process and cost?
4. What compliance certifications apply (SOC 2, ISO, HIPAA, PCI-DSS, GDPR, CCPA)?
5. Are required certifications obtained or on timeline to obtain?
6. What is the timeline to compliance if certifications not yet obtained?
7. Has the company had any regulatory violations or enforcement actions?
8. Is a compliance officer or counsel overseeing regulatory obligations?
9. What is the process for tracking and managing regulatory changes?
10. If data privacy (GDPR, CCPA), what is the company's compliance status?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Licenses and permits matrix: list of all required licenses with status (current, renewal date, cost)
- Regulatory roadmap: if regulated industry, documentation of compliance requirements and timeline
- Certifications status: list of relevant certifications with status (obtained, in-progress, timeline)
- Regulatory counsel: identification of outside counsel advising on compliance
- Compliance function: description of compliance team, processes, and budget allocation
- Regulatory filings: evidence of timely filing of required regulatory reports
- Environmental compliance: if applicable, environmental permits and compliance status
- Data privacy compliance: GDPR, CCPA, and other privacy law compliance status
- Quality assurance: if applicable, quality or audit certifications and audits
- Regulatory correspondence: any correspondence with regulators regarding compliance inquiries or issues

## Module 7: Governance Structure

### Definition
Formalization of governance including board composition, committee structure, decision-making framework, and corporate policies. Governance infrastructure supports scalability and demonstrates organizational maturity.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Board charter: written board charter documenting board responsibilities, composition, and procedures
- Committee structure: if appropriate for stage, audit and compensation committees established with written charters
- Board meeting schedule: regular board meeting schedule with documented minutes
- Board resolutions: material decisions documented through board resolutions
- Shareholder agreement: if multiple shareholders, written shareholder agreement documenting rights and obligations
- Bylaws current: corporate bylaws current and reflect actual governance practices
- Related-party transaction policy: policy for handling potential conflicts of interest or related-party transactions
- Document retention policy: written policy for retention of corporate documents
- Code of conduct: written code of conduct or ethics policy for employees
- Whistleblower policy: mechanism for reporting compliance violations or ethical concerns

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- No board charter: board responsibilities and procedures not documented; governance ad-hoc
- No committees: if Series B+, audit and compensation committees not established
- Board meetings infrequent: board meets less than quarterly; decisions not documented
- Board resolutions absent: major decisions made informally without board resolutions
- No shareholder agreement: multiple shareholders with no written agreement governing rights
- Bylaws outdated: bylaws from incorporation, not updated to reflect current governance
- No governance policies: no written policies on conflicts, related-party transactions, or document retention
- Related-party transactions undisclosed: company involved in transactions with founders or investors without disclosure or governance
- No code of conduct: no written ethical guidelines for employees
- Governance informal: governance reliant on oral understandings between shareholders

### Key Assessment Questions
1. Is there a written board charter? What does it say about board composition and responsibilities?
2. How often does the board meet? Are minutes maintained?
3. Are there board committees? If yes, what are their composition and charters?
4. Is there a shareholder agreement? What does it cover?
5. Are corporate bylaws current? When were they last updated?
6. How are related-party transactions handled? Is there a disclosure and approval process?
7. Is there a code of conduct or ethics policy?
8. Is there a whistleblower or compliance hotline?
9. How are document retention and record-keeping handled?
10. What is the process for board compensation and expense reimbursement?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Board charter: written charter documenting board composition, responsibilities, and meeting procedures
- Board minutes: minutes from recent board meetings (past 2-3 quarters) showing decisions and attendance
- Committee charters: if committees exist, written charters for audit, compensation, and other committees
- Shareholder agreement: written agreement among shareholders documenting rights and governance
- Corporate bylaws: current bylaws reflecting actual governance practices
- Related-party transaction policy: policy for disclosure and approval of related-party transactions
- Code of conduct: written code of conduct or ethics policy for employees
- Document retention policy: written policy for retention of corporate records and documents
- Whistleblower policy: mechanism for reporting compliance violations
- Conflict of interest policy: policy for disclosure and management of conflicts of interest

## Module 8: Data Protection and Privacy

### Definition
Assessment of data privacy and security compliance, including GDPR (EU), CCPA (California), and equivalent regulations. Data protection is increasingly critical and violations can result in significant fines, operational disruption, or customer churn.

### Strong Evidence Looks Like
- Privacy policy in place: written privacy policy posted on website disclosing data collection and use
- Data processing agreements: if customer data handled, data processing agreements in place with customers
- GDPR compliance (if EU data): if collecting EU data, GDPR compliance framework in place (data minimization, consent, DPA)
- CCPA compliance (if California data): if collecting California resident data, CCPA compliance framework in place (opt-out, disclosure)
- Data security measures: documented security measures (encryption, access controls, audit trails)
- Security audit: third-party security audit or penetration test completed with results reviewed
- Data breach protocol: incident response plan for data breaches with notification procedures
- Data retention policy: documented policy on how long data is retained before deletion
- Privacy by design: privacy considerations incorporated into product design, not added later
- No material breaches: no history of data breaches or privacy violations
- DPA signed with customers: for SaaS or service providers, data processing agreements signed with customers

### Weak or Absent Evidence Looks Like
- No privacy policy: company website has no privacy policy or policy is vague
- No data processing agreements: company collecting customer data with no written agreements
- GDPR non-compliance: company collects EU data but has not implemented GDPR safeguards
- CCPA non-compliance: company collects California data but has not implemented CCPA opt-out
- Security measures unclear: company unable to document security practices or encryption in place
- No security audit: no independent security audit; security practices undocumented
- Data breach history: company has had prior breaches or privacy violations
- Data retention unclear: company unclear on how long data is retained; no deletion process
- Privacy bolted on: privacy concerns addressed retroactively; not incorporated in product design
- Unencrypted sensitive data: sensitive customer or user data stored in plaintext or unencrypted
- No incident response plan: no documented process for responding to data breaches

### Key Assessment Questions
1. What customer and user data does the company collect?
2. Is a privacy policy published and what does it disclose?
3. If EU data collected, what GDPR safeguards are in place?
4. If California data collected, what CCPA safeguards are in place?
5. What data security measures are in place (encryption, access controls)?
6. Has the company conducted a security audit or penetration test?
7. Has the company had any data breaches? If yes, what was the scope and impact?
8. What is the company's data retention policy? How long is data retained?
9. What is the incident response plan for data breaches?
10. Are data processing agreements in place with customers and subprocessors?

### What to Look for in the Submission
- Privacy policy: published privacy policy disclosing data collection, use, and retention
- Data processing agreements: DPA template or executed DPAs with customers
- GDPR compliance documentation: if EU data collected, documentation of GDPR compliance (lawful basis for processing, DPA, privacy notice)
- CCPA compliance documentation: if California data collected, documentation of CCPA compliance (opt-out mechanism, privacy notice)
- Security audit report: third-party security audit or penetration test report with findings and remediation
- Security practices documentation: description of encryption, access controls, audit trails, and other security measures
- Incident response plan: documented plan for responding to data breaches with notification procedures
- Data retention policy: written policy describing how long data is retained and deletion process
- Subprocessor list: if customer data shared with subprocessors, list of subprocessors and notification procedure
- Breach history: confirmation that no material breaches have occurred or disclosure of any breaches and remediation
- Privacy impact assessment: if applicable, documentation of privacy-by-design approach

## Assessment Notes for Scoring Agent

Evaluate legal, compliance, and corporate governance systematically across the eight modules using this framework:

**Gather Evidence**

1. Request corporate documents: articles, bylaws, board resolutions from past 2+ years, shareholder agreements
2. Obtain cap table and stock ledger: trace cap table to board resolutions and stock certificates
3. Conduct contract review: request all material contracts and review for problematic terms or missing documentation
4. Perform IP audit: obtain list of patents, trademarks, domain names; verify company ownership through assignments
5. Identify litigation exposure: ask management about any disputes, claims, or regulatory issues; verify through court dockets
6. Assess regulatory compliance: document applicable regulations and confirm company compliance status (licenses, certifications, filings)
7. Review governance documentation: board minutes, shareholder agreements, governance policies
8. Data privacy assessment: document data collection practices and privacy/security compliance
9. Conduct background check: background check on company and founders
10. Engage outside counsel: consider hiring outside legal counsel to conduct comprehensive due diligence

**Score Each Module**

For each of the eight modules, assign a risk rating:
- **Green (Clean)**: No issues identified. Documentation is in place, in order, and current. No known defects, litigation, or compliance gaps. Company is well-organized legally and governance is strong.
- **Yellow (Minor Issues)**: Some issues identified but remediation is straightforward. Missing documentation can be obtained or prepared. Compliance gaps have clear path to resolution. Issues are not likely to impede transaction or operations.
- **Red (Material Issues)**: Significant issues that could impede transaction or create material liability. Examples: disputed IP ownership, active litigation with material exposure, regulatory violations, major compliance gaps, major corporate governance defects.

**Synthesize Domain Score**

Roll up individual module scores to derive overall Domain 10 score:
- Count Green modules. If 6+ of 8 modules are Green, legal and governance position is strong and clean.
- Count Yellow modules. 2-3 Yellow modules are normal and manageable; more than 3 Yellow indicates material work to remediate.
- Count Red modules. Even 1 Red module requires detailed investigation and mitigation plan. 2+ Red modules suggest transaction risk is too high without significant remediation.

**Red Flags**
- IP ownership disputed: founder or employee retains rights to critical IP; company doesn't own its core assets
- Active litigation material: pending lawsuits with >$500K exposure; litigation outcome uncertain
- Regulatory violations: company has received fines, cease-and-desist orders, or other enforcement action
- Regulatory non-compliance material: critical licenses expired or certifications not obtained; company operating without required authorization
- Personal guarantees: founders have personally guaranteed material company obligations; personal liability exposure
- Cap table defects: cap table incomplete, outdated, or inconsistent with stock ledger; ownership unclear
- Incorporation defects: company not properly incorporated, registered, or operating in unauthorized jurisdictions
- Data breach material: company has had material data breaches affecting customer confidentiality or regulatory compliance
- Governance absent: no board, shareholder agreements, or policies; governance entirely informal
- Undisclosed contingencies: material contingent liabilities or obligations discovered during diligence not previously disclosed

**Green Flags**
- IP ownership clean: all IP properly assigned to company; founder and employee IP assignment agreements signed
- No litigation: company has no pending litigation; clean litigation history
- Regulatory compliant: all required licenses, permits, and certifications current; no violations or enforcement actions
- Cap table clean and current: cap table is up-to-date, mathematically sound, reconciles to stock ledger
