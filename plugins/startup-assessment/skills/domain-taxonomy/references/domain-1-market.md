# Domain 1: Market and Opportunity Assessment Reference

## Domain Overview

The Market and Opportunity domain evaluates the attractiveness and viability of the market being targeted. This domain assesses whether the startup is chasing a real, significant, and addressable market opportunity with favorable timing and favorable competitive dynamics. Strong market assessment demonstrates that the founding team understands their customer, has validated demand, and is entering at a moment when structural conditions favor their solution.

This domain is foundational to all other domains—a compelling market opportunity justifies investment in product development and go-to-market execution. Conversely, a weak market opportunity makes even excellent execution insufficient. Your assessment here feeds directly into the company's potential upside ceiling and the reasonableness of revenue projections.

---

## Module 1: Problem Definition

### Definition
The specific market failure, customer pain point, or unmet need being addressed. A strong problem definition quantifies the impact of the problem and the scope of customers affected. It articulates why the problem exists, why existing solutions are inadequate, and what the cost of the problem is to customers (time, money, risk, friction).

### What Good Looks Like

- **Quantified impact**: The problem is translated into measurable business terms (revenue loss, cost waste, time inefficiency, safety risk). Example: "Sales teams spend 8 hours per week on manual data entry at an average loaded cost of $500/week per rep, affecting 45,000 enterprise sales reps globally ($1.17B TAM)."
- **Root cause analysis**: The submission identifies why the problem exists—is it technological, regulatory, behavioral, structural? Does the team understand the causal mechanism?
- **Proof of pain**: Evidence that customers actually experience and acknowledge the problem. This may include research interviews, survey data, or customer quotes demonstrating unprompted recognition of the pain.
- **Addressability**: The problem is clearly something that can be addressed through a product or service intervention, not an unsolvable structural problem.
- **Contrast to existing solutions**: The submission explicitly addresses why current alternatives (manual processes, competing products, workarounds) are inadequate—what is the gap?

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Assumed rather than validated**: The team asserts that a problem exists without evidence. "We believe sales teams waste time" without customer research.
- **Too narrow or too broad**: The problem is so specific that it affects only a handful of customers, or so vague that it's unclear what's actually being solved (e.g., "companies want to be more efficient").
- **Addressed by existing solutions**: The problem is not actually unmet. Customers have workarounds or competitive products that adequately address it, yet the submission doesn't acknowledge this.
- **Externally driven rather than customer-driven**: The team identifies a problem that sounds plausible but customers have not articulated as urgent. Example: "We're solving a problem that will matter once AI advances further."
- **Missing economic justification**: The problem is real but affects only low-value activities or has trivial impact ("saves 30 minutes per month for 10 people").

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- Can you point to specific customer research, interviews, or data proving customers recognize this problem unprompted?
- What is the estimated cost of the problem to customers in annual terms? How did the team calculate this?
- Why do existing solutions fail to address this adequately? What is the specific gap?
- How many potential customers face this problem, and what percentage would consider it urgent or high-priority?
- If the founding team solved this problem perfectly, what would change for the customer (revenue increase, cost reduction, time savings, risk reduction)?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Customer quotes or research summaries** demonstrating awareness and frustration with the problem
- **Quantified impact analysis** using bottom-up or industry data (e.g., "Average order value loss due to abandoned carts is $X, affecting Y merchants")
- **Explicit acknowledgment of existing solutions** and articulation of their gaps
- **Customer persona descriptions** that link specific personas to specific problems and their costs
- **Before/after scenarios** showing how customer workflows or economics change if the problem is solved

## Module 2: Market Sizing (TAM, SAM, SOM)

### Definition
Quantification of the total addressable market (TAM—the entire market opportunity assuming the company could serve every potential customer), serviceable addressable market (SAM—the portion of TAM the company can realistically target given geographic, segment, or product constraints), and serviceable obtainable market (SOM—the realistic revenue capture over a specific time horizon, typically 5–10 years). Sound market sizing combines top-down industry estimates with bottom-up validation from customer data.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Dual methodology**: The submission uses both top-down (industry reports, market research) and bottom-up (customer surveys, pricing research, adoption rate assumptions) approaches to triangulate market size. Numbers should converge or be reconciled explicitly.
- **Documented sourcing**: Market size claims include citations (e.g., "Gartner estimates the global CRM market at $45B in 2024"). Avoid unsourced figures.
- **Clear segmentation boundaries**: TAM/SAM/SOM are defined by segment, geography, use case, or customer type. Example: "TAM is mid-market manufacturing in North America (defined as 8,000 facilities with 100–1,000 employees). SAM is our initial focus on discrete manufacturing in automotive and aerospace verticals (2,200 facilities)."
- **Bottom-up validation**: Assumptions are validated through customer research. Example: "Surveys of 50 target customers show 78% would adopt a solution at our target price point, implying SOM of $45M by year 5 based on X acquisition rate."
- **Reasonable capture rates**: SOM reflects realistic market penetration (typically 5–15% of SAM by year 5 for venture-scale businesses), not fantasy capture rates (we'll get 50% of the market).
- **Sensitivity analysis**: The submission acknowledges key drivers of market size (pricing, adoption rate, customer expansion) and shows how changes in these assumptions affect outcomes.

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Single methodology**: Market size is derived only from top-down reports without any validation of the underlying assumptions. "Gartner says TAM is $50B" with no attempt to bottom-up validate this.
- **Unsourced numbers**: Market size claims appear without citation or methodology. "TAM is $10B" with no explanation of how this was calculated.
- **Inflated capture assumptions**: SOM assumes the company will capture 40% of SAM by year 5, or implies conversion rates inconsistent with industry norms.
- **No segmentation**: TAM/SAM/SOM are not clearly bounded. "Our market is every company" is not a market size; it's an evasion.
- **Circular logic**: Market size is justified by reference to competitor valuations or growth rates, rather than independent demand analysis.
- **Ignored heterogeneity**: Market size treats all customers as identical, when actual customer value, willingness to pay, and adoption readiness vary dramatically by segment.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- Can you identify the top-down market research sources cited (reports, analyst firms)? Are these credible and recent (within 3–5 years)?
- What bottom-up evidence supports the TAM/SAM/SOM? How many target customers were surveyed on pricing, willingness to pay, or adoption timeline?
- Is the SOM achievable given realistic conversion rates for comparable products in the space? For SaaS, is payback period and CAC:LTV ratio consistent with industry benchmarks?
- How sensitive is the market size to changes in key pricing or adoption assumptions? What happens if adoption is 50% of the team's estimate?
- Does the market size actually change meaningfully if the problem is solved? (E.g., for cost-reduction solutions, is the savings substantial enough that customers will pay?)

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Explicit TAM/SAM/SOM figures** with definitions of what each includes (geography, segments, customer types, price points)
- **Top-down sourcing** (analyst reports, industry research) with proper citations and dates
- **Bottom-up research** (customer surveys, pricing studies, adoption benchmarking) summarizing methodology and sample size
- **Segment-level breakdown** showing TAM/SAM/SOM across different customer personas or verticals
- **5-year revenue forecast** with explicit assumptions on market penetration, pricing, and customer expansion
- **Sensitivity table** showing TAM/SAM/SOM under different assumptions (high/medium/low case)

## Module 3: Market Dynamics

### Definition
Assessment of the underlying trends, forces, and structural conditions driving market growth and attractiveness. Market dynamics include secular growth drivers (e.g., digitalization, regulatory mandates, demographic shifts), adoption curves and competitive intensity, regulatory tailwinds or headwinds, and the shape of demand growth (linear, exponential, constrained by adoption friction).

### What Good Looks Like

- **Secular trend identification**: The submission identifies 2–3 major trends creating tailwinds. Examples: "Increasing regulatory pressure to automate compliance (EU regulations mandate real-time reporting by 2025), growing enterprise data volume driving need for analytics, and consolidation in the industry increasing operational scrutiny."
- **Timing alignment**: The company's entry window is justified by secular trends or inflection points. "Digital transformation spend increased 40% post-pandemic, creating expanded budgets for solutions in this category."
- **Adoption curve analysis**: The submission articulates where the market is in its adoption S-curve (early, growth, mature) and why this matters. "The market is in early growth phase (5% adoption of target customer base), offering a 3–5 year window of high growth before commoditization."
- **Competitive dynamics**: Market dynamics explain why competitive intensity is favorable or unfavorable. "High barriers to entry (capital intensity, regulatory approval) keep competitor count limited, allowing for premium pricing and customer stickiness."
- **Regulatory environment**: Headwinds and tailwinds are explicitly assessed. "Upcoming data privacy regulation will require customers to invest in compliant infrastructure, creating demand for our solution."
- **Customer behavior shifts**: Evidence of changing customer behavior or willingness to pay. "78% of target customers have increased budget allocation to this category YoY; vendor consolidation is accelerating, favoring vendors with integrated solutions."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Assumed trends without evidence**: "This market is growing fast" without citing growth rates, analyst forecasts, or behavior change data.
- **Backward-facing narrative**: Market dynamics are explained through past growth without forward-looking thesis. "The market has grown 20% YoY for the past 3 years" without articulation of what will drive future growth.
- **Ignoring headwinds**: The submission identifies tailwinds but ignores or minimizes headwinds. Incumbent competition, customer switching costs, regulatory obstacles, or adoption friction are glossed over.
- **Conflation of trends with causation**: Correlation is treated as evidence of causal impact. "AI growth + our AI solution = market is huge" without demonstrating causal link to demand for this specific solution.
- **Vague or generic trends**: "Digitalization" or "AI is growing" without translating this into specific impact on target customers' needs or budgets.
- **Timing thesis absent**: The submission does not explain why this moment is favorable versus 3 years ago or 3 years from now.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What are the 2–3 primary secular trends supporting demand growth? Can you cite growth rates or analyst forecasts for these trends?
- Where is this market in its adoption curve? Is the early-mover window closing or just opening?
- What regulatory, technological, or structural changes are creating tailwinds or headwinds for this category?
- How sensitive is market growth to macroeconomic conditions? (E.g., does recession eliminate demand?)
- Are there substitutes or adjacent categories that are displacing demand from this market?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Explicit identification** of 2–3 macro trends with citations (analyst reports, economic data, regulatory calendars)
- **Adoption curve position** with evidence (e.g., % of TAM that has adopted competitive solutions, adoption pace in early customer base)
- **Regulatory landscape assessment** identifying relevant regulations and their effective dates
- **Competitive intensity analysis** showing barriers to entry, incumbent response likelihood, and fragmentation vs. consolidation dynamics
- **Demand elasticity analysis** showing customer sensitivity to price, value, and economic conditions
- **Quantified growth rate** (CAGRs for the market and subsegments) with sourcing and time horizon

## Module 4: Timing Thesis

### Definition
The argument for why this specific moment in time represents a viable or optimal entry window for the startup's solution. A strong timing thesis explains why the problem exists now (as opposed to previously), why customers are ready to adopt the solution now (versus later), and why competitive intensity is favorable now.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Inflection point identification**: The submission identifies a specific inflection point or threshold that has recently been crossed. Examples: "AI model accuracy recently exceeded 90% in this use case (Q4 2024), enabling practical enterprise applications"; "Regulatory deadline (2025) created a compliance investment cycle"; "Customer technology stack maturity reached X, enabling integration with our solution."
- **Customer readiness evidence**: Data or research showing customers are actively seeking solutions now. "Customer surveys show 62% of target customers are in active evaluation phase now vs. 18% 12 months ago."
- **Competitive window justification**: The submission explains why competitive response is delayed or unlikely. "Incumbent vendors are locked into a different architecture, creating a 18–24 month window before they can credibly compete."
- **Macroeconomic alignment**: Market conditions are favorable. "Post-layoff environment has increased procurement scrutiny for efficiency-improving solutions, expanding our addressable market."
- **Technology maturity**: Technology or infrastructure underlying the solution has reached critical maturity. "GPU costs decreased 40% in the past 18 months, making real-time processing economically feasible."
- **Explicitness about risk**: The submission acknowledges alternative timing scenarios. "If adoption proceeds slower than historical comparable (SaaS adoption in 2010), the market window may extend 2–3 years; if faster adoption (similar to cloud migration post-2008), the window compresses."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Vague or post-hoc timing arguments**: "The time is right" without specific evidence of inflection points or readiness.
- **Timing argument conflates market growth with entry timing**: "The market is growing 30% YoY" does not explain why this is the right moment to enter; markets can grow for years without being ripe for a specific entrant.
- **Ignores competitive response timing**: The submission assumes incumbents will not or cannot respond, without evidence.
- **Circular reasoning**: Timing is justified by the company's readiness ("We're ready now") rather than market readiness.
- **No window boundaries**: The submission does not articulate when the window closes or what triggers window closure.
- **Assumes market freeze**: The submission implies that if the company does not enter now, the opportunity will not re-emerge; rarely is this true in dynamic markets.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What specific inflection point or threshold has recently been crossed that enables this opportunity now?
- What evidence shows customers are actively seeking solutions to this problem now (vs. would seek them later anyway)?
- How long is the competitive window before incumbents or adjacent competitors can credibly compete? What assumptions underpin this?
- What could accelerate or delay the window opening/closing? (Regulatory changes, technology maturation, competitive moves, macroeconomic shifts)
- If the company enters 12 months later, how would outcomes differ? What would be lost vs. gained?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Specific inflection point** with date and quantified threshold crossed (e.g., "Regulatory deadline: January 2025"; "Technology benchmark achieved: Model accuracy >90%")
- **Customer readiness data** showing purchasing intent, budget allocation, or evaluation activity timing
- **Competitive response timeline** with assumptions about incumbent technology maturity, road map, and organizational agility
- **Macroeconomic indicators** if relevant (budget cycles, procurement trends, hiring/retention changes affecting customer priorities)
- **Window closure analysis** identifying what triggers closure and timeline for closure
- **Scenario analysis** showing outcomes under early-entry, optimal-entry, and late-entry scenarios

## Module 5: Competitive Landscape

### Definition
Comprehensive mapping of direct competitors (products solving the same problem for the same customer), indirect competitors (products solving the problem differently or for adjacent customers), substitutes (workarounds or alternative approaches), and white space (underserved segments or use cases where competitive intensity is lower).

### What Good Looks Like

- **Exhaustive competitor list**: The submission identifies all plausible competitors, including established vendors, adjacent vendors, and emerging startups. A mature market should list 10+ competitors; a nascent market 3–5.
- **Multidimensional mapping**: Competitors are evaluated across 4–5 dimensions (price, feature set, target segment, go-to-market approach, technology approach). Example: Feature matrix showing which competitors address ease of use, customization, real-time processing, etc.
- **Strength/weakness analysis**: For each significant competitor, the submission articulates specific strengths (why customers choose them) and weaknesses (unmet customer needs, technology gaps, go-to-market blindspots).
- **Substitute identification**: The submission identifies non-product solutions, workarounds, or adjacent categories that serve the same underlying need. Example: "For spend management, the primary substitute is manual spreadsheet-based tracking and Excel plugins."
- **Market share or positioning data**: Estimates of competitor scale (revenue, customers, market share) based on crunchbase, G2, analyst reports, or customer research.
- **White space articulation**: The submission identifies underserved segments, use cases, or customer needs not addressed by existing competitors. Example: "Competitors focus on large enterprise; SMB segment is underserved."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Competitor denial**: The submission ignores or dismisses relevant competitors. "Our main competitors are just three vendors" when 10+ plausible competitors exist.
- **Vague competitive advantage**: Competitors are described generically ("They're expensive," "They're not user-friendly") without specific evidence or articulation of what the startup does better and why customers will switch.
- **Ignoring indirect competitors and substitutes**: The submission focuses on direct competitors but misses that customers are actually using spreadsheets, hiring consulting services, or using adjacent products to solve the problem.
- **No market share or traction data**: Competitors are listed but no evidence of their scale or market presence is provided.
- **No white space**: The submission does not articulate where competitive intensity is lower or where unmet needs exist. Implies the startup will compete head-to-head with entrenched incumbents.
- **Assumption of permanent differentiation**: The submission asserts differentiation without explaining why competitors cannot replicate it or why the startup's advantage is durable.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- Can you list all direct competitors solving this problem for this customer? How many exist? What are their names and approximate scale?
- For the 3–5 largest/most-relevant competitors, what are their strengths and weaknesses relative to the startup's planned solution?
- What is the primary substitute customers currently use (spreadsheets, manual processes, adjacent products)? What is the cost and friction of the current solution?
- Are there underserved segments (company size, geography, use case, customer type) where competitive intensity is lower?
- What are the top 2–3 reasons customers might choose a competitor over this startup's solution? Can the startup address these?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Comprehensive competitor list** with company names, approximate revenue/customers if available, and primary product focus
- **Feature/positioning matrix** showing how the startup and key competitors map across pricing, features, target segments, or technology approach
- **Strength/weakness assessment** for 3–5 primary competitors, with specific evidence
- **Substitute analysis** identifying non-product solutions customers currently use (spreadsheets, manual processes, outsourced services, adjacent products)
- **White space mapping** showing segments, use cases, or customer needs where competitive intensity is lower
- **Customer research evidence** (G2 reviews, customer interviews, NPS data) showing why customers choose incumbents and what they would change

## Module 6: Competitive Positioning

### Definition
The basis of competitive differentiation—what the startup will do better than competitors and why that differentiation is durable and valuable to customers. Strong competitive positioning is grounded in the startup's specific capabilities or approach and articulates a causal link to customer value.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Specific differentiation**: The startup articulates 2–3 specific ways it will compete differently. Examples: "We enable real-time processing at 10x lower cost than competitors by leveraging edge computing rather than cloud"; "We focus on the mid-market SMB segment where competitors have abandoned support, allowing us to charge 30% less and achieve higher NPS."
- **Durable differentiation**: The submission explains why competitors cannot easily replicate the differentiation. This may be due to: technology/architecture advantage, organizational focus/culture, network effects, customer lock-in, or cost structure advantage rooted in the business model.
- **Customer value linkage**: The submission connects the differentiation to specific customer outcomes. Example: "Lower cost (our differentiation) translates to better unit economics for the customer, enabling expansion to mid-market segment (customer value), which competitors cannot address profitably."
- **Competitive response considerations**: The submission acknowledges that competitors may respond and articulates barriers to response. Example: "Competitors own large installed bases requiring expensive migration off legacy infrastructure; our greenfield approach gives us cost advantage that competitors cannot match without cannibalizing existing revenue."
- **Segment-specific positioning**: The submission may articulate that positioning differs by segment. Example: "For enterprise, we compete on integration/customization; for SMB, on simplicity and cost."
- **Evidence of differentiation**: The submission provides evidence of differentiation through beta customer feedback, product demos, pilot results, or technical specifications.

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Generic or easily replicated differentiation**: "We're easier to use" or "We have better customer service" without evidence or explanation of why competitors cannot match this.
- **Differentiation not valued by customers**: The startup has differentiated on a feature or approach that customer research shows is not a primary decision driver.
- **Assumed rather than demonstrated**: The submission asserts differentiation without evidence from customer feedback, product tests, or competitive assessments.
- **Differentiating on the wrong dimension**: Competitors may have moved on from the dimension where the startup is claiming advantage. Example: "Our integration is better" when the competitive battleground has shifted to AI/ML capabilities.
- **No explanation of durability**: The submission does not explain why differentiation is defensible or how quickly competitors could match.
- **Price-only positioning**: The startup's only differentiation is a lower price point, which is easily matched by competitors with scale economies.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What are the 2–3 primary ways the startup will compete differently from incumbents? Which is most defensible and most valuable to customers?
- Why can't competitors easily replicate this differentiation? Is it rooted in technology, business model, organizational capability, or customer lock-in?
- How does the differentiation translate to customer value? (Cost savings, time savings, risk reduction, strategic capability, etc.)
- What evidence from customer research, product testing, or pilots validates that this differentiation is valued and motivates purchase decisions?
- How might competitors respond to the startup's entry? What is the timeline for competitive response, and what barriers exist to rapid replication?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Clear articulation** of 2–3 specific differentiation points with concrete examples
- **Durability rationale** explaining why differentiation is defensible (technology lock-in, business model advantage, organizational capability, network effects, customer lock-in)
- **Customer value translation** linking differentiation to specific outcomes customers care about (cost, time, quality, risk, capability)
- **Evidence of differentiation** from customer feedback, product specs, pilot results, or technical benchmarking
- **Competitive response analysis** showing how long differentiation advantage persists and what triggers competitive convergence
- **Segment-level positioning** if applicable, showing how positioning varies by customer type or use case

## Module 7: Customer Segmentation

### Definition
Clear definition of target customer segments—the specific customer types, personas, or verticals the startup will prioritize. Effective segmentation breaks down customers by behavior, decision-making profile, willingness to pay, adoption readiness, and value drivers. The startup should articulate which segments it will target first and why.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Multi-dimensional segmentation**: Customers are segmented not just by company size or vertical, but by behavior, decision-making profile, and economics. Example: "Primary segment: Mid-market manufacturers (200–2,000 employees) with discrete production processes, high procurement scrutiny, and budget authority decentralized to operations leaders."
- **Persona development**: For each segment, the startup articulates a realistic customer persona including: title, budget, decision-making authority, primary pain points, alternative solutions in use, willingness to pay, and adoption timeline.
- **Segment prioritization**: The startup explicitly prioritizes which segments to target first and why. Example: "Segment A (small private companies) has highest pain intensity and fastest decision cycles; we'll target this first to achieve quick wins and references, then expand to Segment B (enterprise) where deal cycles are longer but contract values are higher."
- **Segment economics**: For each segment, the startup articulates expected CAC, pricing, LTV, and whether unit economics work in each segment. Example: "Segment A supports $10K CAC with $15K annual contract value; Segment B supports $50K CAC with $100K ACV; blended CAC:LTV is favorable at 1:5."
- **Adoption readiness**: The submission assesses segment-level adoption readiness. Which segments are actively evaluating solutions? Which are further back in the buying journey?
- **Competitive intensity by segment**: The submission identifies where competitive intensity is lower. "Competitors focus on enterprise; SMB is underserved."

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Undifferentiated market**: "We're going after all mid-market companies" without specificity on customer profile, decision-making, or value drivers.
- **Generic personas**: Customer descriptions are shallow or generic ("The operations manager who wants efficiency").
- **No prioritization**: The startup plans to target all segments simultaneously rather than sequencing entry by segment difficulty/opportunity.
- **Segment economics not modeled**: No analysis of whether CAC/LTV/pricing works in each segment.
- **Conflation of segments**: Different customer types with different decision-making profiles and pain points are lumped together.
- **No adoption readiness assessment**: The submission assumes all segments are equally ready to adopt without evidence.

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- What are the 3–5 primary customer segments the startup will target? How are they defined (company size, vertical, use case, decision-making profile)?
- For each segment, what is the customer persona (title, budget, decision-making authority, pain points, alternatives in use)?
- Which segment will the startup target first, and why? What is the sequencing for expansion to other segments?
- For each segment, are unit economics (CAC/LTV/pricing) favorable? Does willingness to pay justify customer acquisition cost?
- What evidence exists that each segment recognizes the problem and is ready to adopt a solution?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Detailed customer personas** for each segment, including title, budget, authority, decision-making timeline, primary pain points
- **Segment prioritization matrix** showing TAM, SAM, competition, adoption readiness, and unit economics for each segment
- **Segment-specific economics** (pricing expectations, estimated CAC, LTV, payback period) for each primary segment
- **Go-to-market sequencing** showing which segment to target first and why (quick wins, reference value, economics, competitive advantage)
- **Adoption readiness assessment** for each segment with evidence of current solution use, budget allocation, and evaluation timeline
- **Competitive mapping** by segment showing where competitive intensity is lower

## Module 8: Demand Validation

### Definition
Evidence that real, monetizable demand exists for the solution—proof that customers recognize the problem, are willing to pay to solve it, and are ready to adopt the solution. Demand validation may include customer research (surveys, interviews), pilots and proofs of concept, letters of intent or LOIs, customer commitments, and behavioral data demonstrating interest or willingness to engage.

### What Good Looks Like

- **Quantified customer research**: The startup has conducted research with 30+ target customers (not advisors, not press, not friends) asking about problem severity, alternative solutions, pricing, and purchase intent. Results are quantified: "76% of surveyed customers rank this problem as top-3 priority; 61% have budget allocated to solve it in the next 12 months; 52% expressed interest in evaluating our solution."
- **Pilot or PoC commitment**: The startup has secured commitments from customers to pilot or test the solution, ideally with clear success criteria and timeline. Example: "Three customers have committed to 4-week pilots beginning Q2 2025."
- **Letters of intent (LOIs)**: Customers have signed non-binding LOIs indicating intent to purchase upon product availability or at specified pricing. Example: "Five customers have signed LOIs for a combined $500K annual contract value."
- **Pricing validation**: Evidence that customers will pay the intended price point. This may come from pricing research, pilot customers, or comparative analysis. Example: "When presented with pricing comparable to [competitor], 68% of surveyed customers indicated they would pay this amount; 34% indicated premium pricing up to 1.5x is acceptable."
- **Behavioral signals**: Evidence of customer interest through actions, not just stated intent. Website traffic from target customers, whitepaper downloads, webinar attendance, feature request volumes, or survey response rates all indicate engagement.
- **Urgency indicators**: Evidence that customer demand is urgent (not just interesting). Customers are actively evaluating competitors, have budget allocated now, or face regulatory/operational deadlines driving decision timelines.
- **Independent validation**: Demand validation comes from real customers, not internal team members or advisors. Clear sample sizes and methodology are documented.

### What Weak/Absent Looks Like

- **Anecdotal evidence only**: "I talked to three customers and they all said this is a problem" without systematic research, quantification, or sample size disclosure.
- **Advisor or consultant feedback**: Demand validation comes from advisors or industry experts, not from actual target customers.
- **Leading questions or confirmation bias**: Research questions are phrased in ways that pressure affirmative responses ("Don't you think automation would help your business?").
- **Stated intent without commitment**: Customers said "this sounds interesting" or "I'd evaluate it" but have not committed to pilots, LOIs, or purchases.
- **No pricing validation**: The startup has not tested whether customers will pay the intended price point.
- **Weak sample size**: Research is based on 5 or fewer customer interviews with no quantification of results across the sample.
- **Behavioral signals not demonstrated**: No evidence of actual customer interest or engagement (website traffic, downloads, survey responses).

### Key Questions to Answer in Assessment

- How many target customers has the startup surveyed or interviewed about this problem? What percentage of contacted customers responded? (Response rate indicates survey validity.)
- What were the specific research questions asked, and what were the quantified results? (Look for clear quantification of problem severity, purchase intent, and willingness to pay.)
- Has the startup secured any customer commitments to pilot, test, or purchase the solution? If so, how many, and what are the economic commitments?
- What evidence shows customers will pay the intended price point? Has pricing been tested with customers?
- What behavioral signals indicate real customer interest (website traffic, whitepaper downloads, webinar attendance, NPS, survey response rates)?
- Are there competitive products customers are evaluating? Does this signal genuine buying intent vs. general curiosity?

### What to Look for in the Submission

- **Customer research summary** including: number of interviews/surveys conducted, response rate, specific questions asked, and quantified results on problem severity, purchase intent, and pricing acceptance
- **Pilot or PoC commitments** with customer names (or anonymized description), scope, timeline, and success criteria
- **Letters of intent** from customers indicating purchase intent at specified pricing and timeline
- **Pricing research** showing customer willingness to pay, competitive pricing analysis, and value-based pricing rationale
- **Behavioral signal metrics** (website traffic, resource downloads, survey engagement, webinar attendance) indicating customer interest
- **Comparative evidence** (customers evaluating competitors, budget allocation timelines, regulatory deadlines) demonstrating urgency

## Cross-Domain Dependencies

The Market and Opportunity domain feeds into and is informed by:

- **Domain 2 (Solution and Product)**: Market insights drive product definition. Competitive landscape and customer segmentation validate the product roadmap and feature prioritization. Demand validation proves product-market fit.
- **Domain 3 (Business Model)**: Market sizing and customer segmentation drive pricing strategy, unit economics, and revenue targets. Competitive positioning and customer willingness to pay inform pricing power and margin assumptions.
- **Domain 4 (Go-to-Market)**: Customer segmentation and demand validation drive go-to-market sequencing and channel strategy. Competitive positioning informs messaging and differentiation.
- **Domain 5 (Traction)**: Demand validation and early customer wins are the first indicators of traction. Revenue history should align with market sizing and TAM assumptions.

Conversely, these domains inform market assessment:

- **Traction**: Early customer wins and pilot results validate market opportunity and customer willingness to pay.
- **Product**: Product capabilities must align with competitive positioning claims and customer needs identified in market research.
- **Business Model**: Unit economics must be achievable within the market conditions and customer segments identified.

## Assessment Notes for the Scoring Agent

When assessing the Market and Opportunity domain:

1. **Weight problem definition heavily**: If the team has not validated that a real problem exists and matters to customers, all other domains are speculative. Demand validation is the foundation.

2. **Triangulate market sizing**: Do not accept market size at face value. Verify that top-down and bottom-up approaches converge. Be skeptical of unsourced numbers or inflated capture assumptions.

3. **Assess timing honesty**: Does the timing thesis explain why now is favorable vs. alternatives? Be skeptical of "timing is always right for a good solution" arguments; timing actually matters.

4. **Check competitive awareness**: Does the team understand the competitive landscape, or is it in denial about alternatives? Companies that claim they have no competitors are often wrong.

5. **Validate differentiation durability**: Is the competitive differentiation defensible, or is it a feature easily replicated by competitors? Pricing alone is not durable differentiation.

6. **Link demand to monetization**: Does customer demand translate to willingness to pay, or are customers interested but unwilling to commit budget? LOIs and pilot commitments are much stronger signals than stated interest.

7. **Look for segment economics**: Even in a large market, if the startup's target segments do not support attractive unit economics, growth will be constrained. Does the submission model segment-level CAC/LTV?

8. **Assess customer research quality**: Research from 3 advisors is not equivalent to research from 30 target customers. Weight sample size, response rates, and independence heavily.

---

## Summary: Good Market and Opportunity Assessment

A strong Market and Opportunity assessment demonstrates:

- **Validated problem** with quantified impact and customer research proving the problem is real and urgent
- **Realistic market sizing** with convergent top-down and bottom-up methodologies
- **Favorable market dynamics** driven by secular trends and timing inflection points
- **Competitively favorable positioning** with clear differentiation that is durable and valued by customers
- **Segmented customer understanding** with prioritized go-to-market entry and realistic unit economics
- **Demand validation** through customer research, pilots, LOIs, or behavioral signals proving customers will buy

The Market and Opportunity domain is foundational. Without it, all other excellence is built on sand.
